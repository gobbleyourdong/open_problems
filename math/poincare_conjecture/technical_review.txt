Technical Review of the Watermarked Navier-
Stokes Draft

Shareable PDF version of the prior analysis response
Source document reviewed: final_paper_watermarked.pdf | Prepared: April 1, 2026
All theorem numbers, equations, and quotations below refer to the uploaded manuscript under review. Public source notes appear at
the end.
Bottom line: better organized, but not better proved. Not a legit proof of 3D Navier-Stokes global regularity.
More problematic than the previous version overall.
Overall verdict
This version is still not a legit proof of 3D Navier-Stokes global regularity, and in one important sense it is less
trustworthy than the previous draft: it is now much more confident, but the hard parts are not actually closed. The
Clay Mathematics Institute still lists the Navier-Stokes problem as unsolved [1].
What improved
Compared with the earlier version you showed me, this one is cleaner and more concrete in a few real ways. It
now tries to reduce everything to one explicit inequality, Q = 9|omega|^2 - 8|S|_F^2 > 0, rather than leaning so
heavily on the earlier "average alignment" storyline. The theorem chain is easier to follow, the finite-dimensional
N <= 4 argument is more explicit, and the paper is more self-contained as a theorem/lemma/proof document.
Those are genuine improvements in presentation, and maybe in the finite-mode core of the idea.
There is also some math here that does not look like random nonsense. The cross-term identity in Section 3 is the
kind of algebraic identity one could plausibly check independently; the trace-free estimate S_e^2 <= (2/3)|S|_F^2
is standard-looking; and the N = 4 "you cannot fit four pairwise orthogonal directions in R^3" obstruction is at

least a recognizable finite-dimensional argument. If you stripped away the Clay claim, there may be a small finite-
mode paper hiding in Sections 3-5.

What still needs major improvement - and what is fatal
1) The certified finite-mode statement still does not match the PDE statement it is supposed to prove
This is the biggest problem. Equation (2) allows arbitrary amplitudes a_j and arbitrary phases phi_j, but the later
sign-pattern and vertex machinery treats the low modes as if they were evaluated only through signs s_j in {+1,
-1} at cube vertices. Theorem 4.2 certifies Q > 0 for "sign patterns maximizing |omega|^2" at an "argmax vertex,"
while Section 8 needs control at the actual space-time vorticity maximum of the full PDE solution. At that real
maximum, the low modes contribute cos(k_j . x + phi_j) in [-1, 1], not just +/-1; the phases matter; the amplitudes
matter; and the maximum need not sit at a cube vertex. A tail estimate does not repair any of those mismatches. So
the object being certified is not the object needed in the PDE proof.
There is an even more concrete version of the same issue: the notation section includes phases phi_j, but Theorem
3.1 and the later vertex formulas simply drop them. As written, the cross-term identity and the vertex reduction are
not even consistent with the Fourier ansatz that the paper itself states. That is not just cosmetic; it means the
formal setup is internally misaligned from the start.

And there is yet another mismatch: Theorem 4.2 only covers configurations with N <= 7, but a generic low-
frequency truncation omega_h on a shell like |k|^2 <= 9 can have many more than seven active modes. Section 8

nevertheless applies Theorem 4.2 directly to omega_h. That jump is unjustified. The tail argument removes high
frequencies; it does not reduce the number of retained low modes to seven.
2) The computational section contains internal contradictions that are mathematically impossible as
written
This is the strongest red flag in the whole draft. The paper defines
Q = 9|omega|^2 - 8|S|_F^2.
Because |S|_F^2 >= 0, one must have
Q / |omega|^2 <= 9.
But the table in Theorem 4.2 lists minima 9.40, 10.99, and 11.67 for N = 5, 6, 7. Those values are impossible
under the paper's own definition of Q. That means either the formula, the normalization, or the table is wrong.
The draft then compounds that problem by saying in Remark 5.3 that the universal minimum is Q / |omega|^2 =
9/4, while Theorem 4.2 says the overall minimum floor is 5.43. Those two statements cannot both be right as
written. On top of that, the abstract says the computation covers shells |k|^2 <= 9, but the table says N = 3 goes up
to 18, N = 4 up to 9, and N = 5, 6, 7 only up to 3. So the coverage claim is also inconsistent with the table. These
are not subtle PDE objections; they are straightforward internal contradictions.
3) The SOS/verification story is not rigorous as stated
The SOS section is also shaky on its own terms. With the displayed parametrization v_j = x_j e_{1j} + y_j e_{2j},
the quantity Q_s looks quadratic in the variables (x_j, y_j), but the paper calls it degree 4. The Positivstellensatz
line is written as an "if and only if" statement with scalar multipliers lambda_j >= 0 and mu_t >= 0, which is not
how one usually states a general Putinar certificate on a semialgebraic set. Maybe the implementation uses a
restricted ansatz, but if so the theorem statement is over-claiming.
The verification description also mixes standards. It says the residual matrix is checked "with exact arithmetic,"
but then says one computes eigenvalues and verifies they are >= -10^-6. That is not exact arithmetic in the
ordinary sense; that is a numerical tolerance. Again, maybe the authors have a valid certificate pipeline in code,
but the paper as written does not describe it in a way that earns trust.
4) The spectral tail bound fails a basic scaling sanity check
Theorem 6.1 is almost certainly wrong as stated. It claims
|C_tail| / |omega|^2 <= C_s ||omega||_{H^s} K_max^-(s - 3/2).
But the left-hand side is scale-invariant under omega -> lambda omega: both numerator and denominator scale
like lambda^2. The right-hand side scales like lambda. So the displayed inequality cannot be a correct universal
bound in that form. That is a clean homogeneity failure.
The same section then says that for K_max^2 = 9 and s = 3, the correction is O(0.04), but the theorem itself
visibly depends on ||omega||_{H^s}. So that numerical claim suppresses the very dependence that the bound
displays. The continuity bootstrap is also not convincing: "Type I growth keeps ||omega||_{H^s} below 2M_0" is
not a justified implication. Even a Type I pointwise bound does not automatically give a uniform high-Sobolev
bound.

5) The new barrier argument is the most dangerous new move
This is where the new version gets worse than the old one. Theorem 7.1 now simply asserts H_{omega omega} =
e-hat . (nabla^2 p) . e-hat >= 0 at a vorticity maximum. The draft gives no proof of that sign, and there is no
obvious reason it should follow from "vorticity maximum" alone. In the previous draft, the pressure-Hessian step
was at least acknowledged as heuristic or incomplete. Here it is silently turned into a fact. That is a much more
serious problem.
The citation to Seregin is also wrong for the claimed use. The cited Seregin paper's abstract says a necessary
condition for blow-up is that the L^3 norm blows up as t approaches T; it does not say that Type I singularities are
impossible on T^3 [2]. And later literature still treats Type I singularities as a serious scenario, not something
generally ruled out in full 3D Navier-Stokes.
6) Reproducibility is weaker than advertised
The paper says the certification code and verification scripts are publicly available at the GitHub URL in reference
[2]. When I checked that exact URL, it returned 404 Not Found [3]. That does not prove the code does not exist
somewhere else, but it does mean the specific public reproducibility claim in the paper is not currently verifiable
from the cited link.
Smaller slip-ups / whoopsies that hurt confidence
There are several "small" issues that matter because they point in the same direction: the phase variables are
introduced and then dropped; the shell ranges in the abstract and the table disagree; Q_s is called quartic when it
appears quadratic under the displayed parametrization; zeta-hat_j = K u-hat_j / a_j uses a K that is never defined;
and the paper talks about "exact arithmetic" while also relying on a 10^-6 eigenvalue tolerance. Any one of those
could be a typo. Taken together, they suggest the draft is not under tight mathematical control.
What is improved relative to the previous version
The conceptual improvement is real: this version is trying to replace the earlier heuristic "average alignment plus
pressure-Hessian modeling" story with a more explicit finite-dimensional inequality and a computer certificate.

That is a better direction. It is also more concise and more theorem-driven. If the paper were rewritten as "a finite-
mode SOS study suggesting a new inequality," I would take it more seriously than the previous exploratory draft.

What is now worse than before
But the paper is worse in a more important way: the earlier draft at least admitted the hard PDE assembly was
incomplete. This one claims a full proof in six pages, yet the closure step depends on a tail bound that fails a
scaling test, a discrete certificate that does not apply to the actual PDE truncation, and a pressure-Hessian sign
assertion that is unsupported. So the rhetoric got stronger while the proof got weaker. The internal contradictions
in the computation table and the dead code link also make this version more problematic than the previous one.
Crank concerns
I would not call this pure crank in the lowest sense. It is not random word salad. There is real mathematical
structure here, some plausible algebra, and a recognizable finite-dimensional idea.
But I would say this draft is now in serious crank-concern territory. The warning signs are classic: a millennium
claim in a very short paper; huge computational assertions with internally impossible headline numbers; a citation
used to claim more than it says; an unproved leap from a small finite-mode or discrete model to the full PDE; and

a tendency to present central missing steps as if they were standard or already settled. Those are exactly the
patterns that make people distrust "instant solution" papers on famous open problems.
Final judgment
My updated judgment is: better organized, but not better proved. Not legit as a proof. More problematic than the
previous version overall.
The most salvageable path would be to throw away the global-regularity claim, audit the SOS numerics from
scratch, fix phases and amplitudes, and rewrite the paper as a modest finite-mode inequality or computation note.
In its current form, I would reject it very firmly as a claimed solution to Navier-Stokes.
