## The move this attempt makes

**Claim.** The question "what is reality really made of" is malformed. Reality IS its converged compression — the regularity stack that any competent compressor converges on given enough observations. The classical ontologies are not disputing what reality is made of; they are arguing over which label best captures that converged stack. Once the compression backbone is accepted, the dispute becomes terminological.

This is not a trivial dissolution of a serious question. It is specifically Wittgensteinian: the question appears to demand an answer of the form "reality is made of X" where X is some substance more basic than the structure we can observe. But the compression view says: there is no X beneath the structure. The structure is all there is, because the structure is what "is" means.

## Walking the seven ontologies

PROBLEM.md lists seven competing ontologies. Each, under the compression view, turns out to be pointing at the same converged regularity stack with different emphasis.

### 1. Physicalism

**Claim.** Reality is physical stuff.

**Compression reading.** "Physical stuff" is whatever has thermodynamic, spatiotemporal, and causal structure — whatever obeys the laws of physics. Under the compression view, "the laws of physics" are the minimum K-specification that predicts observations. So "physical stuff" is "whatever is described by the converged compressed regularities." Physicalism is correct by definition, once its substrate is clarified as "whatever the converged compression picks out."

The interesting version of physicalism is the claim that the converged compression is *exhausted* by physics, with no leftover mental or mathematical or abstract residue. This is a scientific-program commitment, not a metaphysical one. Whether it is right depends on empirical developments about what the converged compression will ultimately include.

### 2. Mathematical Universe (Tegmark)

**Claim.** Reality is a mathematical structure. Every consistent structure exists.

**Compression reading.** This is physicalism plus the further claim that the converged compression's output is literally a mathematical structure (a set of objects with relations and axioms). Under the compression view, this is a claim about the SHAPE of the converged compression, not about its existence. Compressed regularities ARE mathematical structures; calling them that is a naming choice. Tegmark's strong form (every consistent mathematical structure exists) is not forced by the compression view — it is an additional speculative commitment.

The interesting part of Tegmark's hypothesis is the modal component: existence extends to every consistent structure. The compression view is compatible with this if you interpret "every possible compressor would converge on some structure; reality is the structure it converges on for our observations; other structures are realities for other observers." But the stronger metaphysical reading (all structures exist as realities equally) is optional.

### 3. Information (Wheeler's "it from bit")

**Claim.** Reality is information. Bits precede atoms.

**Compression reading.** Under the S/K bifurcation from `what_is_information`, this ontology is either:
- **Strong S-informationalism:** reality is distinguishable states; every physical property is a bit-count. This is roughly the holographic-principle picture.
- **Strong K-informationalism:** reality is compressed structure; the "bits" are descriptions, and the fundamental thing is the K-function that generates them.

The compression view is a K-monism: reality is structure, and structure is K-content. Wheeler's "it from bit" is probably the closest classical ontology to this, modulo the ambiguity about whether "bit" means S or K.

**Corollary.** Under the compression view, "information" is not a competitor to "physical stuff" — they are the same thing under different names. The apparent debate between physicalists and informationalists is a debate about which vocabulary to use for the converged compression.

### 4. Process (Whitehead)

**Claim.** Reality is events, not substances. Verbs not nouns.

**Compression reading.** Process philosophy emphasizes dynamics over static structure. Under the compression view, both matter: the K-content specifies both states (what can be) and transitions (how states evolve). Process philosophy is correct to emphasize that a complete compression includes dynamics — the equations ARE the process. But it is wrong to deny the usefulness of state-talk: compressed regularities have both a "state" face and a "transition" face and both are needed.

### 5. Idealism

**Claim.** Reality is mental. Consciousness is primary.

**Compression reading.** Under the compression backbone, minds are compressors of regularities. Idealism claims that the compressors come first — that the experience-structure is the fundamental thing and "physical reality" is a construction from it. This is a coherent position but it confuses the compressor with the compressed.

**The decisive compression-view response:** if consciousness is fundamental and everything is a construction of it, we still need to ask WHY the constructions have the specific K-structure they have — why the laws of physics, why the specific periodic table, why the specific cosmological constants. The constructions are not arbitrary. They are constrained by something. That something is what "external reality" means. Idealism cannot drop the external without losing the non-arbitrariness of experience, and the non-arbitrariness is what we were calling reality in the first place.

### 6. Neutral monism

**Claim.** Reality is neither mental nor physical. Both are aspects of something else.

**Compression reading.** Neutral monism is closest in spirit to the compression view. It says the mental/physical distinction is a distinction between two faces of one substrate. Under the compression view, that substrate is structured regularity — K-content — and the mental and the physical are two ways of talking about K-content (the compressor's view and the compressed's view, or equivalently, the first-person and third-person perspectives on structured regularity). Russellian monism is a specific variant of this and maps particularly cleanly.

### 7. Relational (Rovelli)

**Claim.** Reality has no absolute state. Everything is relations.

**Compression reading.** Relational physics emphasizes that physical quantities are defined relative to reference systems, not as absolute properties. Under the compression view, this is the observation that K-specifications are relative to the compressor's vocabulary and frame of reference. Different observers with different vocabularies will compress the same underlying regularity stack into different-looking specifications, while tracking the same regularities. Relational physics and the compression view are highly compatible.

## The convergence claim

Under the compression view, all seven ontologies are pointing at the same thing: **the converged compression of observations**. They differ in emphasis and vocabulary, not in substance. Physicalism emphasizes the substrate's thermodynamic/causal face. Mathematical universe emphasizes its formal structure. Informationalism emphasizes its description-theoretic face. Process emphasizes its dynamics. Idealism emphasizes the compressor side of the compressor/compressed pair. Neutral monism emphasizes their unity. Relational physics emphasizes frame-dependence.

**None of these seven positions is wrong.** Each highlights a feature of the same underlying phenomenon: **a regularity stack that competent compressors must eventually converge on, given sufficient observations and time**.

The "what is reality REALLY made of" question presupposes that there's something more than the converged regularity stack — some substance beneath the structure that would tell us "really" which of the seven ontologies is right. The compression view denies this presupposition. There is nothing beneath the structure. The structure IS reality. The seven vocabularies are seven ways of talking about it, and the best one for a given purpose depends on what the purpose is.

This is a deflationary answer. It does not solve the "hard problem of reality" so much as dissolve it.

## The anti-problem: "Why is there something rather than nothing?"

From PROBLEM.md:

> *What would it mean for reality to NOT exist? If nothing exists, what is doing the "nothing"?*

This is Leibniz's question. The compression view has a specific response:

**Compression presupposes a substrate.** You cannot compress nothing. If there is genuine nothingness — no regularities, no structure, no distinctions — then there is no compressor, no compressed, and no "reality" in any operational sense. The question "why is there something rather than nothing" is asking: why is there a substrate for compression to operate on?

**Two answers available:**

**(a) Genuine nothingness is impossible.** The concept "nothing" is unstable under examination: "nothing" is a distinction between zero and something else, and distinctions are structure, and structure is not nothing. Attempting to specify genuine nothingness produces a contradiction: "nothing" as a stable state requires a specification, and specifications are somethings. This is a version of Parmenides's argument, stated in compression terms.

**(b) Nothingness is possible; we just happen to be in a something.** This is Leibniz's anthropic-ish response. Nothingness could have been the case, but was not, and we cannot say why. The question has no explanation beyond the fact that something is the case. This is compatible with the compression view but adds nothing to it.

The compression view mildly favors (a) because it makes "nothing" conceptually incoherent rather than merely unexplained. The Parmenidean argument, reformatted: nothing is not a possible state because states are structured and structure is the opposite of nothing. The concept "nothing" is a convenient fiction the mind uses, not a genuine metaphysical possibility.

**Honest caveat.** (a) is an old argument with serious objectors. The compression view does not uniquely resolve it. But the compression view does explain WHY the argument is attractive: compressors require substrates, and a substrate-less state cannot be compressed and therefore cannot be a "reality" for any compressor. The concept of total nothingness is an edge case that breaks the machinery compression depends on.

## Is reality a simulation?

A popular modern form of the substrate question is the simulation hypothesis (Bostrom): are we in a simulation?

Under the compression view, this is asking: is the converged compression of our observations the SAME compression that would describe a "ground truth" underlying reality, or is it a LAYER on top of some more fundamental compression being run by an outer system?

**Three options:**

- **Ground reality.** Our compression directly tracks the fundamental substrate. No further layers.
- **Nested compression.** Our compression tracks a layer that is itself the output of a more fundamental compression being run by an outer system (a computation in the sense of `what_is_computation`). The outer compression may or may not have observers.
- **Infinite regress.** Every compression is a layer on a more fundamental compression. There is no bottom.

**The compression view is neutral on which of the three is correct.** It does say that the question only bites if there are empirical differences between the three cases. If the observations are the same, the three are observationally equivalent and no experiment distinguishes them. The simulation hypothesis therefore reduces to whether there could be empirical signatures of an outer computation — a question the simulation literature has been pursuing.

**Philosophical contribution.** Under the compression view, the simulation hypothesis is not a claim about the furniture of reality; it is a claim about how many layers of compression are stacked before you reach a layer without an outer observer. The difference between "real reality" and "simulated reality" dissolves if no layer can see its outer layer.

## What this attempt closes

- The seven ontologies are reorganized under the compression view and shown to be compatible rather than competing.
- The "really made of" form of the substrate question is dissolved.
- The simulation hypothesis is reframed as a question about layers of compression rather than about the fundamental furniture.
- The Leibniz question receives a compression-flavored response that mildly favors Parmenidean impossibility of genuine nothingness.

## What remains

- The physical bounds on K-content (see what_is_information/gap.md R1).
- Whether the converged compression converges at all, or whether physical reality has a structure that exceeds any finite compression. This is related to physical Church-Turing and to the Gödel residue for mathematics.
- Whether the compression view leaves any room for genuine novelty, free will, or emergence — or whether these are all reduced to "compression subroutines we haven't built yet."
