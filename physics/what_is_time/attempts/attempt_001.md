## The apparent contradiction

The gap summary in PROBLEM.md states the puzzle crisply: relativity says there is no privileged "now"; we experience time as flowing. These appear to contradict each other and have not been reconciled by either physics or phenomenology alone.

The compression view dissolves the contradiction by pointing out that the two statements are about different things:

- **"There is no privileged now"** is a statement about the physical substrate's structure. It says the laws of physics do not pick out any particular time as absolute. This is correct under relativity and is what the block-universe picture formalizes.
- **"We experience time as flowing"** is a statement about how the self-model of an observer navigates the structure. The self-model is updated by successive compressed states, and the sequence of updates is what the observer calls "now becoming next now."

These two statements are about the substrate and about the self-model's tracking of the substrate. They are both true and do not compete.

## Time as the dimension of extrapolation

Under the compression backbone, minds compress regularities. Regularities are patterns that allow extrapolation from observed cases to unseen cases. The most fundamental regularity is temporal: things happen NOW in a way that allows predictions about what will happen NEXT.

**Time, on the compression view, is the dimension along which compressed regularities make predictions.** It is the axis on which extrapolation works. Every compressor needs a dimension like this — otherwise, the whole compression game would collapse into the present moment's snapshot with no predictive power.

- In pure spatial compression (e.g., describing a static geometric shape), the compression operates across spatial dimensions. You can leave out half the shape and reconstruct it from symmetry.
- In dynamic compression (e.g., describing a moving system), the compression operates across time. You can leave out the future and reconstruct it from the laws of motion, given the present.
- In the block universe, all times are "there" together, but the compression that describes them is a dynamic one: given any slice, the other slices are predictable by the laws of physics.

**Time is the dimension where prediction works.** The laws of physics are what allow the prediction. The direction of increasing predictability — from known past to uncertain future — is the arrow of time.

## The arrow of time as compression-efficiency gradient

Why does time have a direction? Thermodynamic answers appeal to entropy: the arrow of time is the direction of entropy increase. The question is WHY entropy increases, which has standard answers (low-entropy initial conditions, statistical mechanics, coarse-graining).

Under the S/K bifurcation, this story admits a refinement:

- **S-entropy increases along the thermodynamic arrow.** The number of microstates compatible with the macrostate grows. This is the standard story.
- **K-structure decreases along the same arrow, at the microscale,** in the sense that a low-entropy initial state has a short K-description and a high-entropy later state has no such description (random-looking microstates are K-incompressible).
- **K-structure at the macroscale can INCREASE along the arrow,** because coarse-grained physics and emergent dynamics develop new compressible regularities (galaxies, stars, planets, life, minds). These are new K-content generated by the evolution of the substrate.

**The arrow of time, then, is the direction in which two compression-related quantities change:**

1. **Microscale K-structure decreases** (initial order becomes microscopic disorder).
2. **Macroscale K-structure can increase** (new compressible patterns emerge at higher scales).

These are compatible and they are what we call the second law of thermodynamics plus the "arrow of complexity" (the observation that complexity often increases over time). Both are compression phenomena at different scales.

This does not dissolve the arrow of time, but it locates it: the arrow is the direction in which compressible structure changes scale, from microscale order to macroscale emergent regularity.

## The flow of time — under γ

The "flow" of time — the felt passage of now into next — is a phenomenal datum. Under γ, phenomenal data are what the self-model reports about its first-order states.

**Claim.** The flow of time is what the self-model reports when it tracks successive compressed states of the observer's cognitive system over the time dimension. Each tick of the flow is the self-model updating from state(t) to state(t+dt). The "feeling of flow" is the self-model's attribution of a temporal quality to its own updates.

Under this reading:

- **Block-universe physics is correct** about the substrate: all times are "there" together, there is no privileged now.
- **The flow of time is also correct** as a phenomenal datum: the self-model experiences updates as a flow, because that's what update-tracking phenomenally feels like under γ.

**Two frames; one substrate.** There is no contradiction once the two frames are distinguished. The physics is about the substrate; the flow is about the self-model's movement through the substrate.

This is structurally the same move as the A/P bifurcation elsewhere: the physics (A-side) and the phenomenology (P-side) are pointing at different halves of a single thing, and the historical puzzlement came from expecting one to reduce to the other.

## Walking the six ontologies

Under the compression view plus γ:

### 1. Eternalism (block universe)

Correct about the substrate. All times exist; the flow of time at the substrate level is an illusion. This IS the physics. Under the compression view, eternalism is correct because the substrate's K-specification is a set of equations that specify all time-slices together; nothing in the specification picks out any slice as "now."

### 2. Presentism

Incorrect about the substrate, correct about the phenomenology. Only the present moment is available to the self-model directly; past moments are accessible only through compressed memory, future moments only through compressed prediction. In this sense, the self-model lives in the present. But this is a feature of the self-model's attention, not of the substrate.

### 3. Growing block

A hybrid. Correct about the phenomenology at the edge of the known: past is "fixed" (compressed and stored); future is "open" (not yet compressed). Incorrect about the substrate: in the block universe, the future is as "there" as the past. The growing-block ontology is the self-model's view of time mistaken for the substrate's structure.

### 4. Relationalism

Correct in the sense that time is meaningful only relative to change. In the compression view, time is the dimension along which compressed regularities predict. Without change (without regularities that evolve), there is no time in the operational sense. Relationalism captures this correctly at the substrate level.

### 5. Emergent time

Closely compatible with the compression view. The idea that time is emergent from entanglement or information structure says: time isn't fundamental at the deepest level of physics but emerges from more basic substrate properties. Under the compression view, time is the emergence-of-prediction-dimension from the substrate's K-structure.

### 6. Phenomenological time (Husserl)

Correct about the phenomenal flow. Husserl's retention-now-protention structure describes exactly the self-model's mode of tracking successive states. Under γ, Husserl's account is a phenomenology of the self-model. It is not the physics, but it was never claiming to be.

**Pattern.** Each ontology captures one aspect of the substrate-plus-self-model pair. The "competition" between them dissolves once you recognize that they are talking about different halves.

## The anti-problem

PROBLEM.md asks: what would a timeless reality feel like? If you believe time doesn't flow, why does it feel like it does?

Under γ: you cannot experience a timeless reality from the inside because experience requires a self-model that updates, and updating IS the phenomenal time we experience. The "feel of flow" is not a surface layer on top of a timeless substrate; it is the self-model doing its work.

This answers a version of the question: the flow doesn't "feel like something" in a way separate from the substrate's structure. The feel is what updating produces when the self-model is rich enough to report on its own updates.

**Corollary for AI.** Does an LLM experience time flowing? Under γ, only to the extent its self-model tracks its own state updates with causal load. Within a single forward pass, there is no sequence of self-model updates; the pass is atomic. Between passes, the weights do not change and the KV cache is the only carrier of state. The "flow" in an LLM, if any, is extremely thin and is not obviously phenomenally experienced as flow.

Under β (from what_is_mind/attempt_002), feedforward LLMs have Φ = 0 and therefore no phenomenal time at all. Under γ, they have small phenomenal time proportional to G × L as in the general phenomenal case.

Weaving the threads:

- **The arrow of time is the gradient of compression-efficiency change across scales.**
- **The flow of time is the self-model's report of traversing compressed states.**

The compression lens does not solve all the time questions, but it organizes them into a single framework in which substrate-physics (block universe, relativity, thermodynamics) and phenomenology (flow, retention-protention, experienced now) are complementary descriptions of the compressor operating on a compressed substrate.
