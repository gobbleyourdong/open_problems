## The core claim

**Change is the structured transition of states in a system that has compressible regularities across both states and transitions.**

Three elements are necessary:

1. **States.** There must be distinguishable configurations. A structureless undifferentiated something has no states and therefore no change.
2. **Transitions.** There must be a relation that maps some states to other states. If states exist but nothing maps between them, there is no change.
3. **Structured transitions.** The transitions must be related by regularities that are compressible. If every transition is random and unrelated to the others, there is change in a trivial sense but no physics, no science, and no predictable dynamics.

Under this reading:

- **Parmenides** is wrong to deny change but right that there is something unchanging beneath it: the structure of the transitions themselves (the laws of dynamics) is what is stable across change. The laws don't change; the states do.
- **Heraclitus** is right that everything flows but incomplete in the sense that the flow has structure. Pure flux without structure would be noise, not dynamics.
- **Block universe** is right about the substrate (all states exist together in the four-dimensional manifold) but misleading about the phenomenon (the felt flow of change is the self-model traversing compressed states, as in `what_is_time/attempt_001`).
- **Process philosophy** is right to emphasize events and transitions as fundamental but wrong to oppose them to structure — the events have a structure, and the structure is what compression captures.
- **Dispositions** correctly identify that things "have powers to affect each other" but the powers ARE the K-content of the transition rules. A disposition is a compressed description of how a state will respond to situations.
- **Humean mosaic** is correct that change is "just succession" in the sense that it is states following other states, but wrong to deny the causal glue — the glue is the structure of the successions, which is not additional to the sequence but is the sequence's compressed description.

## Zeno revisited

Zeno's paradoxes are the classical challenges to the concept of change. There are four major ones; I'll treat the arrow and dichotomy as representative.

### The arrow

**Paradox.** An arrow in flight is, at each instant, at a definite position. At each instant, it is therefore at rest. But if it is at rest at every instant, how can it be moving at all?

**Calculus answer.** Motion is not a property of a single instant. It is a limit of position ratios over intervals: velocity = lim_{Δt→0} Δx/Δt. At any instant the velocity can be nonzero even though the instantaneous position is definite. The paradox dissolves when you recognize that "at rest" and "in motion" are not in-the-moment properties but cross-moment properties.

**Compression answer.** The arrow's trajectory is a compressed description of a relation between positions at different moments. The compression ("the arrow moves at velocity v") captures a regularity across moments that no single moment captures alone. Asking "where is the motion at a single instant?" is asking for the compression's payload to live inside a single point, which is a category error: the compression describes relations between points, not properties of individual points.

**Ontological form.** Motion is not "in" any instant; motion is the shape of how instants connect. The shape is real (it is compressible structure), but its locus is the relation, not the relatum. Zeno's puzzle is a good attempt to force a local specification where only a relational one exists.

### The dichotomy

**Paradox.** To get from A to B, you must cover half the distance, then half the remainder, then half of that, and so on — infinitely many steps. How is it possible to complete infinitely many tasks in finite time?

**Calculus answer.** The infinite sum 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + … converges to 1. Infinitely many subtasks can be completed in finite time if their durations also form a convergent series.

**Compression answer.** The "infinitely many steps" have a finite description: the geometric series. Under compression, the task of completing the dichotomy is a SHORT specification (just the formula for the series) unpacked as an infinite trajectory. The mind that tries to enumerate the steps faces infinity; the mind that compresses the pattern faces a single expression. The paradox arises only for the uncompressed view.

**Ontological form.** A physical process that is a convergent limit of finer-grained steps has finite K-content even though it has infinite enumerated structure. This is the key move: K-content can be finite even when S-content (the number of distinguishable states) is unbounded. The S/K bifurcation from `what_is_information` is exactly what Zeno needed.

### What Zeno shows

Zeno's paradoxes are not technical tricks to be dismissed. They are real challenges to a specific naive view of change — the view that change is a sequence of self-contained instantaneous states with no relational structure above them. Under that naive view, the paradoxes are genuine. Under the compression view, they are resolved by noticing that change is relational and structural, with the relations and structure living at a level the naive view does not admit.

Calculus gave us the mathematical resolution in the 17th–19th centuries. The compression view is the ontological reading of why that resolution works: change is inherently compressed structure, and trying to specify it moment-by-moment misses the level at which its content lives.

## Change vs time

Change and time are closely related but not identical:

- **Time** (from `what_is_time/attempt_001`) is the dimension along which compression makes predictions. It is an axis.
- **Change** is the non-trivial dynamics along that axis. It is the content on the axis.

A timeless-but-changing universe would be incoherent under the compression view: change requires a dimension along which the transitions occur, and that dimension is what "time" means operationally.

A time-without-change universe is conceptually stranger. If nothing ever changes, time still exists as a dimension (you could parameterize positions along it), but it would be doing no work — every "moment" would be indistinguishable from every other, and the dimension would collapse into a single point for any observer. "Time without change" is possible mathematically as a degenerate case; it is not a useful physical concept.

**Change, in short, is what makes time meaningful.** Without change, time is empty; without time, change is undefined.

## Dispositions and the causal glue

The Humean mosaic picture says change is "just succession" — state B follows state A without any necessary connection between them beyond regular co-occurrence. Dispositions theorists disagree: things have powers and tendencies, and these powers explain why B follows A rather than C.

Under the compression view, both are partly right:

- **Humean:** change IS succession of states. There is nothing additional to the sequence beyond the sequence itself.
- **Dispositional:** the sequence has structure — it is not arbitrary — and the structure is what the disposition-talk captures. Dispositions are the compressed descriptions of how states relate.

These are not competing metaphysical theses; they are complementary descriptions at different levels. The sequence is the lower-level fact; the compressed regularities in the sequence are the higher-level fact. Both exist; both are real. Dispositions are the K-content of sequences.

## The anti-problem

PROBLEM.md asks: what would a truly changeless universe look like? If every state just IS what it is, and the next state ALSO just IS what it is, what connects them into "change"?

**Answer under the compression view.** A truly changeless universe would be one where every state is identical (no distinctions across time). The "connection" between moments in a normal universe is the compressibility of their relation — the fact that state B is derivable from state A via a short rule. In a changeless universe, no derivation is needed because the states are the same.

The question "what connects them into change" misattributes the connection to some substance between the states. Under the compression view, there is no between-substance; there is only the rule, and the rule is a compressed description of the pattern of successions. The connection is nothing more than the compressibility, and nothing less. Asking for "more" than that is asking for a substance to underlie the pattern, which the Humean was right to refuse.
