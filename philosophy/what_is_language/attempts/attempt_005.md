## What is the function question?

Mountain A (behavioral) asked: *what does language look like from the outside, and can we distinguish human and LLM language behaviorally?* The finding was that most behavioral separators have closed.

Mountain D (functional) asks a different question: *what is language FOR?* This is an evolutionary and cultural question rather than a structural one. The hope is that functional framing will reveal features the behavioral framing misses.

## Candidate functions

The literature on language function includes at least these, each associated with specific researchers and traditions:

1. **Coordination of cooperation** — language lets agents coordinate action in pursuit of shared goals. Tomasello's shared intentionality framework.
2. **Teaching and transmission of knowledge** — language lets cultural learning ratchet across generations. Henrich's cultural evolution program.
3. **Social grooming / gossip** — language maintains social bonds in groups too large for manual grooming. Dunbar's "Grooming, Gossip, and the Evolution of Language."
4. **Deception and manipulation** — language lets agents misrepresent for strategic advantage. Sperber and Mercier's argumentative theory.
5. **Thought scaffolding** — language is a tool for internal cognition, not just external communication. Clark's "supersizing the mind," Carruthers's language-of-thought reconsidered, Vygotsky's inner speech.
6. **Cumulative culture** — language is the substrate that makes cumulative cultural evolution possible at all. Without language, you cannot build on the previous generation's insights at scale.
7. **Emotional expression** — language communicates internal states that are otherwise inaccessible to observers.
8. **Planning and abstract reasoning** — language enables reasoning about absent, hypothetical, or counterfactual states of affairs. Hauser, Chomsky, and Fitch's "faculty of language in the broad/narrow sense."

These are not mutually exclusive. Most contemporary theorists accept that language serves several of these functions simultaneously and that the question "what is language FOR" is a question about the *dominant* selection pressure that shaped the capacity, not about the only thing it does.

## Under compression, what is language for?

From what_is_number attempt_001 and what_is_language attempt_002, compression is a cross-cutting theme. Applied here:

**Language is the compression and transmission substrate for social regularities.**

Unpacking:

- **Compression** — Language encodes recurring social patterns (norms, practices, factual knowledge, procedural skills, causal relationships, counterfactuals) in symbolic form that is dramatically shorter than the original experiences.
- **Transmission** — Language makes those compressed patterns copyable across minds and across generations. Without language, each generation has to rediscover most of what its parents knew.
- **Social** — The patterns being compressed are largely social: what people do, what they value, what they know, how they coordinate. Physical and ecological knowledge piggybacks on the social substrate.
- **Regularities** — Compression works because social life has deep regularities (people repeat behaviors; institutions persist; norms stabilize). Language is efficient exactly because the targets of encoding are highly redundant.

Under this reading, most of the candidate functions fall out:

| Candidate | Falls out of compression+transmission? |
|-----------|-----------------------------------------|
| Coordination of cooperation | Yes — coordinated action requires shared models; language transmits models. |
| Teaching | Yes — teaching IS compressed transmission of patterns. |
| Social grooming / gossip | Partly — gossip is transmission of social-pattern updates. |
| Deception | Yes — deception exploits the transmission channel that cooperation requires. |
| Thought scaffolding | Yes — compression makes patterns manipulable internally as well as externally. |
| Cumulative culture | Yes — transmission with compression is what enables ratcheting. |
| Emotional expression | Partly — emotions are internal patterns that need dedicated compression because of their high informational value. |
| Planning and abstract reasoning | Yes — compressed patterns can be recomposed into novel combinations, which is what abstract reasoning is. |

**Finding.** The compression framing is a unifying lens on the function literature. Most candidate functions are either special cases or consequences of "compress and transmit social regularities." This is not a new theory of language; it is a restatement of convergent claims from multiple traditions under a single abstraction.

## Do LLMs fulfill the functions of language?

Going through the list:

1. **Coordination of cooperation.** Partially. LLMs can coordinate with humans who are using them (agents, assistants, pair programmers). They cannot coordinate in real-time embodied tasks without specific tooling. Limitation: no persistent stakes across sessions.
2. **Teaching.** Yes. LLMs are used extensively as teaching tools. They transmit compressed knowledge to learners effectively.
3. **Social grooming / gossip.** No. LLMs are not in a social group with ongoing relationships. They do not maintain bonds.
4. **Deception.** Partially. LLMs can be used to deceive (at the human user's direction) and can themselves produce false confident outputs that function as deception. What they lack is the strategic-agent cast of the classic deception function.
5. **Thought scaffolding.** Yes, for users. LLMs are increasingly used as extended cognition. For themselves, unclear — do they use their own language as internal thought?
6. **Cumulative culture.** Yes, partially. LLMs absorb cumulative cultural products and re-transmit them. They can add, but their contributions are not yet an autonomous culture.
7. **Emotional expression.** Partially. LLMs produce emotion-laden outputs that track human affective patterns but are not themselves expressions of internal states (by most accounts). Under γ, there might be some internal state tracking — but it is weak (attempt_003 on mind: L is low).
8. **Planning and abstract reasoning.** Yes. LLMs plan, reason abstractly, and handle counterfactuals at least at the level of competent human users.

**Summary.** LLMs fulfill most of the functions of language. They fail or underperform specifically on:

- Functions requiring **ongoing relationships and memory** (social grooming, stakes-coordination).
- Functions requiring **genuine internal states** to be expressed (authentic emotional expression).
- Functions requiring **strategic agency** over time (deception, long-horizon planning).

## Convergence with Mountain A

Mountain A (behavioral, what_is_language attempt_003) found that three residual separators remain between human and LLM language:

- Grounding to a non-text world (perception-based)
- Long-range discourse consistency (memory-based)
- Sample efficiency in learning (compression-based)

Mountain D (functional, this attempt) finds that LLMs underperform on:

- Ongoing relationships and memory
- Genuine internal states being expressed
- Strategic agency over time

**The mappings are tight.**

- Mountain A's "long-range consistency" ↔ Mountain D's "ongoing relationships and memory." Same residue, different description.
- Mountain A's "grounding to non-text" ↔ Mountain D's "internal states being expressed." Both point at the absence of a non-linguistic substrate that the linguistic surface is supposed to be about.
- Mountain A's "sample efficiency" ↔ Mountain D's "strategic agency over time." Different at first glance, but both depend on a system that persists and learns incrementally from its own experience.

**The two mountains converge on the same wall.** The residual gap between human and LLM language, from either the behavioral or the functional standpoint, is about the *host* of language: continuity, persistence, memory, grounding, and agency. The linguistic capacity itself, once hosted properly, appears to do what language is supposed to do.

This is not a defeat of the systematic approach's Multiple Mountains principle. It is a successful application of it: two independent framings agreed on the diagnosis. The wall is real.

## What this confirms and what it leaves open

**Confirms:**
- The residual language-specific gap is about hosting, not about language-qua-language.
- The behavioral and functional framings are not competing accounts — they are two views of the same underlying fact.
- The compression lens unifies disparate function-theoretic accounts.

**Leaves open:**
- Whether adding the missing host properties to an LLM (persistent memory, grounded perception, agency over time) would produce something that, under γ, has full P-meaning and P-language. This is a buildable empirical program.
- Whether the functions LLMs do NOT fulfill are accidental omissions or essential features of the category "language." The answer depends on whether language can be separated from its normal host. If yes, LLMs do language without hosting; if no, LLMs do something-language-shaped but not language proper. This is a definitional question the previous attempts left dangling.

## The gap, propagated

From what_is_language/gap.md after attempt_004:

> *Human children reach fluent language on ~10⁷ linguistic tokens. Frontier text-only LLMs need ~10¹³. What accounts for the ~10⁶× compression that humans enjoy?*

This attempt refines the answer. Most of the candidate compression mechanisms (grounding, social feedback, embodiment) are also what language is FOR. They are the host properties that make language functional in the full sense. The compression ratio and the hosting gap are the same phenomenon viewed from two angles: what it takes to LEARN language cheaply is very similar to what it takes to HOST language fully.

This is a real unification. It means that closing the sample-complexity gap and closing the functional gap would be the same achievement: build a system with the host properties, and it would learn cheaply and function fully at the same time.
