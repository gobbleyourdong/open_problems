# attempt_001 — The Bifurcation of Meaning

**Date:** 2026-04-09
**Status:** Foundation. Claims that the word "meaning" has been doing two distinct jobs in the literature; separating them dissolves several apparent deadlocks and sharpens the residual gap.

## Cross-reference

This attempt is in conversation with the what_is_language track (attempts 001–004). Those attempts pushed toward the conclusion that the residual language gap routes through either "meaning" or "consciousness" depending on framing. This attempt takes the meaning fork.

## The claim

The word "meaning," as used in philosophy of language and philosophy of mind, bundles two distinct concepts that rarely coincide in recent debate. Separating them clarifies every major position. I will call them:

- **Access-meaning (A-meaning)** — the structured regularity that supports competent linguistic behavior: inference, paraphrase, disambiguation, entailment, reference-tracking, pragmatic inference, and appropriate novel use.
- **Phenomenal-meaning (P-meaning)** — the felt grasp, the "what it is like" to understand a word, the inner intentional act of meaning something by a term.

The labels parallel Ned Block's access-consciousness / phenomenal-consciousness distinction, because the structural situation is the same: a property with a functional-behavioral specification and a phenomenal specification that historically got fused and that now, empirically, come apart.

## Why the distinction is forced

**Before LLMs.** The distinction was not forced, because every system that had access-meaning also plausibly had phenomenal-meaning (all fluent language users were human). Treating them as one property was cheap and usually harmless.

**After LLMs.** A-meaning is now demonstrably producible without a corresponding P-meaning being demonstrable. LLMs pass competence benchmarks for A-meaning (paraphrase, entailment, reference resolution, pragmatic inference — see what_is_language attempt_003). Whether they have P-meaning is exactly the contested question. The empirical situation has split an old composite into two components and forces us to pick a label for each.

## How the six classical ontologies behave under the bifurcation

### 1. Reference theory (early Frege, Russell, Mill)

**Pre-bifurcation claim.** Meaning is what the expression refers to.

**A-meaning recast.** Competent use involves tracking referents successfully across contexts. LLMs do this within the text-distribution they can access.

**P-meaning recast.** Reference cannot be the whole story (Frege already knew this — "morning star / evening star"). What needed adding was always something closer to P-meaning or to sense-as-mode-of-presentation.

**Bifurcation verdict.** Partially true of A-meaning for concrete nouns, insufficient for the full concept.

### 2. Use theory (Wittgenstein, late Philosophical Investigations)

**Pre-bifurcation claim.** Meaning is use in a language-game.

**A-meaning recast.** Use theory *is* a theory of A-meaning. It is fundamentally about how competent users deploy words, what they accept as appropriate, and how the linguistic community corrects and reinforces use. LLMs participate in this.

**P-meaning recast.** Use theory is silent about P-meaning. Wittgenstein himself resisted phenomenal readings.

**Bifurcation verdict.** A clean theory of A-meaning. Does not attempt P-meaning. Under-acknowledged strength: LLMs are a test case where use theory makes crisp predictions and seems to hold up.

### 3. Causal theory (Kripke, Putnam)

**Pre-bifurcation claim.** Meaning is fixed by a causal chain from ostensive baptism.

**A-meaning recast.** Causal chains matter for the stability of reference under change of descriptive content. This is a property of the symbol's place in a historical and social network. LLMs inherit such chains through their training text, with mediation.

**P-meaning recast.** The causal theory is indifferent to P-meaning. It is a theory about the *social metaphysics* of reference.

**Bifurcation verdict.** A theory of A-meaning with specific claims about reference stability. Neutral on P-meaning.

### 4. Conceptual role semantics (Harman, Block, Brandom, Peacocke)

**Pre-bifurcation claim.** A concept's meaning is determined by its inferential role in the cognitive system.

**A-meaning recast.** This IS A-meaning, almost by definition. If two systems have the same inferential dispositions, they have the same A-meaning.

**P-meaning recast.** Some conceptual role theorists try to extend to P-meaning ("what it is like to reason with this concept"), but the canonical version is functional.

**Bifurcation verdict.** The most direct theory of A-meaning on offer.

### 5. Embodied semantics (Lakoff, Barsalou, Glenberg)

**Pre-bifurcation claim.** Meaning is grounded in sensorimotor experience.

**A-meaning recast.** Embodiment provides a *compression prior* on A-meaning: grounded agents learn the same A-meaning with less data. (See what_is_language attempt_002 for the sample-complexity framing.) This is a claim about learning efficiency, not a claim about the nature of the learned artifact.

**P-meaning recast.** Embodiment theorists often slide into the P-meaning claim: sensorimotor grounding IS what gives words their felt grasp. This is closer to a P-meaning theory in disguise.

**Bifurcation verdict.** Two theories under one name. The sample-complexity claim is scientifically live. The felt-grasp claim is a P-meaning theory and inherits all the hardness of that category.

### 6. Distributional semantics (Firth, Harris, Lenci; LLM era)

**Pre-bifurcation claim.** Meaning is statistical context. "You shall know a word by the company it keeps."

**A-meaning recast.** The distributional hypothesis is a complete theory of A-meaning: word identity is inferential role approximated by distributional context. LLMs are the empirical confirmation that this theory is sufficient for A-meaning.

**P-meaning recast.** Distributional semantics is silent on P-meaning. It does not claim to provide it and does not need to.

**Bifurcation verdict.** The strongest current theory of A-meaning. Explicitly does not attempt P-meaning.

## The pattern

| Ontology | A-meaning | P-meaning |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Reference | Partial | No |
| Use (Wittgenstein) | **Yes** | No |
| Causal | Partial | No |
| Conceptual role | **Yes** | No |
| Embodied | Learning prior | Sometimes, disguised |
| Distributional | **Yes** | No |

**Every major theory of meaning is a theory of A-meaning.** Each is silent, dismissive, or smuggled-in on P-meaning. The Chinese Room argument, the grounding problem, and the "LLMs don't really understand" line all live on the P-meaning side — where no theory commits, where no measurement is defined, and where the debate has been circular for decades.

## What the bifurcation dissolves

- **Chinese Room.** Searle's argument attacks A-meaning theories *at the P-meaning level*. Once you separate the two, the argument does not show that the room lacks A-meaning; it shows only that the man inside lacks P-meaning. Those are compatible. The systems reply, read through this lens, is: "the room has A-meaning; whether it has P-meaning is a separate question the argument does not reach."
- **Distributional vs. grounding disputes.** Distributional theorists are doing A-meaning; grounding theorists (in their strong readings) are doing P-meaning. They talk past each other because they are theorizing different things. Once split, distributional theory wins the A-meaning ledger cleanly, and grounding theory retreats to its proper home on the P-meaning ledger.
- **The LLM "understanding" question.** Split into two questions. Do LLMs have A-meaning? Yes, per every measurement we have. Do LLMs have P-meaning? Unknown, and probably not answerable by behavioral methods.

## What the bifurcation sharpens

The real gap is no longer "what is meaning?" The real gap is:

> **Is P-meaning a real phenomenon distinct from A-meaning, or is it a residual illusion left over from the pre-LLM era when the two had no reason to come apart?**

Two live positions:

**(i)** P-meaning is real and distinct. It requires phenomenal consciousness. The gap is the hard problem. → Bridge to what_is_mind.

**(ii)** P-meaning is what A-meaning feels like from the inside of a system with self-modeling. There is no separate phenomenon. Once you have A-meaning plus meta-representation, you have meaning. Done.

Neither position is currently refuted. But note: position (ii) is now empirically testable in a way it was not five years ago. A system with complete A-meaning plus meta-representation can be built and studied. If such a system reports felt grasp indistinguishably from humans, and if no behavioral test distinguishes, the parsimonious conclusion is (ii). If a behavioral test DOES distinguish, we learn something about what P-meaning would have to be.
