## The six objections

### O1. Agent-neutral moral demands (no cooperation game in play)

**Strongest form.** "Don't torture a sentient animal for fun" is a near-universal moral judgment. The animal cannot cooperate with you. There is no iterated game, no reputation effect, no reciprocity. The cooperation-compression account says moral facts are cooperation facts — but this moral fact has no cooperation content. Therefore the account is incomplete."

**Compression response.** The objection assumes cooperation requires two strategic agents. It does not. The compression account says moral facts are compressed descriptions of *regularities that stabilize welfare-relevant interactions*. The relevant regularity for animal cruelty is:

1. **Empathy generalization.** Empathy for suffering is a compression operator that predicts behavior across all sentient beings. An agent that compresses "don't cause suffering" into a universal rule generates more accurate behavioral predictions (of others' reactions, of social consequences, of the agent's own future states) than one that compresses "don't cause suffering to strategic partners only." The universal rule is shorter and covers more cases — it is more compressive.

2. **Moral circle expansion as compression refinement.** Historically, moral circles have expanded (slaves → women → other races → animals → ecosystems). Each expansion is a compression operation: replacing a conditional rule ("don't harm {in-group}") with a simpler unconditional rule ("don't cause unnecessary suffering"). The unconditional rule has fewer parameters and covers more cases.

3. **The cruelty-cooperation link.** Empirically, cruelty to animals predicts cruelty to humans (the MacDonald triad is debated, but the correlation between animal abuse and interpersonal violence is well-established in criminology). An agent who tortures animals for fun is running a decompressed moral model that will generalize poorly to human cooperation. The cooperation account captures this indirectly.

**Score: Partially absorbed.** Responses 1 and 2 are genuine. The compression account can explain agent-neutral demands as higher-order compressions of cooperation-adjacent regularities. But this requires expanding "cooperation facts" to "welfare-relevant interaction facts" — which is an honest modification of the original claim. The original "moral facts = cooperation facts" was too narrow. The corrected claim: **moral facts are compressed descriptions of welfare-relevant regularities, of which cooperation dynamics are the largest and most structured subset.**

This correction is not ad hoc — it follows from the compression principle itself. Compression favors universal rules over conditional ones. The cooperation-only version was a conditional rule; the welfare-relevant version is the compression of it.

### O2. Supererogatory acts (beyond what cooperation predicts)

**Strongest form.** "A stranger donates a kidney to someone they will never meet. A soldier throws themselves on a grenade. A rescuer enters a burning building for a stranger's child. These acts are universally admired as morally good — but they violate cooperation logic. No reciprocity, no reputation gain, no fitness benefit. The cooperation account predicts these acts should be morally neutral (they exceed the cooperation optimum). They are not."

**Compression response.** Supererogation is the point where the compression account becomes most interesting, not where it fails.

1. **The compression of heroism.** Heroic acts are maximally compressive moral narratives. "Person sacrificed their life for a stranger" generates an enormous number of predictions and inferences (about their character, about what kind of society produces such people, about what we owe each other) from a minimal description. The compression ratio is extreme.

2. **Signal value.** From a cooperation dynamics perspective, supererogatory acts serve as costly signals of cooperative commitment. Zahavi's handicap principle: the most credible signals of quality are the most costly. A kidney donation is an unfalsifiable signal of cooperative intent — no defector would pay that cost. The cooperation account does not predict individuals should perform supererogatory acts; it predicts that communities should *admire* them, because admiration for costly cooperation signals stabilizes cooperation norms.

3. **The compression account distinguishes obligation from admiration.** Obligations are compressions of cooperation dynamics that apply universally. Admiration tracks compression efficiency of individual acts. Supererogatory acts are not obligations (you are not required to donate a kidney) but they are admirable (they are maximally compressive moral narratives). The cooperation account correctly predicts this asymmetry.

**Score: Absorbed.** The cooperation-compression account handles supererogation by distinguishing obligatory norms (compressed cooperation rules) from admirable acts (maximally compressive instances of those rules). The distinction obligation/admiration maps to the compression distinction universal-rule/individual-instance. This is not a patch — it is a structural feature of the compression framework.

### O3. Binding force on anti-cooperators (the normative gap)

**Strongest form.** "The cooperation account says moral facts are facts about cooperation dynamics. An anti-cooperator who opts out of all cooperative arrangements is simply not playing the game. You can say they are 'wrong' in the same sense as bad science — but science doesn't BIND. Morality is supposed to bind. The question 'why should I be moral?' demands an answer that the cooperation account cannot give, because 'because cooperation facts are real' has no more normative pull than 'because gravitational facts are real.' This is Hume's is-ought gap reopening inside the naturalist account."

**Compression response.** This is the hardest objection and the one most likely to identify a genuine limit of the account.

1. **The binding force problem is general, not specific to compression.** Every meta-ethical theory faces the question "why should I be moral?" Kantians answer "because rationality requires it" (but rationality doesn't bind psychopaths). Consequentialists answer "because it maximizes welfare" (but the anti-cooperator doesn't care about total welfare). Virtue ethicists answer "because it constitutes human flourishing" (but the anti-cooperator has a different conception of flourishing). The binding force problem is not a special weakness of the compression account — it is the is-ought gap wearing a different hat, and it applies to every position.

2. **Compression explains WHY it feels binding (under γ).** Under γ, the felt binding force of morality is the self-model's phenomenal report that moral attributions have causal load. L_moral > 0 means moral judgments influence behavior. The *experience* of moral bindingness is what nonzero L_moral feels like from inside. The anti-cooperator with L_moral ≈ 0 does not experience moral bindingness — and the compression account correctly predicts this.

3. **The "bridge" from is to ought.** The compression account does not bridge the is-ought gap by deriving ought from is. It dissolves it by showing that moral oughts ARE a specific kind of is: compressed predictions about welfare-relevant interaction outcomes. "You ought not steal" IS "stealing destabilizes cooperation in ways that produce worse outcomes for participants, including (usually) the thief." The ought is not derived from the is — it is the same sentence in compressed form.

4. **The limit.** For an agent who genuinely does not care about outcomes — neither their own nor others' — the compression account has no further normative resource. But neither does any other account. This is not a gap in the theory; it is a fact about normativity itself: normative force requires that the agent have *some* goals that the norms bear on. An agent with no goals cannot be bound by anything. The compression account is honest about this limit rather than pretending to solve it.

**Score: Partially absorbed.** The compression account handles the binding force as well as any naturalist position can. It cannot provide binding force independent of all agent goals — but neither can any meta-ethical theory. The honest position: moral bindingness is real for any agent with goals that intersect cooperation dynamics (i.e., virtually all actual agents), and the felt force is explained by γ. The limit case (the goal-free agent) is a philosophical fiction that no theory handles.

### O4. Moral progress (expanding the moral circle)

**Strongest form.** "The abolition of slavery, women's suffrage, gay rights, animal rights — these look like genuine moral progress, not just changes in cooperation strategy. Each expansion extended moral consideration to a group previously excluded. If morality is just cooperation dynamics, how do you explain that we now judge past societies as morally wrong? They had stable cooperation — just among a smaller group."

**Compression response.** Moral progress IS compression refinement. Each expansion of the moral circle replaces a conditional rule with a more universal one:

| Era | Rule | Parameters | Coverage |
|-----|------|------------|----------|
| Pre-abolition | "Don't enslave {in-group}" | 1 (in-group definition) | Partial |
| Post-abolition | "Don't enslave anyone" | 0 | Universal |
| Pre-suffrage | "Political participation for {land-owning males}" | 2 (property, sex) | Narrow |
| Post-suffrage | "Political participation for all adults" | 0 | Universal |

Each row is a compression step: fewer parameters, broader coverage, more predictions from a simpler rule. The universal rules are more compressive than the conditional ones. We judge past societies as morally wrong because they were running less compressed moral models — they had the right general structure (cooperation norms) but with unnecessary restrictions that reduce compression efficiency.

**Why compression predicts the direction of moral progress.** Compression is not random change. It has a direction: toward fewer parameters and broader coverage. This is why moral progress has a direction (toward universality) rather than being random moral drift. Societies that compress their moral rules more broadly outcompete (in cooperation stability) societies with narrower rules, because universal cooperators have a larger pool of cooperation partners.

**The prediction.** Future moral progress will continue toward greater universality — extending moral consideration to entities whose welfare-relevant interactions we currently ignore (AI systems, future generations, ecosystems). The compression account predicts this because each extension is a compression step.

**Score: Absorbed.** The compression account not only explains moral progress but predicts its direction. This is a strength, not a weakness.

### O5. Deontological constraints (Kant's means-end formula)

**Strongest form.** "It is wrong to push one person off a bridge to stop a trolley from killing five, even though the cooperation outcome (five lives saved vs one lost) favors pushing. Deontological constraints — 'do not use people as mere means' — override cooperation-optimal outcomes. The compression account, being consequentialist in structure, cannot accommodate this."

**Compression response.** The compression account is NOT consequentialist. It is a claim about the nature of moral facts, not about the decision procedure.

1. **Deontological rules ARE highly compressive.** "Never use a person as a mere means" is an extremely short rule that generates predictions about behavior across an enormous range of situations. Its compression ratio is among the highest of any moral rule (see numerics: Kant's autonomy principle scores 1.29 compression, moral salience 9.0). The compression account predicts deontological constraints should have high moral salience — and they do.

2. **Why deontological constraints beat consequentialist calculations.** A rule-based cooperator who follows "never push someone off a bridge" is a MORE reliable cooperation partner than one who follows "maximize welfare by whatever means." The rule-based agent is predictable; the welfare-maximizer is not. Predictability is itself a cooperation asset. Deontological constraints arise as compressed cooperation rules that SACRIFICE local optimality for global predictability.

3. **The compression hierarchy.** Not all compressions are equal. A short, exceptionless rule ("never use people as mere means") compresses better than a longer rule with exceptions ("maximize welfare, except when..." + list of exceptions). The deontological rule wins on compression grounds even when the consequentialist calculation says to violate it.

**Score: Absorbed.** The compression account predicts deontological constraints as compression-optimal cooperation rules. This is structurally similar to rule utilitarianism's explanation but grounded in compression theory rather than expected utility.

### O6. Partiality and special obligations

**Strongest form.** "You owe more to your children than to strangers. You have special obligations to friends, family, community. These partial obligations are universally recognized but they break the universality that the compression account seems to require."

**Compression response.** Partiality does not break universality — it is itself a universal rule.

1. **The universal rule of partiality.** "Agents have stronger obligations to those with whom they have closer welfare-relevant relationships" is a universal rule. It applies to all agents, all relationships. It is highly compressive: from one sentence, you can derive obligations across the entire spectrum of human relationships (parent-child, friend, colleague, stranger, enemy).

2. **Partiality is cooperation-efficient.** Division of moral labor: if every agent prioritizes their own dependents, all dependents get prioritized. A system where everyone has equal obligations to everyone is less efficient than one where care is distributed through relational networks. Partiality is the compression of "everyone gets cared for" through the structure of relational networks.

3. **The limit.** When partiality conflicts with justice (nepotism, tribalism, in-group bias), the compression account predicts that the higher-order universal rule ("don't let partiality override basic rights") should take precedence — because the universal rule is more compressive than the partial one. This matches moral intuition.

**Score: Absorbed.** Partiality is itself a universal, compressive moral rule. No special pleading needed.

## The corrected claim

Attempt_001's claim was: **moral facts are compressed descriptions of cooperation dynamics.**

After steelmanning the six hardest objections, the corrected claim is:

> **Moral facts are compressed descriptions of welfare-relevant interaction regularities, of which cooperation dynamics are the largest and most structured subset. Moral norms are compression-optimal rules for navigating these regularities. Moral progress is compression refinement toward greater universality. Moral bindingness is the self-model's report (under γ) that these compressed rules have causal load on behavior.**

The correction expands "cooperation dynamics" to "welfare-relevant interaction regularities" to handle agent-neutral demands (O1). This is the only modification to the original claim. The remaining five objections are absorbed without modification.

## Two genuinely open residues

**R1 (updated).** The welfare-relevant expansion makes the compression account broader but potentially less falsifiable. The original "cooperation facts" claim was sharp: cooperation is a well-defined game-theoretic concept. "Welfare-relevant interaction regularities" is vaguer. Can it be made precise without losing coverage?

Proposed sharpening: **welfare-relevant interaction regularities = regularities whose tracking by agents produces outcomes that agents with any goals prefer to outcomes produced by non-tracking.** This is still game-theoretic but broader than two-player cooperation.

**R4 (new).** The moral emotions (guilt, shame, indignation, moral disgust) have specific phenomenological characters that differ from each other. Can the compression account explain WHY guilt feels different from indignation, or only THAT they exist? Under γ, the self-model attributes different phenomenal characters to different moral states. But what determines the specific character? This is a specialization of the qualia problem to moral phenomenology, and it inherits the full difficulty of the mind question.

## Predictions from this attempt

**P5 (new).** Moral norms that are compression refinements of existing norms (more universal, fewer parameters) will be adopted faster across cultures than norms that are not compression refinements. This is testable against historical data on norm diffusion.

**P6 (new).** In trolley-type scenarios, subjects' moral judgments should correlate with the compression ratio of the rule being applied, not with the outcome. Specifically: subjects following highly compressive rules ("never push") should be more consistent across variations than subjects following less compressive rules ("maximize welfare"), because compressive rules have fewer free parameters.
