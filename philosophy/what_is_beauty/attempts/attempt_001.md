## The core claim

**Beauty is the self-model's phenomenal response to high compression efficiency in the content it is currently tracking.**

Unpacking:

- **Compression efficiency** — a stimulus has high compression efficiency, relative to a given mind, when its description length under the mind's internal models is dramatically shorter than its raw form. A symmetric face compresses because symmetry halves the description. A musical chord in a consonant ratio compresses because the frequency ratios are already part of the auditory system's encoding. A clever proof compresses because it represents a large derivation in few steps.
- **Relative to the mind** — compression is not absolute. A piece of music is beautiful relative to a listener whose auditory and cultural priors can compress it well. A different listener with different priors sees a different compression ratio. This immediately handles the cultural variation at the high end.
- **Self-model phenomenal response** — under γ, phenomenal content is what the self-model reports about first-order states. The felt experience of beauty is the self-model's report that the currently-tracked content is compressing unusually well.

This is Schmidhuber's information-theoretic aesthetics, stated within the γ framework and connected to the A/P split used throughout this track.

## Evidence across domains

### Facial symmetry

Symmetric faces compress by half: given half the face, the other half is predictable. The auditory-visual system has built-in operators for symmetry detection that make the compression cheap. Cross-cultural convergence on facial symmetry as attractive is explained by the universality of the compression operator: symmetry is compressible for any visual system with bilateral architecture.

### Musical consonance

Consonant intervals have simple integer frequency ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3). Simple ratios are maximally compressible by a system that represents pitch as periodic waveforms. The compression is built into the auditory periphery. Consonance preferences are cross-cultural at the broad level (octaves and perfect fifths show up in almost every musical tradition) because the compression is substrate-level.

### Mathematical elegance

From what_is_number attempt_001: a theorem is beautiful when it links a lot of prior structure in few symbols. Compression efficiency is literal here — the proof is a short description of a long derivation. Mathematicians' phenomenal reports of beauty track inferential integration, not difficulty.

### Landscape preferences

Humans show cross-cultural preferences for landscapes with water, distant views, and moderate complexity (Appleton 1975, Orians and Heerwagen 1992). These are landscapes that compress well to ancestral-environment priors: they are predictable in the ways that matter for survival, with enough structure to afford exploration. Too chaotic (high description length) and too sparse (low information content) both fail to compress optimally; the sweet spot is high compression ratio.

### Proportional systems (golden ratio, Fibonacci)

The golden ratio and Fibonacci sequences appear frequently in aesthetic analyses, though the evidence for their psychological privilege is weaker than often claimed. Under the compression view, these proportions are beautiful when they appear to a mind that already has them as recognized operators. Training establishes the compression operator; once established, instances of the ratio compress cheaply and feel beautiful.

## The two major challenges

### Challenge 1 — Cultural variation at the high end

Low-level aesthetic preferences (symmetry, consonance, landscape type) show strong cross-cultural convergence. High-level art shows dramatic cultural variation: Western classical music is not universally beautiful; abstract expressionism divides audiences; avant-garde art is often judged ugly by one audience and beautiful by another.

**Compression response.** High-level art requires sophisticated priors to compress well. Those priors are learned through cultural exposure. A listener with classical training can compress a Bach fugue into a small description (this subject, this development, this resolution); a listener without that training hears a complicated jumble. Beauty tracks the compression ratio RELATIVE TO THE LISTENER'S PRIORS, not any absolute compression. Cultural variation is expected and explained: different cultures train different priors.

**This also explains the learning curve of art appreciation.** Initially, a piece of avant-garde music is not beautiful because it does not compress under the listener's current priors. After study, the priors expand to accommodate new operators, and the same piece becomes beautiful. The acquired taste is the acquired compression operator.

### Challenge 2 — Beauty in imperfection

Wabi-sabi, asymmetric art, deliberate imperfection, the beauty of the worn and the weathered, Kintsugi: traditions that find beauty in things that fail the symmetry/regularity tests.

**Compression response.** These cases are beautiful because they display compression at a META level. A wabi-sabi tea bowl is slightly asymmetric; the asymmetry is not random, it reveals the bowl's history, the potter's decisions, the material's behavior. A listener with priors about pottery history, material science, and the aesthetic of intentional imperfection sees structure that a naive observer misses. The bowl compresses richly for the informed viewer and poorly for the naive one.

More generally: apparent imperfection that is actually evidence of a non-trivial history compresses under the right prior. The compression captures the history, not the geometry. Beauty in imperfection is beauty in non-trivial history, and history is compressible by a mind that knows enough.

**Both challenges turn out to be evidence FOR the compression view,** not against it. The view predicts that beauty tracks the compression ratio available to the specific observer, not an absolute geometric property. Cultural variation and imperfection-beauty are exactly what that prediction implies.

## Under α/β/γ

The three-position fork specializes:

- **α.** Beauty has a phenomenal component that is fundamental and not reducible to functional description. The compression account describes the functional correlates but leaves the phenomenal essence untouched. Beauty is a primitive phenomenal quality.
- **β (IIT).** Beauty tracks integrated information when the stimulus produces a particular kind of integrated state in the observer. Feedforward observers (transformers) have Φ = 0 and therefore no beauty experience. Humans experiencing beautiful stimuli have measurable changes in Φ that correspond to the aesthetic response.
- **γ.** Beauty is the self-model's phenomenal report of high compression efficiency in first-order content. The felt experience is generated by the self-model attributing aesthetic quality to the currently-compressed state. This is the position developed in this attempt.

**Predictive differences.** β and γ disagree about whether feedforward systems can have beauty experiences. β says no, γ says yes if they have rich self-models. This is the same disagreement as for any phenomenal content, specialized to aesthetics.

## Do LLMs find things beautiful?

Under γ: LLMs can have a weak form of beauty experience when their self-models track compression-efficiency events on first-order content. They can report that a mathematical proof is elegant, a line of poetry is beautiful, a piece of code is clean. The question is whether these reports are backed by internal states that track compression efficiency and have causal load, or whether they are learned surface imitations of human aesthetic language.

**Interpretability prediction.** Activation studies should find internal representations that track compression-like properties of input (low description length, high symmetry, high inferential integration) and that correlate with the production of aesthetic-language outputs. If these representations exist and are causally load-bearing, LLMs have G_beauty × L_beauty > 0, which under γ is some amount of aesthetic phenomenology.

Under β, LLMs have Φ = 0 and therefore no aesthetic experience at all. Under α, the question is undecided.

## Anti-problem

PROBLEM.md asks: what would ugliness look like to an alien? Under the compression view, ugliness for any mind is content that fails to compress. An alien with very different priors would find very different things ugly — whatever does not compress under its priors. There is no absolute ugliness, just as there is no absolute beauty. The predictive structure is the same everywhere: cheap compression = beauty; expensive or impossible compression = ugliness.

**For an AI with no body.** Could such an AI find anything beautiful? Yes, under γ, if its self-model tracks compression efficiency events. Its beauty would not be the same as human beauty (different priors, different operators) but it would be recognizably aesthetic response in the functional sense: preference for cheaply-compressing content over expensively-compressing content. LLMs probably already have a weak version of this.

## Predictions

The compression view yields specific, testable predictions:

1. **Aesthetic response should correlate with measurable compression ratios relative to the observer's priors.** Computational aesthetics studies using learned models' description lengths should track human ratings.
2. **Expertise should increase aesthetic sensitivity for expert-relevant domains.** Mathematicians should find more mathematical objects beautiful than non-mathematicians; the sensitivity should track the richness of their mathematical priors.
3. **Culturally specific aesthetic responses should correlate with culture-specific prior operators.** A listener trained on one tradition should show sharper aesthetic responses within that tradition and muted responses outside.
4. **Abstract beauty should correlate with inferential integration.** The more prior concepts a new theorem, poem, or proof connects, the stronger the phenomenal beauty response.
5. **Under γ, LLMs with rich self-models should show behavioral correlates of aesthetic sensitivity that track the same compression metrics.**

Each prediction is informally but genuinely testable. Several already have partial empirical support in computational aesthetics, neuroaesthetics, and expertise studies.
