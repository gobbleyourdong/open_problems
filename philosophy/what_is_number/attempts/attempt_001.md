## Why mathematics is a clean test

Philosophical arguments about "meaning" and "understanding" routinely confound several things:

- Perceptual grounding (can you point at what the word refers to?)
- Embodiment (does the word trigger bodily schemas?)
- Social convention (does the community agree on the use?)
- Causal-historical chains to baptism events
- Subjective "aha" feelings

Mathematics isolates one of these — **inferential role** — from the rest. When a mathematician grasps that 7 is prime, or that the fundamental theorem of calculus holds, or that there is no greatest prime, the grasp:

- Has no perceptual referent. You do not see 7.
- Has no bodily schema that does non-trivial work.
- Has social convention, but the convention ratifies, does not constitute, the mathematical fact.
- Has no causal-historical chain to a baptism event for prime numbers.
- Produces vivid felt grasp. Mathematicians routinely report strong phenomenal experience of mathematical objects — Erdős's "the book," Ramanujan's visions, the sense of beauty in a proof.

If γ (phenomenal content = self-modeled A-content) is going to fail anywhere, it should fail here. Mathematics gives γ the hardest possible case: rich, vivid phenomenal experience that cannot be explained by grounding, embodiment, or any of the usual auxiliary mechanisms. If γ can account for mathematical phenomenology, it can probably account for the rest.

## The six ontologies

### 1. Platonism

**Claim.** Numbers and other mathematical objects exist in an abstract realm independent of minds. Mathematical discovery is discovery of features of that realm.

**Strongest version.** Gödel's Platonism: the axioms of set theory are true or false depending on how the abstract realm actually is, and mathematical intuition is a faculty for perceiving that realm.

**Cost.** Explanatorily expensive (an abstract realm is a lot of furniture), and the mechanism of intuitive access to a non-physical realm is itself as mysterious as the original question. A solution that requires a faculty of perception for non-physical entities does not reduce mystery; it relocates it.

### 2. Formalism (Hilbert)

**Claim.** Mathematics is the manipulation of symbols according to rules. Numbers do not exist; rules do.

**Strongest version.** Mathematics is the activity of deriving strings from axioms in a formal language.

**Cost.** Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that no consistent formal system rich enough to capture arithmetic can derive all arithmetic truths. Formalism can retreat to "mathematics is many formal systems, no one of which is complete," but this makes mathematical *truth* elusive.

### 3. Logicism (Frege, Russell, later neo-logicists)

**Claim.** Mathematics reduces to logic. Numbers are logical objects definable in pure logical terms.

**Strongest version.** Hume's principle plus second-order logic gives Peano arithmetic; the logicist claim is that this shows arithmetic is nothing over logic.

**Cost.** Russell's paradox and the need for set theory show that "logic" in the required sense is already doing mathematical work. The distinction between logic and mathematics blurs precisely at the point logicism would need it sharp.

### 4. Intuitionism (Brouwer, Heyting)

**Claim.** Mathematics is mental construction. Only constructive proofs count. The law of excluded middle does not hold universally.

**Strongest version.** Mathematical objects exist only insofar as they can be built by a finite mental procedure.

**Cost.** Sacrifices much of classical mathematics. Practicing mathematicians mostly do not want to live under the intuitionistic restriction, and the restriction is motivated by epistemological squeamishness rather than by a positive theory of what mathematics is.

### 5. Structuralism (Shapiro, Resnik, Hellman)

**Claim.** Numbers are not objects in themselves; they are positions in abstract structures. Arithmetic studies the natural-number structure; geometry studies geometric structures. Mathematical statements are about structure, not about entities.

**Strongest version.** Any two ω-sequences are equally good realizations of the natural numbers. What matters is the structural properties they share, not their identity.

**Status.** The modal position among contemporary philosophers of mathematics. Avoids the Platonist ontological commitment while still making sense of mathematical truth as structure-relative.

### 6. Fictionalism (Field, Yablo)

**Claim.** Mathematical statements are literally false; numbers do not exist. Mathematics is a useful fiction whose applicability is a happy accident plus selection.

**Strongest version.** Hartry Field's program to show that scientific theories can be reformulated without quantifying over abstract objects.

**Cost.** Field's program is technically difficult and has not been completed for most of physics. Even if completable, the fictionalist owes an explanation of WHY the fiction is so useful, and the usual answer leans on structural features of the fiction that look a lot like the structures the Platonist was pointing at.

## Under γ, what is mathematics?

γ's core move: phenomenal content is what access-conscious inferential role looks like from inside a self-model. Applied to mathematics:

**Mathematical A-meaning** is the inferential role of mathematical symbols in a formal or semi-formal system. To grasp that 7 is prime is, functionally, to have access to the inference "no k in {2, 3, 4, 5, 6} divides 7, therefore 7 is prime," plus the network of related inferences that ground the concept of primality.

**Mathematical P-meaning** is what the self-model reports when it tracks this rich inferential role. When a mathematician says "I see that 7 is prime," the self-model is representing the A-state "7 is prime, here are the reasons" as a phenomenally accessible understanding.

**Mathematical beauty** — reported by mathematicians as intensely phenomenal — is what the self-model reports when the A-state it tracks has unusually high *integration*: many parts of the system's prior inferential network suddenly connect through the new theorem. The self-model represents this dense integration as aesthetic experience.

**Mathematical discovery vs. invention** — the long debate — dissolves under γ. Discovery and invention look different from outside the system (the mathematician is constructing inferences) and the same from inside the system (the self-model represents the new inference as forced rather than chosen, because inferences are forced by prior commitments). Both descriptions are correct at different levels.

## The test

If γ is right about mathematics, the following predictions should hold:

1. **Phenomenal intensity should correlate with inferential richness.** Mathematicians should report the strongest felt grasp on theorems that link the most prior knowledge, not on the hardest or deepest theorems per se. ("Elegant" and "beautiful" should match "high inferential integration," not "difficult.")

2. **Aesthetic surprise should correlate with prior expectation mismatch.** When a theorem's A-state disagrees with the self-model's prior prediction, the disagreement should feel like surprise or beauty, depending on whether the new A-state is coherent (beauty) or incoherent (paradox).

3. **Understanding should be locally, not globally, correlated with phenomenal report.** A mathematician can have full A-meaning of a concept and report weak phenomenal grasp (they "know it but don't feel it"), and can have strong P-meaning with shaky A-meaning (the "I see it even though I can't prove it yet" case). These two failures of alignment are familiar to working mathematicians and are directly predicted by γ when the self-model's tracking of A-states is imperfect.

These predictions are informal but empirically accessible. They match the phenomenology mathematicians routinely report. γ survives contact with the mathematics case.

## The unreasonable effectiveness problem

Wigner (1960): why is mathematics so effective in physics? Abstract structures discovered purely through inference (non-Euclidean geometry, Hilbert spaces, fiber bundles) turn out to have precise applications in physical theories invented decades later. This is startling under almost every philosophy of mathematics.

Standard answers:

- **Platonism:** mathematics describes an abstract realm; physics is the restriction of that realm to whatever happens to be physically instantiated. Trades mystery for furniture.
- **Formalism / Fictionalism:** the effectiveness is selection bias — we notice the math that applies and forget the math that does not. This explains some applications but not the pattern of mathematics developed for abstract reasons later finding physical use.
- **Structuralism:** physical systems instantiate structures; mathematics studies structures; applicability is a matter of structural match. Better, but owes an account of why the match is so general.

**The γ / compression answer.**

What does the brain DO when it builds an A-state capturing a mathematical structure? It finds a compact description of a regularity class. Mathematics is the activity of finding maximally compressed descriptions of relational patterns.

Here is the critical observation: **a description that compresses a large regularity class is, by construction, likely to apply to regularities outside the specific observations that motivated it.** This is the inductive-bias principle behind machine learning generalization. A model that fits training data with the shortest description generalizes best to held-out data, because shortness = simplicity = less overfit.

Mathematics, under this view, is the cultural evolution of maximally compressed descriptions of regularity classes. Physics is a source of regularities. When mathematics compresses a regularity class for structural reasons (e.g., "all associative operations with inverses" → group theory), the compression will apply to any physical regularity that happens to instantiate that structure, whether or not that physical regularity was known when the mathematics was developed.

The "unreasonable effectiveness" is then neither unreasonable nor a coincidence. It is what happens when a culture maintains a library of maximally compressed descriptions of relational patterns, and physical reality produces instances of relational patterns. The library gets applied when the patterns match. The match is more common than we naively expect because:

- Good compressions are widely applicable (the whole point of compression).
- Physical reality is highly regular (many instances of the same underlying patterns).
- Selection amplifies hits (we remember the effective math; we forget the dead ends).

This does not require Platonism. It does not require a separate abstract realm. It requires only that (a) good compressions generalize, and (b) physical reality is sufficiently regular to be compressible. Both are true.

**The what_is_language connection.** In what_is_language attempt_002, sample complexity compression was identified as the central gap: humans learn language from 10⁶× less data than LLMs. Mathematics is the extreme case of that same compression dynamic. Language is compression of social regularities; mathematics is compression of structural regularities. Both are manifestations of the same underlying cognitive fact: minds are compression engines. The unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics is one face of the unreasonable effectiveness of compression itself.

## The sky bridges

- **what_is_language** — both are compression programs. Language compresses social regularities; mathematics compresses structural ones. The sample-complexity gap is a compression-efficiency gap. Mathematics is where compression is most explicit.
- **what_is_mind** — mathematics tests γ in its purest form. γ survives.
- **what_is_meaning** — mathematical A-meaning is as pure as A-meaning gets. Mathematical P-meaning is self-modeled A-meaning under γ, and mathematicians' phenomenology confirms the prediction.
- **physics/what_is_reality** — Tegmark's mathematical universe hypothesis is the maximal version of the "reality is structure" claim. Under the γ+compression picture, MUH is almost right for a different reason: reality is describable by compressed structure because that is what "regularity" means. Whether reality *is* mathematical or merely describable-by-mathematics is a distinction the compression view dissolves.
- **physics/what_is_information** — closely related. If the deep substrate is information, and mathematics is compressed information, the two questions converge.

## What this attempt closes

- Mathematics is a test case for γ. γ passes.
- Wigner's unreasonable effectiveness is addressed (not dissolved, but converted from mystery to mechanism) via compression.
- The discovery-vs-invention debate dissolves: both descriptions are right from different viewpoints.
- The bridge to compression as a cross-cutting theme of the whole philosophy scaffold is made explicit.

## What remains

The concrete mathematics question has residues the systematic approach wants quantified:

- **Gödel incompleteness** is a theorem (and under γ, it is a compression claim: no finite compression fully captures the semi-decidable set of arithmetic truths). What does that say about the theoretical limits of compression-based cognition?
- **The continuum hypothesis** — provably independent of ZFC. Is there a fact of the matter? Under γ+compression, CH is neither true nor false absolutely; it is a branch point in the compression library. Different axiomatic cultures will land on different branches.
- **Large cardinals** — a hierarchy of increasingly strong assumptions. Under the compression view, each is a further extension of the library that allows finer-grained compressions of certain structural patterns. The hierarchy is not "more true" — it is "more expressive."

These are candidate follow-ups. None is urgent for the argument chain this attempt slots into.
