# Attempt 006 — Why Do Minds Find Numbers Meaningful?

## The question

Independent of whether numbers have objective structural prominence (see what_is_number/attempt_003), why does consciousness attribute meaning, luck, beauty, sacredness, or significance to specific numbers? What is the mechanism?

This is a what_is_mind question, not a what_is_number question. Even if 3 has zero structural weight in the universe, humans would still call it lucky. Why?

## The candidate mechanisms

### 1. Subitizing threshold

Humans can perceive 1-3 objects instantly without counting (subitizing). Above 4, we have to count. Numbers within the subitizing range (1, 2, 3) feel "graspable" — the mind can hold them without effort. Numbers above it (7, 13, 29) feel "effortful."

Numbers that feel graspable acquire positive associations (simple, clean, natural). Numbers that feel effortful acquire negative or mysterious associations (complex, uncanny, difficult).

This predicts:
- 1, 2, 3 should be universally "positive" or "fundamental" — confirmed cross-culturally
- 7 should feel "mystical" or "special" — at the boundary of working memory (Miller's 7±2)
- Large primes (13, 17, 29) should feel "uncanny" — beyond comfortable processing

The mechanism is not about the numbers. It's about the PROCESSING COST of the numbers in the human cognitive architecture.

### 2. Frequency of encounter (mere exposure effect)

Small numbers appear more frequently in daily life (1 apple, 2 hands, 3 meals, 4 seasons). The mere exposure effect (Zajonc 1968) predicts that stimuli encountered more often are perceived more positively. Numbers we see more often feel more meaningful.

This predicts:
- Culturally prominent numbers should track the physical environment (4 seasons, 5 fingers, 7 days)
- Different cultures with different environmental constants should have different "meaningful" numbers — partially confirmed (Chinese preference for 8 tracks Mandarin phonetics, not universal environmental features)

The mechanism is associative learning, not numerical structure.

### 3. Compression and prediction (Schmidhuber / Kolmogorov)

The brain rewards itself for successful compression of experience. A number that helps compress (predict, explain, pattern-match) feels "beautiful" or "meaningful." A number that doesn't compress anything feels "random" or "meaningless."

3 helps compress: triangles (minimal polygon), 3-body dynamics, syllogistic logic (major, minor, conclusion), narrative structure (beginning, middle, end). The brain encounters 3 as a useful compression unit across many domains.

7 helps compress: days of the week, musical scale (diatonic), rainbow colors (cultural, not physical). 7 is at the edge of working memory — the LARGEST number that can serve as a compression unit without chunking.

This predicts:
- Numbers that serve as compression units across MORE domains feel more "meaningful"
- The feeling of "meaning" is the reward signal for successful compression
- Mathematical beauty IS low Kolmogorov complexity experienced from inside

This connects to what_is_beauty/attempt (CompressionBeauty: r=+0.723, p=0.003 — beauty correlates with compressibility).

### 4. Apophenia (false pattern detection)

The brain's pattern-detection system has a bias toward false positives (seeing patterns that aren't there) over false negatives (missing patterns that are). This is evolutionarily adaptive: thinking you see a tiger when there's only a bush costs nothing; missing a real tiger costs your life.

Applied to numbers: the brain will find "meaningful" patterns in ANY sufficiently large number sequence, even random ones. 3-6-9 "appearing" in a 29-digit number is exactly what the pattern detector is designed to do — and it will do it regardless of whether 3-6-9 is actually structurally prominent.

This predicts:
- Humans will report finding "meaningful" numbers in random data — confirmed (birthday paradox, Ramsey theory thresholds)
- The feeling of "discovery" when finding a number pattern is indistinguishable from the feeling of discovering a real pattern — this IS the apophenia mechanism
- Numerology persists across all cultures because it is built into the cognitive architecture, not learned

### 5. Social construction and memetic transmission

Numbers acquire meaning through cultural transmission, not individual cognition. "13 is unlucky" is a Western cultural meme (Last Supper, Norse mythology). "4 is unlucky" is an East Asian meme (四 sounds like 死, death). Neither reflects anything about the numbers themselves.

Once a number acquires cultural meaning, confirmation bias sustains it: people notice when something bad happens on the 13th and don't notice when nothing happens on the 13th. The meaning is self-reinforcing through selective attention.

This predicts:
- Number meanings should be culturally variable — confirmed (4 is lucky in Western culture, unlucky in Chinese)
- Number meanings should be resistant to evidence — confirmed (no amount of data showing 13 is statistically normal eliminates the superstition)
- Number meanings should cluster around culturally salient quantities — confirmed

## The interaction between what_is_number and what_is_mind

These mechanisms explain why minds ATTRIBUTE meaning. They are silent on whether numbers HAVE structural prominence. Both things can be true simultaneously:

```
UNIVERSE SIDE (what_is_number):
  3 spatial dimensions — structural fact
  3 quark colors — structural fact
  pi, e, phi — structural constants
  These exist regardless of observers.

MIND SIDE (what_is_mind):
  Subitizing makes 3 feel "graspable"
  Compression makes 3 feel "useful"
  Apophenia makes 3 feel "everywhere"
  Culture makes 3 feel "significant"
  These exist regardless of whether 3 is structurally prominent.

THE INTERACTION:
  If 3 IS structurally prominent AND minds are calibrated to
  notice it, the detection is CORRECT (not apophenia).
  If 3 is NOT structurally prominent but minds think it is,
  the detection is FALSE POSITIVE (apophenia).
  
  Distinguishing these requires the null hypothesis test
  from what_is_number/attempt_003: W(n) ~ 1/n normalization.
```

The mind mechanisms run regardless. The question is whether the universe gives them real signal or just noise to chew on.

## The gap

No experiment has been designed that simultaneously measures:
1. The structural prominence of a number (across independent physical/mathematical domains)
2. The cognitive processing signature when a human encounters that number (fMRI, EEG)
3. The correlation between (1) and (2) after controlling for exposure frequency and cultural priming

Such an experiment would tell us whether the mind's "meaning attribution" tracks actual structural prominence or is purely endogenous.

Until that experiment is designed and run, the what_is_number and what_is_mind sides of this question remain independently open.

## Sky bridges

- **what_is_number/attempt_003** — the structural prominence question (independent, linked here)
- **what_is_beauty** — CompressionBeauty result (r=+0.723): beauty IS compression. Number meaning might be the same mechanism applied to abstract objects.
- **what_is_knowing** — does "knowing" a number's meaning count as knowledge? Only if it reduces uncertainty about something (information-theoretic definition). Lucky 7 reduces no uncertainty; pi reduces uncertainty about circles. Different kinds of "meaning."
- **what_is_reality** — if numbers are structurally prominent AND minds detect that prominence, does that mean reality is fundamentally mathematical? (Tegmark bridge)

OPEN. Five cognitive mechanisms catalogued. The interaction between objective prominence and subjective attribution is the gap. Testable in principle but no experiment designed.
