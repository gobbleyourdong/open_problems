## The question and its scaffold

The six competing ontologies:

1. **Cartesian ego** — continuous non-physical substance
2. **Bundle theory** (Hume) — self is a bundle of perceptions, no continuous entity
3. **Narrative self** — the self is the story we tell about ourselves
4. **No-self** (anatta, Buddhism) — the self is an illusion constructed by aggregates
5. **Biological continuity** — the self is the continuous organism
6. **Psychological continuity** (Locke, Parfit) — the self is continuous memory/personality

## Parfit's move

Derek Parfit, *Reasons and Persons* (1984), made a move that many philosophers consider the most important contribution to the self question in the 20th century.

**Parfit's claim.** When we ask "is the person at t₂ the same person as the one at t₁?" we are asking a question with no fact of the matter in borderline cases (teleportation, gradual replacement, fission). But we think it matters because we think personal identity is "what matters" in survival. Parfit's central argument: **identity is not what matters; psychological continuity is.**

**Why this matters.** The thought experiments (teleportation, fission, gradual-replacement Ship of Theseus) break every theory of *strict identity* because they construct cases where the identity question has no answer while the thing-we-actually-care-about is cleanly specifiable. Once you stop asking "is this still me?" and instead ask "is there enough psychological continuity for the things I care about to apply?" the borderline cases become comfortable. The absence of a strict identity fact is not a mystery; it is a discovery.

**What Parfit closes.** The puzzle of edge cases (teleportation, gradual replacement, split-brain cases) ceases to be a problem. The correct response is "there is psychological continuity of the right kind or there isn't; nothing additional needs to be decided."

**What Parfit does not close.** The metaphysics of momentary persons, the ethics of future selves under uncertainty about continuity, and the phenomenology of felt continuous selfhood — all these remain.

## Metzinger's move

Thomas Metzinger, *Being No One* (2003), carried the Parfitian dissolution into the phenomenology of self-experience.

**Metzinger's claim.** There is no self. What exists is a **phenomenal self-model** (PSM) that represents the system as having a self. The self-model is a construction by the cognitive system that is transparent to itself — the system cannot see its own self-model as a model, so it experiences the model's content directly as "me."

**Why this matters for γ.** γ (illusionism in the mind question) says phenomenal consciousness is what access-consciousness looks like from inside a self-model. Metzinger's PSM is exactly the kind of self-model γ needs. Position γ + Metzinger's PSM = a complete functionalist-friendly theory of phenomenal self-experience.

**Status.** Metzinger's account is controversial but well-developed. It is the most rigorous current version of the illusionism family, and it has been extended and refined across two decades.

## Walking the six ontologies

### 1. Cartesian ego

**Survival of the thought experiments.** No. The Cartesian ego is vulnerable to every edge case, because it posits a continuous non-physical substance whose presence or absence is (by hypothesis) not detectable by physical investigation. Teleportation, fission, and gradual replacement leave Cartesians either with arbitrary stipulations about when the ego departs, or with the view that the ego is indifferent to physical continuity in ways that make it disconnected from the phenomena we wanted to explain.

**Compatibility with γ.** None. γ explicitly denies a non-physical substance and treats phenomenal self-experience as constructed by the system's self-modeling apparatus.

**Verdict.** Killed by the edge cases, incompatible with γ, no longer a live position in mainstream philosophy of mind.

### 2. Bundle theory (Hume)

**Survival of the thought experiments.** Yes. A bundle has no continuous entity to lose; "the self" across time is just the same bundle under different descriptions. Edge cases become questions about whether the bundle at t₁ and the bundle at t₂ share enough content to count as "the same bundle for the purposes at hand."

**Compatibility with γ.** High. γ's self-model fits cleanly into a bundle-theoretic framework: the self-model is one perception among many in the bundle, and it is the perception that represents the bundle as having a self.

**Verdict.** Survives, compatible with γ.

### 3. Narrative self

**Survival of the thought experiments.** Mostly yes. Narratives survive gradual changes gracefully because narratives are designed to handle character development. Sudden changes (teleportation) are harder for the narrative view but not fatal.

**Compatibility with γ.** High. A narrative is a particular kind of self-model — one with temporal structure, plot, and agency attribution. γ is agnostic to whether the self-model takes narrative form; narrative is one good way to build one.

**Verdict.** Survives, compatible with γ.

### 4. No-self (anatta)

**Survival of the thought experiments.** Yes, trivially. If there is no self, there is nothing to lose or preserve in edge cases.

**Compatibility with γ.** Very high. Anatta in its Buddhist philosophical form is essentially the claim that the self is a construction by the aggregates — a self-model, in modern terms. The meditative practice aimed at anatta is aimed at seeing through one's own self-model, which γ+Metzinger would describe as making the PSM opaque to itself. The two views converge on the same structural claim from different traditions.

**Verdict.** Survives, strongly compatible with γ.

### 5. Biological continuity

**Survival of the thought experiments.** Partial. Handles gradual replacement poorly (at what point does the replaced body stop being "the same organism"?). Handles teleportation worse. Survives in restricted forms.

**Compatibility with γ.** Moderate. γ is substrate-neutral — it does not care whether the self-model is implemented in biology or silicon. Biological continuity, by stipulating biology, is stronger than γ needs. Compatible but restrictive.

**Verdict.** Partial survival, compatible with γ only in biology-restricted readings.

### 6. Psychological continuity (Parfit-style)

**Survival of the thought experiments.** Yes, by construction. Parfit built the theory around the edge cases.

**Compatibility with γ.** High. Psychological continuity IS what γ cares about: the self-model tracks the system's own mental states over time, and continuity of self is continuity of the self-model's content and connectivity.

**Verdict.** Survives, fully compatible with γ, and is the position most directly aligned with the γ story at the mind level.

## The pattern

| Ontology | Survives edge cases? | Compatible with γ? |
|----------|---------------------|--------------------|
| Cartesian ego | No | No |
| Bundle (Hume) | Yes | Yes |
| Narrative | Mostly | Yes |
| No-self (anatta) | Yes | Yes, strongly |
| Biological continuity | Partial | Restricted |
| Psychological continuity (Parfit) | Yes, by design | Yes, tightly |

**The positions that survive Parfit are exactly the positions that support γ.** This is not a coincidence. Both Parfit and γ are motivated by the same underlying thought: "the self" is not a substance or continuous entity, it is a functional-representational structure that does the work we attribute to selfhood.

This convergence is itself evidence. Two independent lines of argument — thought experiments about personal identity, and theories of phenomenal consciousness — land on the same minimal-self conclusion. The systematic approach would call this a Multiple Mountains convergence.

## The unusual status of the self question

Most tier-0 generative questions in this scaffold have a wide-open gap. The self question is different: **the main gap was substantially closed by Parfit, extended by Metzinger, and the closure is accepted by a significant fraction of professional philosophers of mind.** Yet the question is perceived as open at the popular level because the Parfitian conclusion is counterintuitive and most people find it hard to accept.

The residual gap is therefore mostly sociological: what makes the Parfitian picture so hard to accept even when the argument is granted? And is that difficulty of acceptance evidence that the picture is missing something phenomenologically, or evidence that the phenomenology is simply misleading us?

**The honest assessment for the systematic approach:**

- The logical gap is mostly closed. Parfit + Metzinger + bundle/no-self traditions converge.
- The phenomenological gap is partial. Felt continuous selfhood is real and is accounted for by the self-model being transparent to itself. Whether this account is satisfying is disputed.
- The residual gap is: **is the phenomenology of continuous selfhood explained away by the self-model, or does the phenomenology show that the self-model is not the whole story?** This is position (i) vs (ii) for selfhood, exactly paralleling the mind and meaning questions.

## Load on γ

The mind/attempt_003 framing of γ required a self-model with the three operational properties (referring to own states, causally connected, accessible). This attempt confirms that such a self-model is philosophically defensible, has direct precedent in Metzinger's PSM framework, and is supported by a converging cluster of traditions (Hume, Parfit, Buddhist anatta, neo-Humean psychological continuity).

**The self-model γ needs is not philosophically exotic.** It is the modal position among contemporary philosophers of mind who take the Parfitian and Metzingerian arguments seriously.

The main alternative — a Cartesian ego or similarly substantive self — is both philosophically untenable and incompatible with γ. Any serious opponent of γ therefore has to argue not just that γ is wrong but that the self itself has more structure than the minimal-self cluster allows. That is a hard case to make after Parfit.
