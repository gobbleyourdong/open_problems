# Attempt 003: The Skin Microbiome — From Dysbiosis to Decolonization to Recolonization

## The Insight

Eczema isn't just an immune disease — it's an ECOLOGICAL disease. The skin microbiome in eczema is a collapsed ecosystem dominated by one pathogenic species (S. aureus), analogous to an algal bloom in a dead lake. Treatment requires not just killing the pathogen but restoring the ecosystem.

## The Ecology of Eczema Skin

### Healthy skin ecosystem
```
DIVERSE COMMUNITY:
  S. epidermidis (dominant commensal, 30-40% of skin bacteria)
  ├── Produces antimicrobial peptides (lantibiotics) that kill S. aureus
  ├── Stimulates keratinocyte AMPs (cathelicidin, β-defensins)
  └── Activates Treg responses via TLR2 signaling
  
  Cutibacterium acnes (sebaceous sites)
  Corynebacterium spp. (moist sites)
  Micrococcus spp.
  Fungal: Malassezia (commensal, lipophilic)
  
  → Competitive exclusion: diverse community prevents any one species from dominating
  → Colonization resistance: S. aureus present at <5% relative abundance
```

### Eczema skin ecosystem
```
COLLAPSED COMMUNITY:
  S. aureus (dominant, 50-90% of bacteria in flaring skin)
  ├── Produces superantigens (SEA, SEB, TSST-1) → polyclonal Th2 activation
  ├── Produces proteases → further barrier damage
  ├── Produces δ-toxin → mast cell degranulation → itch + inflammation
  └── Forms biofilms → resistant to antibiotics and immune clearance
  
  S. epidermidis (reduced)
  Other commensals (depleted)
  Diversity index: LOW (Shannon <2 vs >3 in healthy)
  
  → Positive feedback: S. aureus inflammation → barrier damage → more S. aureus → more inflammation
  → Antibiotic treatment: temporarily reduces S. aureus but doesn't restore diversity → recolonization
```

## Why Antibiotics Fail Long-Term

```
BEFORE ANTIBIOTICS:
  S. aureus dominant, commensals depleted → eczema flare

DURING ANTIBIOTICS (oral or topical mupirocin):
  S. aureus killed → eczema improves temporarily
  BUT: commensals also killed → ecological vacuum

AFTER ANTIBIOTICS:
  Antibiotics stop → regrowth
  S. aureus regrows FASTER than commensals (biofilm advantage, skin niche adaptation)
  → S. aureus dominant again within 1-4 weeks
  → Eczema flare returns
  
  Each antibiotic cycle may WORSEN the long-term ecology (resistance selection)
```

## The Three-Phase Ecological Restoration

### Phase 1: Decolonization (reduce S. aureus)

| Method | Mechanism | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Dilute bleach baths** (0.005% NaOCl, 2x/week) | Directly kills S. aureus; minimal effect on commensals at this concentration | Multiple RCTs show eczema improvement (Huang 2009, JAAD; Chopra 2017) |
| **Vitamin D → cathelicidin** | Endogenous antimicrobial peptide; kills S. aureus selectively | Observational (vitamin D deficiency → low cathelicidin → high S. aureus) |
| **Nasal mupirocin** (2x/day × 5 days) | Nasal S. aureus carriage reseeds skin; decolonize the reservoir | Standard decolonization protocol |
| **Topical antiseptic** (chlorhexidine or triclosan wash) | Broad-spectrum surface decolonization | Moderate evidence |

### Phase 2: Recolonization (restore commensals)

| Method | Mechanism | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Topical S. epidermidis transplant** | Apply commensal bacteria directly to skin; competitive exclusion of S. aureus | Nakatsuji 2017 (Nature Medicine): autologous S. epidermidis transplant reduced S. aureus and improved eczema |
| **Roseomonas mucosa (bacteriotherapy)** | Gram-negative commensal with anti-S. aureus activity | Myles 2018 (JCI Insight): Phase I/II in pediatric eczema showed improvement |
| **Probiotic emollients** | Moisturizer containing live commensal bacteria | Early development; conceptually sound |
| **Reduce antimicrobial product use** | Harsh soaps, hand sanitizers, antibacterial products → destroy commensal diversity | Lifestyle modification |

### Phase 3: Ecosystem maintenance

| Method | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Ceramide emollients** | Maintain barrier → intact barrier supports commensal colonization |
| **Prebiotics on skin** (galacto-oligosaccharides in emollient) | Feed commensal bacteria on skin surface |
| **Gut microbiome restoration** (oral butyrate, prebiotics, probiotics) | Gut-skin axis: gut commensals modulate systemic immune tone → healthier skin immune environment |
| **Avoid unnecessary antibiotics** | Protect restored ecosystem |

## The Nakatsuji Discovery (2017)

This is the eczema equivalent of a landmark finding:

- Eczema patients' own S. epidermidis and S. hominis strains produce antimicrobial peptides
- These AMPs kill S. aureus (including MRSA) but NOT other commensals
- When autologous commensal strains were applied topically to eczema skin:
  - S. aureus colonization DECREASED
  - Eczema severity IMPROVED
  - No adverse effects

**This is bacteriotherapy — restoring the ecosystem rather than killing the pathogen.**

## Pediatric Application

For young children with eczema:
- **Safe interventions**: dilute bleach baths (well-studied in pediatric eczema), ceramide emollients, vitamin D 1,000 IU, age-appropriate probiotics (L. rhamnosus GG), reduce harsh soaps
- **Ecosystem-aware**: minimize antibiotic use (preserve commensal diversity), allow natural microbial exposure (outdoor play, pet exposure if not allergic)
- **The Amish principle**: diverse microbial exposure in early life trains the immune system toward tolerance
