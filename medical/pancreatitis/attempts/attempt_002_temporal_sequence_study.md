# Attempt 002: The Temporal Sequence — Pancreatitis as T1DM Precursor

## The Hypothesis
Acute CVB pancreatitis is not just a parallel disease — it's the PRECURSOR EVENT that seeds islet infection leading to T1DM years later.

```
Timeline:
  Year 0:   CVB pancreatitis (exocrine cells destroyed → clinical symptoms)
            SIMULTANEOUSLY: islet cells infected (no symptoms — endocrine reserve >90%)
  Year 0-X: TD mutants establish in islets
            Slow autoimmune cascade (molecular mimicry, Treg failure)
            Beta cell mass: 100% → gradually declining
  Year X:   Beta cell mass < 20% → clinical T1DM diagnosis
            Nobody connects it to the pancreatitis episode years ago
```

## Evidence For

### 1. CVB serotypes overlap
- Pancreatitis: CVB1, B4 (primary)
- T1DM: CVB1, B4 (primary)
- Same serotypes → same viral population infects both exocrine and endocrine pancreas simultaneously

### 2. CAR receptor on both cell types
- Acinar cells express CAR → CVB entry (exocrine destruction → pancreatitis)
- Beta cells express CAR → CVB entry (endocrine infection → T1DM cascade)
- Single viremia event infects both compartments

### 3. Anatomical proximity
- Islets are EMBEDDED in exocrine tissue
- Inflammatory response to acinar cell lysis exposes islets to:
  - IFN-γ (activates autoreactive T cells)
  - TNF-α (direct beta cell damage)
  - Digestive enzyme leak (trypsin activation → islet damage)
- The pancreatitis inflammatory environment PRIMES the autoimmune response against islets

### 4. Case reports
- Co-occurrence of CVB pancreatitis + T1DM onset documented (rare but reported)
- TEDDY/DAISY studies: enteroviral infections in first 2 years of life correlate with islet autoantibody appearance
- Temporal sequence: viral infection → autoantibodies → clinical T1DM (years later)

## Evidence Against

### 1. Most pancreatitis patients don't develop T1DM
- If pancreatitis always seeded T1DM, we'd see much higher T1DM rates post-pancreatitis
- Counter: only genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DR3/DR4) progress to T1DM
- The pancreatitis provides the trigger; the genetics determine whether the trigger fires

### 2. Many T1DM patients have no history of pancreatitis
- Counter: the islet seeding may occur during SUBCLINICAL pancreatitis
- Or during viremia without overt pancreatitis (direct islet infection)

## The Study

### Design: Prospective cohort
- Enroll patients hospitalized with acute pancreatitis (all causes)
- Test for CVB at admission (stool RT-PCR, serum IgM)
- Stratify: CVB-positive pancreatitis vs CVB-negative pancreatitis
- HLA genotyping (DR3, DR4, DQ2, DQ8)

### Follow-up (5 years)
| Timepoint | Tests |
|-----------|-------|
| 0 (admission) | CVB panel, HLA genotype, islet autoantibodies (GAD65, IA-2, ZnT8, IAA), fasting glucose, HbA1c |
| 3 months | Islet autoantibodies, fasting glucose |
| 6 months | Islet autoantibodies, fasting glucose |
| 12 months | Islet autoantibodies, HbA1c, OGTT |
| 24 months | Islet autoantibodies, HbA1c, OGTT |
| 60 months | Islet autoantibodies, HbA1c, OGTT |

### Primary endpoint
- Islet autoantibody seroconversion rate at 5 years: CVB-positive vs CVB-negative pancreatitis

### Expected result (if hypothesis true)
- CVB-positive pancreatitis: 5-15% seroconversion (especially in HLA-DR3/DR4 carriers)
- CVB-negative pancreatitis: <1% seroconversion (background rate)
- CVB-positive + HLA-DR3/DR4: 15-30% seroconversion

### The intervention extension
If observational arm confirms increased seroconversion:
- Randomize CVB-positive pancreatitis patients to fluoxetine × 3 months vs placebo
- Primary endpoint: autoantibody seroconversion at 2 years
- **This becomes a T1DM PREVENTION trial in a high-risk population**
