# Viral Pancreatitis (Coxsackievirus B)

## The Problem
Acute inflammation of the pancreas caused by CVB infection (primarily B1, B4). Can range from mild edematous pancreatitis to severe necrotizing pancreatitis. Distinct from T1DM autoimmune destruction — this is direct viral cytolysis of exocrine AND endocrine pancreas. Can co-occur with myocarditis.

## Status: UNSOLVED (as viral etiology — general pancreatitis treatment exists)

## Key Biology

| Component | What | Role |
|-----------|------|------|
| Exocrine pancreas | Acinar cells, ductal cells | Digestive enzyme production (amylase, lipase) |
| Endocrine pancreas | Islets of Langerhans | Insulin (beta), glucagon (alpha) production |
| CVB1/B4 | Primary causative serotypes | Tropism for both exocrine and endocrine pancreas |
| CAR receptor | Coxsackievirus entry receptor | Expressed on pancreatic acinar and islet cells |
| Trypsinogen activation | Premature enzyme activation | Autodigestion of pancreas during acute phase |

## The Cascade
1. CVB reaches pancreas during viremia
2. Enters via CAR receptor on acinar/islet cells
3. Viral replication → direct cytolysis
4. Damaged acinar cells release trypsinogen → premature activation → autodigestion
5. Inflammation cascade → edema → necrosis (if severe)
6. Resolution OR persistent infection
7. Persistent CVB in islets → T1DM pathway (separate problem)

## Connection to T1DM
- Acute pancreatitis may be the INITIAL CVB event that seeds persistent islet infection
- CVB pancreatitis + T1DM onset can co-occur (documented in case reports)
- Pancreatitis damages exocrine function; T1DM is the endocrine sequel
- Same virus, same organ, different cell types → different diseases
