# Pancreatitis — Anti-Problem

## The Question
"Why do most CVB infections NOT cause pancreatitis? And why do most pancreatitis episodes NOT lead to T1DM?"

## Part 1: Why Most CVB Infections Spare the Pancreas

CVB is ubiquitous — 60-80% of adults are seropositive. But acute CVB pancreatitis is rare. Why?

### The Kupffer Cell Gate
The hepatitis/models/portal_gatekeeper.md explains this: Kupffer cells clear 95-99% of portal CVB. Only a small fraction reaches systemic circulation. Even less reaches the pancreas via the splenic and superior mesenteric arteries.

**Most infections never deliver enough virus to the pancreas to establish infection.** The ones that do had either:
- Very high oral viral load (large inoculum)
- Impaired Kupffer cell function (liver disease, alcohol, NAFLD)
- Simultaneous hepatic infection (amplification scenario)

### CAR Receptor Density
Not all acinar cells express CAR equally. Individual variation in CAR expression determines susceptibility. High CAR expressors → more susceptible to pancreatic infection.

## Part 2: Why Most Pancreatitis Doesn't Lead to T1DM

If CVB infects both acinar cells (pancreatitis) and islet cells (T1DM precursor) simultaneously:

### The 95% who don't get T1DM after pancreatitis
1. **Islet infection cleared** — immune response that resolves pancreatitis also clears islet infection
2. **No TD mutant establishment** — virus eliminated before 5' deletion occurs
3. **No autoimmune trigger** — resolution is fast enough that molecular mimicry doesn't develop
4. **Genetic resistance** — patient lacks HLA-DR3/DR4 susceptibility alleles

### The ~5% who might
1. **TD mutants establish in islets** during the immunosuppressed recovery phase
2. **HLA-DR3/DR4 carriers** → molecular mimicry triggers autoimmune response
3. **Weak NK cell response** → incomplete clearance
4. **Low selenium** → virus mutates toward increased virulence (Keshan/Beck mechanism)

## The Anti-Problem Insight

The transition from pancreatitis to T1DM is a **double gate**:
```
Gate 1: Does CVB persist as TD mutants after pancreatitis resolves?
  YES (30-40%) → Gate 2
  NO (60-70%) → safe, no T1DM risk

Gate 2: Does the patient have autoimmune susceptibility?
  YES (HLA-DR3/DR4, ~5-10% of population) → T1DM risk
  NO → persistent infection but no autoimmunity → subclinical

Combined: ~30% × ~8% = ~2.4% of CVB pancreatitis → T1DM
This is low enough to escape epidemiological detection
But high enough to matter in genetically susceptible individuals
```

**Antiviral treatment during acute pancreatitis would close Gate 1.** If no TD mutants establish, Gate 2 is irrelevant. HLA genotype becomes a non-issue.
