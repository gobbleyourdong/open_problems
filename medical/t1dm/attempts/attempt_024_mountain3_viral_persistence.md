# Attempt 024: Mountain 3 Deep Dive — Persistent Viral Infection as Continuous Trigger

## The Understudied Problem

Most T1DM research treats the viral trigger as a one-time event: virus infects, breaks tolerance, autoimmunity starts, virus clears, damage continues on its own. But what if the virus DOESN'T clear?

## Coxsackievirus B Persistence

CVB can establish **persistent, low-grade infection** in the pancreas:
- Detected in pancreatic tissue of T1DM patients years after diagnosis (DiPiS study, nPOD autopsy data)
- Found in ductal cells AND remaining beta cells
- Does not cause acute cytopathic effect — instead establishes a smoldering, non-lytic infection
- Produces viral RNA and protein at low levels continuously
- Continuously activates innate immune sensors (TLR3, RIG-I, MDA5) → chronic IFN-α production → chronic inflammation

## Why This Changes Everything

If CVB persists in the pancreas:

1. **The autoimmune attack is not self-sustaining — it's virus-sustained.** Remove the virus and the inflammation may resolve on its own. The immune system isn't "broken" — it's doing its job (fighting a persistent infection). The collateral damage to beta cells is the problem.

2. **Every regeneration attempt fails because new beta cells get infected.** If CVB lives in the pancreatic environment, any new beta cell (from Ngn3+ progenitors, transplanted, or transdifferentiated) that arrives in the pancreas gets infected → presents viral antigens → immune system kills it. The virus is the reason the immune system won't stop.

3. **Mountains 1 and 2 have a hidden assumption**: that the autoimmune process is self-sustaining and could be stopped if we just reset the immune system hard enough. If a persistent virus is driving it, no amount of Treg expansion or FMD cycling will work unless the virus is cleared first.

4. **The intramuscular transplant site (attempt 022) makes more sense now.** Muscle is NOT infected with CVB. Cells transplanted into muscle are AWAY from the persistent viral reservoir. This may be why Sana's intramuscular approach worked — the cells were placed where the virus isn't.

## The Antiviral Angle

What if you treated T1DM as a persistent viral infection?

### Antiviral Candidates
- **Pleconaril**: Enterovirus capsid inhibitor. Tested in clinical trials for other enterovirus infections. Blocks viral entry.
- **Pocapavir**: Another capsid inhibitor. Phase 2 for neonatal enterovirus.
- **Fluoxetine**: The SSRI antidepressant. Unexpectedly potent against CVB in vitro. Blocks viral replication via sigma-1 receptor. Already FDA-approved, safe, cheap (~$10/mo generic).
- **Ribavirin**: Broad-spectrum antiviral. Active against enteroviruses in vitro. Teratogenic — limited use.
- **IFN-λ (lambda interferon)**: The pancreas-specific antiviral. Pancreatic cells express IFN-λ receptors preferentially. Could clear pancreatic CVB without systemic immune activation.

### The DiViD Trial (Norway)
- The Diabetes Virus Detection study collected pancreatic biopsies from newly diagnosed T1DM patients
- Found enteroviral protein (VP1) in islets of 6/6 T1DM patients vs 0/6 controls
- This is the strongest direct evidence for persistent CVB in T1DM islets
- The group proposed an antiviral trial but it has not yet been completed

## The Mountain 3 Protocol Addition

Add to Stage 1 (Clean the Terrain) — BEFORE regeneration attempts:

**Antiviral course:**
- Fluoxetine 20mg daily × 3 months (anti-CVB + antidepressant benefit + safe)
- OR pleconaril (if accessible via clinical trial)
- Goal: clear persistent CVB from the pancreatic environment
- Monitor: enteroviral VP1 in stool/blood (proxy for clearance), IFN-α levels (should drop as viral load drops)

**Then proceed with Stage 2 (FMD + regeneration).** The regenerated beta cells arrive in a virus-free environment. The immune system has nothing left to fight. The inflammation resolves.

## Convergence: Why Deng's Autologous Cells Survived

Attempt 008 mystery: why didn't autoimmunity recur against Deng's autologous CiPSC islets?

New hypothesis: **the transplant site (rectus sheath) has no CVB.** The virus persists in the PANCREAS. Cells placed elsewhere are in a virus-free environment. The immune system has nothing to react to at the new site because the TRIGGER (virus) isn't there.

This unifies:
- Deng (rectus sheath — no virus) → cells survive
- Sana (intramuscular — no virus) → cells survive
- Edmonton (portal vein → liver — no virus) → cells survive initially but eventually fail (immune memory, not viral re-infection)
- Native pancreas (CVB persists) → beta cells die

**The autoimmune attack may be SITE-SPECIFIC, not SYSTEMIC.** The immune system attacks beta cells in the pancreas because that's where the virus is. Beta cells elsewhere are tolerated.

## The Gap (Mountain 3 Final)

Two gaps remain on Mountain 3:
1. **Confirm CVB persistence**: need pancreatic biopsy data from more T1DM patients with sensitive PCR/FISH for enteroviral RNA. nPOD is collecting this.
2. **Test the antiviral hypothesis**: does clearing CVB reduce autoimmune inflammation? Fluoxetine trial in newly diagnosed T1DM measuring IFN-α, autoantibody titers, C-peptide.

Both are clinical trials. Both could start with existing FDA-approved drugs. Both cost <$1M to run as pilot studies.
