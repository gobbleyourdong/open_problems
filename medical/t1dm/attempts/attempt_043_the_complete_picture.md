## The D-Lactate Connection

This is Mountain 6 — or really, a branch of Mountain 3 (environment) that connects to Mountain 2 (regeneration) through metabolism.

### Normal Lactate Metabolism (Cori Cycle)
```
MUSCLE → L-lactate → BLOOD → LIVER → glucose → BLOOD → MUSCLE
(anaerobic glycolysis)    (gluconeogenesis)   (aerobic glycolysis)
```
L-lactate is the normal substrate. Human L-LDH (L-lactate dehydrogenase) converts it efficiently. The cycle is clean — lactate in, glucose out, energy balanced.

### D-Lactate Disruption
```
GUT BACTERIA → D-lactate → BLOOD → LIVER → ???
                                    ↓
                           L-LDH can't process D-lactate efficiently
                           D-lactate ACCUMULATES
                           D-lactate TRIGGERS gluconeogenesis signals
                           but ISN'T consumed by the process
                           ↓
                           NET RESULT: excess glucose production
                           glucose the body didn't ask for
                           glucose the beta cells must handle
```

### Which Bacteria Produce D-Lactate?
- **Lactobacillus acidophilus** (in yogurt, probiotics — paradoxically)
- **Lactobacillus delbrueckii**
- **Streptococcus thermophilus**
- **Enterococcus faecalis**
- **Certain Bifidobacterium species** (but most produce L-lactate)
- **Klebsiella** (pathogenic, in dysbiotic guts)

These thrive on fermentable carbohydrates (sugars, starches, fiber). On keto: starved. On carbs: fed.

### D-Lactate in T1DM — The Hidden Multiplier

The standard T1DM narrative: "beta cells are destroyed, can't make enough insulin for dietary glucose." But what if a significant fraction of the glucose isn't FROM THE DIET? What if the liver is producing EXTRA glucose because D-lactate is triggering gluconeogenesis inappropriately?

This would mean:
1. Even on a "controlled" carb diet, glucose is higher than expected
2. Beta cells work harder than the diet demands
3. The ER stress from overwork produces more neoantigens
4. The autoimmune attack is amplified by metabolic stress
5. Insulin doses are higher than diet alone would require

**For the operator:** 2u/meal on 15-20g carbs. Is that more insulin than 15-20g carbs should need? If a type 2 diabetic on the same carb load needed less insulin, the excess demand might be D-lactate-driven hepatic glucose output.

### Testing for D-Lactate
- **Serum D-lactate level** (requires specific assay — most standard lactate tests measure only L-lactate)
- **Urine D-lactate**
- **Stool D-lactate** (direct measure of gut production)
- **Gut microbiome 16S sequencing** (quantify D-lactate-producing species)

### Treating D-Lactate Overproduction
1. **Diet**: Reduce fermentable carbohydrates (FODMAPs) — starve D-lactate producers
2. **Probiotics**: REPLACE D-lactate producers with L-lactate-only strains (specific Lactobacillus rhamnosus, L. plantarum strains)
3. **Prebiotics**: Feed butyrate producers (resistant starch → Faecalibacterium, Roseburia) to outcompete D-lactate producers
4. **Antibiotics** (targeted): Rifaximin (gut-specific, minimal systemic absorption) to reduce overgrown D-lactate producers
5. **D-LDH supplementation?**: Theoretical — provide exogenous D-lactate dehydrogenase to clear D-lactate. Not available clinically.

## 2026-04-18 audit note (R18 + R19 + R20 from AUDIT_LOG fire 8)

**R18 — D-LDH / D-lactate mechanism wrong.** Humans **do** have mitochondrial D-lactate dehydrogenase (LDHD gene; Flick & Konieczny 2002 *J Biol Chem*; de Bari 2002) — the premise "no human D-lactate metabolism" is false. Additionally, "D-lactate triggers gluconeogenesis without being consumed" is self-defeating (gluconeogenesis is a consumption pathway). The published D-lactic-acidosis literature (Petersen 2005 *NEJM*) reports symptomatic D-lactate at >3 mmol/L, **three orders of magnitude above** what ordinary gut dysbiosis produces. No published human data supports subclinical D-lactate as a T1DM driver. The mechanism should be retracted or downgraded to explicit speculation.

**R19 — CVB → thyroid → Hashimoto's overstatement.** "Three diagnoses, one virus" overstates a hypothesis. CAR (coxsackievirus-adenovirus receptor) expression in thyroid is documented (Fechner 1999 *Cardiovasc Res*) and thyroiditis cases following enteroviral infection exist, but a **causal CVB → Hashimoto's link in humans is not established**. APS-II/III (autoimmune polyendocrine syndromes) with shared HLA-DR3-DQ2 risk is at least as well-supported as a non-viral explanation for T1DM + Hashimoto's co-occurrence.

**R20 — L. plantarum DL-lactate.** *Lactobacillus plantarum* produces **both D- and L-lactate** (heterofermentative-capable; Pessione 2012; Hofvendahl 2000). The Stage-1b probiotic substitution described here as "L-lactate-only strains: L. rhamnosus GG + L. plantarum 299v" is **mis-specified for L. plantarum** (L. rhamnosus GG is correctly L-lactate-only; L. plantarum 299v produces DL). If the clinical goal is pure-L production, L. plantarum should be replaced with a strain like L. rhamnosus GG or L. acidophilus.

**Fix applied:** audit note only (Maps Include Noise v6). R18 is the most load-bearing correction (whole sub-hypothesis collapses); R19 requires downgrading from "confirmed cross-disease" to "hypothesis needing dedicated cohort"; R20 requires a concrete strain substitution if the DL issue is kept load-bearing.
