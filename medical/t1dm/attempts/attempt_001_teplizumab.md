# Attempt 001: Teplizumab (Anti-CD3 Monoclonal Antibody)

## Approach
Deplete or modulate autoreactive T cells by targeting CD3 (the signaling component of the T cell receptor complex). Non-Fc binding humanized anti-CD3 mAb.

## Key Trial
- **TrialNet TN-10**: 76 at-risk relatives with ≥2 autoantibodies
- 14-day IV course of teplizumab vs placebo
- Primary endpoint: time to clinical T1DM diagnosis

## Results
- Median delay: **~2 years** (72.2 months teplizumab vs 24.4 months placebo)
- HR 0.41 (p=0.006)
- FDA approved November 2022 as **Tzield** for Stage 2 T1DM (≥8 years old)
- First disease-modifying therapy for T1DM

## Why It Fails as a Cure
1. **Delays but does not prevent.** Most patients still progress to clinical T1DM.
2. **Non-specific mechanism.** Anti-CD3 modulates ALL T cells, not just autoreactive ones. Causes transient lymphopenia, cytokine release syndrome.
3. **Temporary effect.** The autoimmune process resumes after the drug clears. Would need repeated courses.
4. **No tolerance induction.** Does not teach the immune system to tolerate beta cell antigens. Just hits pause on the attack.
5. **Only Stage 2.** No benefit shown after clinical diagnosis (Stage 3). The window is narrow.

## What It Teaches Us
- The autoimmune attack CAN be slowed by modulating T cells
- Even a blunt instrument (anti-CD3) buys time → a precise instrument (antigen-specific) could buy permanent tolerance
- The 2-year delay suggests the immune system has a "momentum" that can be disrupted
- C-peptide preservation correlates with the delay → residual beta cells survive when the attack is dampened

## The Gap It Reveals
Teplizumab is a sledgehammer. It modulates all T cells. The gap: **target only the autoreactive T cells** (the ones recognizing GAD65, insulin, IGRP, etc.) while leaving the rest of the immune system intact. This is antigen-specific tolerance.

## Status: PARTIAL SUCCESS — delays onset, does not cure
