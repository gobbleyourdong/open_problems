## The Reframing

24 attempts in, Mountain 3's viral persistence finding (attempt 024) forces a complete reframe of Mountain 1.

The entire history of cell replacement for T1DM assumed the problem was:
**"How do we protect transplanted cells from the immune system?"**

The actual problem may be:
**"How do we put cells somewhere the virus isn't?"**

## Why Edmonton Eventually Fails (Reinterpreted)

The Edmonton protocol puts islets in the liver. The liver has no CVB. The cells survive initially WITHOUT the viral trigger driving immune attack. But they fail over 5 years. Why?

Previous explanation: "autoimmune memory persists, eventually finds and kills the cells."

New explanation: **the liver's immune environment (Kupffer cells, NKT cells) is not tolerogenic enough to fully protect allogeneic cells long-term without immunosuppression. The immune system isn't attacking because of autoimmune memory — it's attacking because of ALLOREJECTION (foreign donor cells).**

If you used AUTOLOGOUS cells (Deng-type) in the liver, the allorejection disappears. If you used HYPOIMMUNE cells (Sana-type) in the liver, the allorejection disappears. Edmonton failed because it was foreign cells + hostile site, not because of autoimmunity.

## Mountain 1's New Thesis

Mountain 1 was: "engineer cells that survive the immune system."
Mountain 1 is now: **"put cells where the problem isn't."**

This is radically simpler. You don't need:
- CRISPR (if using autologous cells + virus-free site)
- Immunosuppression (if the site isn't triggering autoimmunity)
- Encapsulation (if there's no attack to encapsulate against)
- HLA-G engineering (if the immune system isn't looking for these cells)

You might just need: **autologous beta cells + intramuscular injection + antiviral clearance of pancreatic CVB.**
