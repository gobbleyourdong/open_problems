# Attempt 008: Autologous iPSC Islets (Deng, Peking University)

## Approach
Take the patient's own cells, convert to chemically induced pluripotent stem cells (CiPSCs) using ONLY small molecules (no viral vectors, no transcription factors), differentiate into islet clusters, transplant under the abdominal rectus sheath.

## Key Results (Cell, 2024)
- **First patient**: 25 years of T1DM
- CiPSCs from patient's own cells → islet clusters
- Transplanted under anterior abdominal rectus sheath (not portal vein, not muscle)
- **Insulin-independent by 10 weeks**
- At 1 year: still producing insulin, time-in-range >98%, HbA1c ~5%
- **No immunosuppression** (autologous = self, so no rejection)
- Two additional patients reportedly showing similar results

## Why It's Not Yet a Cure for Everyone
1. **The autoimmune question.** These are the patient's OWN cells — genetically identical to the beta cells the immune system already destroyed. Why doesn't the autoimmune attack destroy them too? Possible explanations: (a) the transplant site (rectus sheath) is immunologically privileged, (b) the cells are subtly different (CiPSC-derived vs native), (c) the autoimmune response has "burned out" after 25 years. We don't know which. If (c), this only works in long-duration T1DM.
2. **Follow-up is short.** 1 year is remarkable but not definitive. The autoimmune attack may resume.
3. **Manufacturing.** Autologous = one manufacturing run per patient. CiPSC generation + differentiation + QC = months of work per patient. Not scalable to millions.
4. **CiPSC technology is early.** Chemical reprogramming is less efficient and less characterized than Yamanaka factor iPSCs. Reproducibility across patients uncertain.
5. **Transplant site novelty.** Rectus sheath is not standard. Long-term cell survival and function at this site is unknown beyond 1 year.

## What It Teaches Us
- **Autologous cells may bypass both rejection AND autoimmunity.** This is unexpected. If confirmed, the autoimmune problem may be more tractable than we thought — at least in long-duration T1DM where the autoimmune response may have burned out.
- **The transplant site matters.** Rectus sheath may provide an immunologically favorable niche that the portal vein (Edmonton) does not.
- **Chemical reprogramming avoids genetic modification concerns.** No CRISPR, no viral vectors, no oncogene expression. Cleaner safety profile.
- **If autoimmunity truly doesn't recur with autologous cells, the gap is CLOSED for long-duration patients.** The remaining challenge is manufacturing scale and cost.

## The Gap It Reveals
The mystery: WHY don't these autologous cells get destroyed? If we understood the mechanism of protection, we could potentially reproduce it with allogeneic (universal donor) cells — solving the manufacturing problem.

Three hypotheses:
1. **Site privilege**: the rectus sheath is immunologically quiet
2. **Autoimmune burnout**: after 25yr, the autoreactive T cells have exhausted
3. **CiPSC difference**: chemically reprogrammed cells present different antigens

Each hypothesis, if true, opens a different path to a universal cure.
