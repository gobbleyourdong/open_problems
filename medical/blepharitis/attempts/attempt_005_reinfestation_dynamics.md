# attempt_005 — Reinfestation Dynamics and the Treatment-Failure Driver No One Addresses

> Scope: most "treatment-failure" Demodex cases are not biological failures
> of the acaricide — they are reinfestation from reservoirs the protocol
> does not touch. This attempt catalogues the reservoirs, their transfer
> mechanics, and the practical household protocol to close them.
>
> Depends on: attempt_001 (mite biology), attempt_002 (treatment),
> attempt_003 (testing), attempt_004 (alt acaricides).

---

## The missing piece in every protocol

TTO + Xdemvy + ivermectin protocols report 60–90% response rates. The
missing 10–40% is typically attributed to:

- "Patient non-adherence"
- "Refractory disease"
- "Concurrent rosacea"
- "Need longer treatment"

Rarely addressed: **the patient is being reinfested.** Demodex transfers
by intimate skin-to-skin contact and on objects that hold mite debris
(pillowcases, towels, shared cosmetics). If the partner, bedding, or
personal-care items are contaminated, the treatment cycle becomes
Sisyphean.

This attempt is the operational piece: what reservoirs exist, how long
mites survive off-host, what actually decontaminates, and who else in the
household needs to be treated.

## Mite biology constraining transmission

From attempt_001 + additional data:

- **D. folliculorum lifecycle:** 14–18 days (egg → larva → protonymph →
  deutonymph → adult)
- **D. brevis lifecycle:** 16–20 days, slightly longer
- **Off-host survival:** 4–10 days at room humidity; longer (up to 3
  weeks) in high-humidity or oily environments (Nutting 1976; more
  recent texts cite Forton 2020)
- **Lethal temperature:** >58 °C kills within seconds; above 54 °C within
  minutes. Freezer (−20 °C) kills within hours.
- **Nocturnal surface behavior:** adults emerge from follicles at night,
  crawl on skin surface, mate, return. This is the window for transfer
  to bedding and to another sleeper.

**Practical implication:** if a household member with demodicosis and
their untreated partner share a bed, mites transfer nightly. A 6-week
acaricidal regimen on one partner is compromised unless the reservoir
(other partner + shared bedding) is also addressed.

## Reservoir catalogue

### 1. Household partners and close family

**Transmission mechanism:** direct skin-to-skin contact, especially
face-to-face (kissing, shared pillow) or face-on-shoulder contact.
Infants acquire Demodex gradually through face-to-mother contact during
infancy (most adults are Demodex-positive; most infants are not).

**Practical rule:**
- Adult partners of demodicosis patients should be screened at minimum
  for collarettes; empiric co-treatment is reasonable if both share a
  bed nightly and one has confirmed disease
- Children >5 years old in the household: examine for facial
  demodicosis + eyelid symptoms before treating; most children under 10
  are still Demodex-low
- Elderly parent living in the household: very likely Demodex-positive,
  often asymptomatic carrier; significant reservoir if bed-sharing or
  frequent face-touching

**Co-treatment threshold:** shared bed + shared pillows + one confirmed
case = co-treat both partners with at least TTO lid wipes nightly for 6
weeks to prevent ping-pong reinfestation.

### 2. Pillowcases and bedding

**The dominant physical reservoir.** Adult mites crawl onto pillowcases
nightly. Live mites survive up to 10 days on fabric; mite eggs survive
longer (dormant until substrate).

**Decontamination:**
- **Wash pillowcases every 2 days at ≥60 °C with detergent** during
  active treatment — this is the single highest-yield household
  intervention
- Alternative: tumble-dry on high heat 30+ min (kills mites by
  dehydration + temperature)
- Replace pillowcases at 6-week mark (end of treatment); discard old ones
- **Replace pillows entirely if >2 years old** — pillow interior is a
  long-term reservoir difficult to wash; cost of a new pillow ($15–30) is
  trivial relative to reinfestation risk
- Sheets: wash weekly at ≥60 °C
- Duvet/comforter: wash every 2 weeks minimum during treatment; tumble
  dry high heat
- Mattress: vacuum weekly; consider a washable mattress protector on
  high-heat wash cycle

**Common error:** washing on "cold" or "warm" (<50 °C) does not kill
mites. Many energy-saving washers default to 30 °C. Use the sanitize or
hot cycle during Demodex treatment.

### 3. Towels (face / bath)

**Second most important textile reservoir.** Face towels touch the lid
margin daily.

**Protocol:**
- One face towel per person (no sharing)
- Replace the face towel daily during treatment; hot-wash in batches
- Bath towels weekly at high heat
- Hand towels shared? Ideally not — but if unavoidable, wash daily

### 4. Personal care items touching the face

- **Makeup brushes:** replace or deep-clean weekly during treatment;
  discard old eye-makeup products (mascara, eyeliner, eyeshadow)
  entirely and start fresh — cosmetics are significant Demodex reservoirs
- **Mascara wands and old mascara tubes:** contaminated after 1 month of
  use (industry standard); in Demodex patients, after 2 weeks
- **Eyelash curlers:** clean daily with alcohol during treatment; consider
  replacing
- **Face brushes, silicone cleansing devices (Clarisonic-type):** clean
  brush heads with soap + hot water weekly; replace every 3 months
- **Sleep masks and CPAP masks:** wash with soap + hot water daily
  during treatment; replace cloth components if non-washable
- **Glasses and glass cases:** wipe frames with 70% isopropyl daily
- **Pillowcase-contact items:** phone (touches pillow at night, then
  face during day) — disinfect daily
- **Hair:** long hair that touches the face carries mites — tie back at
  night, wash hair 2×/week with shampoo containing TTO or selenium sulfide

### 5. Animals

**Dogs and cats have their own Demodex species** (D. canis, D. cati)
which do not infect humans in normal immunocompetent hosts. However:

- Close face-to-face contact with pets deposits dander + facial oils on
  the human that can carry other microorganisms
- In immunosuppressed humans, cross-species demodex infection has been
  reported but is rare
- The "pet face-licking" habit is not a major Demodex vector but is a
  general microbial contamination vector worth minimizing during
  treatment

### 6. Gyms, saunas, shared workplaces

- Shared towels at gyms: significant reservoir
- Shared yoga mats: possible vector via face-down poses; bring own towel
- Office / workspace face-contact surfaces (phone receivers, VR
  headsets, shared makeup counters): clean or avoid during treatment
- Professional facial / lash extension services: ensure single-use
  applicators and clean work environment; some services with poor
  hygiene are documented reinfestation sources

## Household decontamination checklist (printable)

**Daily during 6-week treatment:**
- [ ] Wash face towel at ≥60 °C
- [ ] Wipe phone and glasses with alcohol
- [ ] Apply nightly acaricide (TTO wipes or Xdemvy)

**Every 2 days:**
- [ ] Wash pillowcase at ≥60 °C + hot tumble dry

**Weekly:**
- [ ] Wash sheets at ≥60 °C
- [ ] Wash makeup brushes with soap + hot water
- [ ] Clean silicone cleansing brush heads
- [ ] Clean sleep/CPAP mask
- [ ] Hot wash gym bag + contained items

**Every 2 weeks:**
- [ ] Wash duvet/comforter at ≥60 °C

**At treatment start (week 0):**
- [ ] Discard all eye makeup (mascara, liner, shadow, pencils)
- [ ] Replace pillow if >2 years old
- [ ] Replace mascara wand and eyeliner brush
- [ ] Hot-wash all towels in the home, mark face towels separately
- [ ] Identify household partners with symptoms → get screened

**At treatment end (week 6):**
- [ ] Discard pillowcases used during treatment; new pair for maintenance
- [ ] Replace face towel
- [ ] Re-check collarettes on slit-lamp

## Co-treatment protocol for household partners

**If partner has ANY of:**
- Facial redness / rosacea
- Eyelid irritation / itching
- Recurring stye or chalazion
- Known facial demodicosis

→ **Co-treat with nightly TTO wipes × 6 weeks minimum**, even if less
symptomatic than the index case.

**If partner is asymptomatic but shares bed/pillow with index case:**
- Simplified prophylaxis: TTO lid wipes 3×/week × 6 weeks is a
  reasonable compromise between no-treatment and full co-treatment
- Target: reduce partner-side reservoir without committing to full
  therapy for an asymptomatic person

**If partner refuses / is not accessible:**
- Separate bedding for 6 weeks if feasible
- No shared pillows
- No face-to-face sleeping
- Face wash immediately upon waking

## Cost of household decontamination

Meaningfully lower than treatment failure:

- New pillow: $15–30
- New face towels (pack of 4): $20–30
- Pillowcase set: $15–30
- Mascara + eyeliner replacement: $30–80
- Makeup brush replacement or deep clean supplies: $20
- Extra high-heat laundry cycles × 6 weeks: $20–30 incremental utility

**Total household decontamination: $120–220**, one-time, at treatment
start. Compared to Xdemvy at $1500–2000 or a failed 6-week treatment
cycle that requires restart: this is trivial and materially improves
response.

---

## What this changes

1. Every Demodex protocol should include the household checklist.
   Without it, the acaricidal treatment is only half the intervention.
2. "Treatment failure" should be differentiated from "reinfestation
   failure" before escalating therapy.
3. Partner screening is a 30-second slit-lamp exam per attempt_003;
   empiric co-treatment for bed-sharing partners is cost-effective.
4. Pillowcase hygiene is the highest-leverage household intervention.
   Wash at ≥60 °C every 2 days during active treatment.
