# attempt_003 — Diagnosis and Testing Pathway for Ocular Demodex

> Scope: what actually gets done (and who does it) to confirm Demodex on
> the eyelid. Closes the gap between "infestation is suspected" and
> "acaricidal treatment is indicated."
>
> Depends on: attempt_001 (biology), attempt_002 (treatment).

---

## The core problem: low-cost, high-yield test is under-used

The single most sensitive test for lid-margin Demodex is a **slit-lamp
exam at 25× or higher with upward eyelash rotation**, looking for
cylindrical collarettes. It takes 30 seconds, costs $0 of additional
equipment, and has ~85% sensitivity (Gao 2005). It is not routinely
performed on blepharitis patients.

This is the adoption gap. Everything below is in service of closing it.

---

## Diagnostic modalities, ranked

### 1. Cylindrical dandruff (collarettes) on slit-lamp

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Equipment | Standard slit-lamp biomicroscope |
| Time | 30–60 seconds per eye |
| Cost to clinic | $0 (incremental) |
| Sensitivity (anterior disease) | ~85% (Gao 2005) |
| Specificity | ~95% — collarettes are pathognomonic when present |
| Who performs | Any ophthalmologist, optometrist; nurse can pre-screen |
| Availability | Every eye-care clinic |

**How:** upper lid gently rotated upward, lash base examined. Collarettes
are waxy/translucent cylindrical sleeves wrapped around the base of 1–2 mm
of lash. They differ from scurf (flaky, non-cylindrical) and from
seborrheic scales (greasy, non-adherent). Absence does not rule out
D. brevis in posterior disease.

**Best single clinical sign for Demodex.** Every patient with blepharitis
symptoms should have this done.

### 2. Lash epilation + light microscopy

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Equipment | Forceps + microscope (40× to 100×) |
| Time | 5 min for 4 lashes |
| Cost to clinic | $0 (incremental) |
| Sensitivity | ~50–70% per-lash, increases with lash count |
| Specificity | ~99% — mites are directly visualized |
| Who performs | Ophthalmologist with microscope access |
| Availability | Most ophthalmology clinics |

**How:** epilate 2–4 lashes per lid (pulled slowly to bring the mite), lay
on a slide with a drop of mineral oil or immersion oil (peanut oil also
works — some studies use glycerol), cover-slip, examine at 40×. Count
mites per lash. Gao 2005 suggested ≥2 mites / 4 lashes indicates
significant infestation; ≥4 mites / 8 lashes for stronger evidence.

**Limitations:** operator-dependent; requires cooperative patient
(epilation is uncomfortable); misses D. brevis.

### 3. In-vivo confocal microscopy (IVCM)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Equipment | Confocal microscope (HRT III Rostock or equivalent) |
| Time | 10–15 min per exam |
| Cost to clinic | $60,000–150,000 equipment; $100–300 per exam |
| Sensitivity | ~90% for both species |
| Specificity | >95% |
| Who performs | Cornea specialists, academic centers |
| Availability | Tertiary / academic only; rarely available in general practice |

**How:** non-invasive imaging of living tissue at 1 µm resolution; mites
visible in follicles (D. folliculorum) and meibomian glands (D. brevis).
Kabataş 2017 used IVCM to confirm D. brevis in chalazion specimens.

**Limitations:** equipment is rare, operator-dependent, not billable in
most insurance regimes as a stand-alone. Research-grade tool, not
primary-care.

### 4. Meibography

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Equipment | Infrared imaging meibographer (LipiView, K5M, Keratograph 5M) |
| Time | 5 min |
| Cost to clinic | $15,000–40,000 equipment; $50–150 per exam |
| Sensitivity/specificity | Not for Demodex per se — for MGD severity |
| Who performs | Dry-eye clinics, comprehensive ophthalmology |
| Availability | Expanding in dry-eye clinics; still minority of practices |

**How:** infrared imaging shows meibomian gland architecture: intact
acini, partial dropout, complete dropout. Dropout is irreversible; imaging
baseline + treatment response informs urgency and dose.

**Role:** not a Demodex test but a disease-severity metric. A patient with
posterior blepharitis + heavy meibomian dropout is the one where acaricidal
treatment has the highest urgency (dropout is progressive; intervention
prevents further loss but does not restore lost glands).

### 5. Standardized Skin Surface Biopsy (SSSB) — for facial rosacea

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Equipment | Cyanoacrylate tape + microscope |
| Time | 5 min |
| Cost to clinic | $5 (tape) + microscope |
| Sensitivity | Quantitative on facial skin |
| Specificity | >95% |
| Who performs | Dermatology (rarely on lid — facial zones only) |
| Availability | Dermatology, rarely ophthalmology |

**How:** adhesive tape pressed on facial skin for 1 min, lifted, placed on
slide, examined for mites per cm². Used to confirm facial demodicosis in
rosacea and POD. Not directly applied to lid margins (tissue fragility).

**Role here:** connects the ocular surface to the facial rosacea diagnosis.
A rosacea patient with confirmed facial demodicosis on SSSB who also has
eyelid symptoms can be treated for blepharitis without separate ocular
mite testing.

### 6. Anti–B. oleronius IgG serology

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Equipment | ELISA (research lab) |
| Time | Days |
| Cost | Research assay, not commercial |
| Sensitivity/specificity | Rosacea-specific; not validated for clinical use |
| Availability | Research only |

**Role:** potential future biomarker. If clinically validated, could
stratify patients by severity and guide acaricidal-therapy duration.
Currently not orderable.

## The practical pathway

**Step 1 — Screening (any blepharitis complaint):**
- Slit-lamp exam for cylindrical collarettes (this is the 30-second step
  that gets skipped)
- If collarettes present → Demodex anterior blepharitis, go to treatment

**Step 2 — If slit-lamp equivocal or anterior-negative but symptoms persist:**
- Lash epilation microscopy on 4 lashes per lid (2 upper, 2 lower)
- Count mites; ≥4 mites / 8 lashes → treat

**Step 3 — If posterior symptoms dominate (MGD, chalazion):**
- Meibography for gland dropout baseline
- Empiric treatment (lid hygiene + acaricide) — formal D. brevis testing
  requires IVCM, which most clinics lack
- If IVCM available, confirm D. brevis before committing to a long course

**Step 4 — If concurrent facial rosacea:**
- Dermatology SSSB on affected facial zones
- Positive SSSB + eyelid symptoms = treat both simultaneously
  (Soolantra on face + TTO/Xdemvy on lids)

**Step 5 — Treatment failure after 6 weeks:**
- Re-examine for collarettes (expectation: substantial reduction)
- Re-count on epilation
- Consider partner/bedding reinfestation — have household test
- If still refractory → IVCM + oral ivermectin

## What the patient actually asks for

When a patient presents with chronic itchy red lids or a recurring
chalazion, they are typically offered:

- Artificial tears
- Warm compress
- Lid scrubs (baby shampoo generic)
- Topical antibiotic-steroid combination (e.g., TobraDex)
- Incision & curettage for recurrent chalazion

None of these are Demodex-targeted. For the patient to receive the
Demodex workup, they need either:

1. A clinician who routinely checks for collarettes (rare), or
2. Self-advocacy — asking "have you checked for Demodex mites? Can I have
   a cylindrical-collarette exam?"

This is the sharpest single expression of the adoption wall. The test is
30 seconds, is free, and is not done.

---

## Self-screening at home

A patient cannot do slit-lamp, but can observe proxies:

- **Cylindrical debris at lash base**, visible in a 10× magnifying mirror
  under bright light — appears as tiny waxy cylinders at the root of some
  lashes. Distinct from flakes (scurf).
- **Matted/crusted lashes on waking** — consistent with nocturnal mite
  activity (D. folliculorum surfaces at night).
- **Recurring stye/chalazion on the same or adjacent lash** — pathognomonic
  pattern regardless of slit-lamp access.
- **Rosacea on central face + eyelid symptoms** — high pre-test probability.

These raise the pre-test probability enough to justify asking for the
slit-lamp exam at the next visit.

---

## Who to see

**First-line:** an **optometrist or ophthalmologist who has a dry-eye /
ocular-surface subspecialty focus**. They routinely screen for Demodex.
The generic "comprehensive eye exam" provider may not.

**Markers of a practice that does this well:**
- LipiView / LipiScan / Keratograph meibographer on site
- BlephEx offered (in-office TTO debridement)
- Discussion of lid hygiene beyond "use baby shampoo"
- Xdemvy (lotilaner) prescribed, or aware of it

**Markers of a practice that probably does not:**
- Chalazion repeatedly excised without Demodex screening
- "Blepharitis, use warm compresses" without slit-lamp collarette exam
- No meibomian gland imaging

---

## What this changes

1. Any recurring chalazion warrants an immediate Demodex workup — not
   another excision.
2. Any rosacea patient with eye symptoms should be screened on both
   eyelid (slit-lamp) and facial skin (SSSB) in the same visit.
3. Pediatric chalazion (Liang 2014 — 69% Demodex-positive) should have
   collarette exam before "observation" is prescribed.
4. Under-deployment of the free slit-lamp exam is the highest-leverage
   single intervention; patient self-advocacy ("please check for
   collarettes") is how this changes at the point of care.
