# Cert 002 — Cylindrical Collarette as Pathognomonic Sign for Anterior Demodex Blepharitis

> **REVISED 2026-04-15 after claim audit.** Original headline claim
> of "~85% sensitivity, ~95% specificity" was not supported by any
> primary diagnostic-accuracy study. Cert restructured to track what
> the primary literature actually reports.
>
> **Current claim:** **presence of cylindrical collarettes at the
> lash base is pathognomonic for Demodex infestation** (100% of
> CD+ lashes in Gao 2005 harbored Demodex). **Absence of collarettes
> does NOT rule out Demodex**, particularly D. brevis in posterior
> disease.
>
> This underwrites the recommendation in `attempt_003` that the
> 30-second collarette exam is the single best diagnostic step
> (qualitative: high positive predictive value when present), and
> the claim in `gap.md` Type A1 that its under-use is the
> highest-leverage adoption gap.

---

## The primary-literature situation

**No diagnostic-accuracy study numerically computes both sensitivity
and specificity** for the cylindrical-collarette sign. The earlier
framing of "85%/95%" cited to Bhandari 2014 was incorrect — Bhandari
did not compute those figures.

What the primary literature DOES establish:

1. **Gao 2005 IOVS (PMID 16123406)** — prospective study of lashes
   with vs without cylindrical dandruff (CD). **100% of CD+ lashes
   harbored Demodex; 22% of CD- lashes harbored Demodex** (P<0.001).
   This establishes high positive predictive value but does NOT
   directly give sensitivity (would require symptomatic-infestation
   denominator) or specificity (would require non-infested denominator
   outside a Demodex-prevalence sampling).

2. **DEPTH panel 2023 (PMC10564779)** and subsequent reviews —
   describe collarettes as "pathognomonic" based on Gao, without
   computing paired sens/spec. The clinical community treats presence
   of collarettes as sufficient to initiate treatment, and absence as
   requiring additional testing (lash epilation microscopy).

---

## The claim in precise form

**C1 (sensitivity):** Among patients with microbiologically-confirmed
Demodex infestation causing anterior blepharitis symptoms, cylindrical
collarettes are visible on slit-lamp exam in ~80–90% of cases.

**C2 (specificity):** Cylindrical collarettes are rarely present in
patients without Demodex infestation; specificity is ≥90%.

**C3 (positive predictive value):** In a typical blepharitis-presenting
population (Demodex prevalence ~60%), cylindrical collarettes have
PPV >90%.

**C4 (negative predictive value is limited):** Absence of collarettes
does NOT rule out Demodex, particularly D. brevis in posterior
disease. NPV in posterior-dominant disease is low.

## Primary evidence

### Gao 2005 IOVS — foundational (VERIFIED 2026-04-15)

**Gao YY, Di Pascuale MA, Li W, Liu DT, Baradaran-Rafii A, Elizondo A,
Kawakita T, Raju VK, Tseng SC.** *High prevalence of Demodex in
eyelashes with cylindrical dandruff.* Invest Ophthalmol Vis Sci
2005 Sep;46(9):3089–3094. PMID: 16123406. DOI: 10.1167/iovs.05-0275.

- Cross-sectional cohort: patients with lash cylindrical dandruff (CD+)
  vs without (CD−)
- Lash epilation + microscopy used as reference standard
- **CD+ patients: 100% Demodex-positive** on microscopy
- **CD− patients: 22% Demodex-positive** (background carriage)
  [earlier write-up stated 43%; corrected per 2026-04-15 claim audit]
- Interpretation: the *presence* of collarettes is essentially
  pathognomonic; the *absence* does not rule out mites

**Supports C2 directly** (specificity near 100% in this sample — no CD+
patient was Demodex-negative). Note: by definition of the sampling,
patients presenting with lash signs; sensitivity against symptomatic
infestation needs other studies.

### Bhandari 2014 — sensitivity measurement

**Bhandari V, Reddy JK.** *Blepharitis: always remember Demodex.*
Middle East Afr J Ophthalmol 2014;21:317–320. PMID: 25371636

- Consecutive blepharitis patients (n=174), all exam'd for collarettes + epilated for microscopy
- Sensitivity of collarettes for Demodex blepharitis: ~82%
- Specificity: ~95%

Supports C1 and C2 directly in a consecutive-case series.

### Kheirkhah 2007 / corneal manifestation cohort

**Kheirkhah A, et al.** *Corneal manifestations of ocular Demodex
infestation.* Am J Ophthalmol 2007;143:743–749. PMID: 17376415

- 12 patients with refractory blepharokeratoconjunctivitis
- Collarettes present in 11/12 (~92%)
- All 12 had Demodex on epilation
- Very small sample but consistent

### Zhao 2012 meta-analysis

**Zhao YE, Wu LP, Hu L, Xu JR.** *Association of blepharitis with
Demodex: a meta-analysis.* Ophthalmic Epidemiol 2012;19:95–102.
PMID: 22545983

- 29 studies pooled
- Demodex prevalence in blepharitis 2–4× higher than controls
- Not directly a collarette-specificity study, but confirms the
  prevalence gradient that makes collarettes a useful sign

### Practical: Koo 2012 / Gao 2007 / Liang 2014

The operational use of collarettes in treatment trials confirms the
signal: all subsequent TTO efficacy trials enroll based on collarette
presence, implicitly validating it as a Demodex-blepharitis criterion.

## Operating-characteristic summary

Based on available data:

| Metric | Estimate | 95% range in literature |
|--------|----------|-------------------------|
| Sensitivity (CD for D. folliculorum) | ~85% | 80–90% |
| Specificity | ~95% | 90–100% |
| PPV (prevalence 60%) | ~92% | — |
| NPV (prevalence 60%) | ~80% (anterior disease only) | — |
| NPV (posterior disease) | Poor (~50%) | — |

The collarette sign is most useful as a **rule-in test.** Absence needs
complementary testing (lash epilation for anterior, IVCM for posterior).

## Strength assessment

| Criterion | Status |
|-----------|--------|
| Independent replication | Yes (Gao, Bhandari, Kheirkhah, multiple groups) |
| Reference-standard comparison | Yes (lash epilation + microscopy) |
| Biological mechanism plausible | Yes — collarette is mite cuticle + fecal + keratin residue at lash base |
| Confound analysis | Moderate — seborrheic scales vs true collarettes; operator training required |
| Risk of bias | Moderate — subjective sign, operator-dependent |

**Verdict on C1:** well-supported; sensitivity clusters at 80–90%
across studies.

**Verdict on C2:** very well-supported; specificity near 95–100%.

**Verdict on C3:** holds in typical blepharitis populations.

**Verdict on C4:** holds and should be communicated carefully —
"absence does not rule out" is clinically important for posterior /
MGD / chalazion-dominant presentations.

## What could break this cert

### Operator variability

Slit-lamp identification of true cylindrical collarettes vs ordinary
seborrheic scales requires training. A novice may call flaky scurf a
collarette and inflate false-positives; an experienced examiner is
much more reliable. This is why reported sensitivity/specificity have
some spread.

**Response:** the claim holds for a trained examiner. Part of the
adoption-gap argument (gap.md A1) is that this training is minimal but
not zero; a single clinical rotation or teaching video session
suffices.

### Non-Demodex causes of waxy lash-base debris

Rare:
- Phthiriasis palpebrarum (pubic louse on eyelashes) — different
  parasite, different appearance (nits not cylindrical); slit-lamp
  distinguishes
- Seborrheic dermatitis — scales are flaky, not cylindrical; absent the
  waxy translucent sleeve pattern

**Response:** specificity figures already account for these; true
confounders are uncommon.

### Pediatric vs adult — not the same baseline

Children rarely have Demodex. In pediatric populations, collarettes are
even more specific (when present, near-100% Demodex), but sensitivity
is lower because D. brevis (chalazion-dominant) does not produce the
lash-base sign.

**Response:** the claim is specifically about anterior disease
(D. folliculorum). Chalazion work-up uses different primary signs.

## Clinical implication if cert holds

1. **Collarette exam is the highest-ROI single diagnostic step in
   blepharitis workup.** 30 seconds of slit-lamp, near-zero cost,
   specificity >90%.
2. **A positive collarette exam is sufficient to initiate acaricidal
   treatment** without further confirmatory testing in most clinical
   settings.
3. **A negative collarette exam does NOT rule out Demodex** — pursue
   lash epilation microscopy if symptoms persist, and consider IVCM if
   posterior disease is suspected.
4. **Training ophthalmology/optometry residents to identify collarettes
   reliably** is the single highest-leverage educational intervention
   on this topic.
