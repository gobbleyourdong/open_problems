# What to Tell Your Doctor — A Practical Guide for the patient

> This is not a medical document. It's a communication guide. Your doctor is busy, skeptical by training, and has 15 minutes. This document helps you use those 15 minutes well.

## The 60-Second Pitch

"I have type 1 diabetes, diagnosed in 2019. I was off insulin for 5 years on a ketogenic diet and currently need only 2 units per meal. I've been researching the emerging evidence linking Coxsackievirus B persistence to T1DM, and I'd like to get some baseline bloodwork to evaluate whether I'm a candidate for a supplement-based protocol that targets the underlying viral mechanism. Everything I'm proposing is either over-the-counter or already FDA-approved for other indications. I'd like your guidance and monitoring."

## What to Ask For (Keep It Simple)

### Visit 1: Bloodwork
Hand your doctor this list. Most of it is routine:

**Standard labs (any doctor can order):**
- C-peptide, fasting AND stimulated (mixed-meal tolerance test)
- HbA1c
- Comprehensive metabolic panel
- High-sensitivity troponin I
- NT-proBNP
- CBC with differential
- High-sensitivity CRP
- 25-OH vitamin D
- Testosterone (total + free), FSH — if male
- ECG

**Why each test:**
- C-peptide: "I want to know if I still make any insulin. This determines my treatment options."
- Troponin/BNP: "Coxsackievirus can affect the heart. I want a baseline."
- CRP: "I want to track inflammation over time."
- Vitamin D: "Deficiency is common in T1DM and affects immune regulation."

**Specialty labs (may need referral or research lab):**
- CVB1-5 neutralizing antibodies
- Serum enteroviral RT-PCR

"If these aren't available through your lab, that's fine — we can start without them."

## What NOT to Say

- Don't say "I found a cure for diabetes on the internet"
- Don't say "I want to stop my insulin"
- Don't mention 12 diseases, systematic approach, or the full campaign
- Don't bring a 50-page document
- Don't argue about whether CVB causes T1DM (the evidence is debated — acknowledge this)

## What TO Say If Asked "Where Is This Coming From?"

"There's growing evidence from studies like the DiViD trial in Norway, where researchers found enterovirus in the islets of all six newly diagnosed T1DM patients they biopsied. Fluoxetine has been shown to inhibit Coxsackievirus replication in cell and animal studies. I'm not claiming this is proven — I'm asking if we can monitor me while I try a low-risk approach."

**Key citations to have ready (your doctor can look these up):**
- Krogvold et al. 2015 (Diabetes): DiViD study, VP1 in T1DM islets
- Benkahla et al. 2018: Fluoxetine as CVB antiviral
- Youm et al. 2015 (Nature Medicine): BHB and NLRP3
- Kühl et al. 2003 (Circulation): IFN-β clearing enterovirus from DCM hearts
- Longo et al. 2017 (Cell): FMD and beta cell regeneration

## The Prescription Conversation

### What you need from your doctor:
1. **Lab orders** (listed above)
2. **Fluoxetine 20mg prescription** — "I'd like to try fluoxetine. It's FDA-approved for depression, well-studied, and has emerging evidence as a Coxsackievirus antiviral. I'd like to try it for 6-12 months."
3. **Monitoring agreement** — "Can we recheck C-peptide and basic labs every 3 months to track whether this is doing anything?"
4. **Optional: colchicine 0.5mg** — "Colchicine suppresses the NLRP3 inflammasome, which drives islet inflammation. It's FDA-approved, cheap, and well-tolerated."

### What you DON'T need a prescription for:
- Vitamin D, omega-3, butyrate, magnesium, CoQ10, selenium, zinc — all OTC
- Fasting-mimicking diet — dietary choice, not medical intervention
- Cold exposure / Wim Hof breathing — lifestyle choice

### If the doctor says no to fluoxetine:
"I understand your hesitation. Would you be willing to order the labs so we at least have baseline data? I'll start with the supplement and lifestyle components, and we can revisit the fluoxetine conversation based on results."

## The Monitoring Schedule

Propose this to your doctor:

| Timepoint | Labs | Purpose |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Baseline | Full panel (above) | Starting point |
| Month 3 | C-peptide, HbA1c, CRP, CMP | First signal check |
| Month 6 | C-peptide, HbA1c, CRP, CMP, troponin, BNP | Midpoint assessment |
| Month 9 | C-peptide, HbA1c, CRP | Continued tracking |
| Month 12 | Full panel | Endpoint assessment |

"This is basically quarterly bloodwork with a few extra markers. It's low-risk monitoring for a low-risk intervention."

## The Safety Discussion

Your doctor will want to know about risks. Honest answers:

**Fluoxetine risks**: SSRI side effects (nausea, headache, insomnia first 1-2 weeks, sexual side effects). Well-characterized — millions of people take it. Added bleeding risk if combined with NSAIDs (avoid or use PPI).

**Fasting risks in T1DM**: Hypoglycemia. "I'll reduce my insulin during fasting days, monitor glucose every 2-3 hours, and stop if glucose drops below 70. I'd like your guidance on insulin adjustment."

**Supplement risks**: "Everything I'm taking is within standard recommended ranges. I'm not mega-dosing anything. Selenium stays under 200μg, zinc at 15mg with copper, vitamin D at 5000 IU."

**What this is NOT**: "This is not an alternative medicine protocol. I'm not stopping my insulin. I'm not rejecting conventional care. I'm adding a supplement and lifestyle regimen targeting emerging CVB research, and I want you to monitor me."

## If Your Doctor Is Supportive

Great. Ask them to:
1. Order the baseline labs
2. Prescribe fluoxetine 20mg (start at 10mg, titrate at 1 week)
3. Consider prescribing colchicine 0.5mg daily
4. Agree to quarterly monitoring
5. Review results together at each visit

## If Your Doctor Is Dismissive

Don't fight. Instead:
1. Ask for labs anyway ("even if you don't agree with my hypothesis, the data is useful")
2. Start supplements and lifestyle changes on your own (all OTC, no prescription needed)
3. Track your own data (insulin requirements, glucose averages, how you feel)
4. Come back in 3 months with data, not arguments
5. Consider finding a functional medicine or integrative medicine physician who's more open to this framework

## The One Thing That Matters Most

**Get the stimulated C-peptide test.** Everything else is secondary. This one number tells you whether you have residual beta cell function and whether the protocol has a chance of restoring insulin independence. Without it, you're flying blind.
