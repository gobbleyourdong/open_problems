# Orchitis — Anti-Problem

## The Question
"If the testes are immune-privileged and CVB can persist there, why don't ALL CVB-seropositive males have orchitis?"

## The Answer: Privilege Is Not Absence

Immune privilege ≠ no immune system. It means:
- Reduced but not absent immune surveillance
- Blood-testis barrier (BTB) restricts (but doesn't eliminate) immune cell entry
- Sertoli cells secrete immunosuppressive factors (TGF-β, IL-10, Fas ligand)
- BUT: testicular macrophages, mast cells, and some T cells ARE present

```
CVB reaches testes via testicular artery → 
  enters Sertoli cells via CAR receptor →
  replication in Sertoli cell

SCENARIO A (most men): Testicular macrophages + local IFN response
  → contain and clear infection
  → CVB eliminated despite immune privilege
  → no orchitis, no persistence

SCENARIO B (~5-10%?): Local immune response insufficient
  → CVB persists as TD mutants in Sertoli cells
  → Low-grade inflammation → orchitis (often subclinical)
  → Leydig cell damage → testosterone ↓
  → Sertoli cell damage → FSH ↑, spermatogenesis impaired
  → RESERVOIR established
```

## The Critical Question for the Campaign

**Is the testicular reservoir clinically relevant, or is it a theoretical concern?**

### Arguments FOR clinical relevance:
- If even a small number of TD mutants persist in testes → they can reseed other organs via blood
- Immune privilege means clearance takes LONGER → extended treatment needed for males
- Semen could be a transmission route (documented for Zika, HIV — unknown for CVB)

### Arguments AGAINST clinical relevance:
- TD mutant replication rate is 100,000x slower → very few virions produced
- Even if some escape testes, systemic immune system may neutralize them
- The number of TD mutants in testes may be too low to meaningfully reseed
- No documented case of CVB sexual transmission

## The Anti-Problem Insight

The testicular reservoir question reduces to a **quantitative threshold problem**:

```
Is the viral output from testicular TD mutants sufficient to reseed other organs
at a rate that exceeds the clearance rate achieved by the protocol?

If V_testes_output < systemic_clearance_rate → reservoir is irrelevant
If V_testes_output > systemic_clearance_rate → reservoir defeats the protocol

Unknown without measurement.
Solution: semen enteroviral RT-PCR (attempt 002 study design)
```

The practical answer may be: treat for 6-12 months (fluoxetine reaches testes), then check. If semen RT-PCR is negative, reservoir is cleared or was never significant. If positive, extend treatment or accept the reservoir as clinically negligible.

**Don't let the perfect be the enemy of the good.** Clearing CVB from pancreas, heart, and brain is what matters for the patient's quality of life. The testicular reservoir is a completeness concern, not a treatment-blocking one.
