## The Claim

**Most modern lip and facial-skin products are composed of lipid bases that Demodex folliculorum and brevis can directly digest via salivary lipases. Chronic application of these products to perioral skin extends Demodex substrate beyond the natural sebum supply of the pilosebaceous unit, raises local mite carrying capacity, and contributes to chronic perioral cheilitis, lip-balm dependency, and a subset of perioral dermatitis cases. The mechanism is documented in dermatology literature — including explicit recommendations to "avoid oil-based cosmetics" for Demodex-prone patients — but is not translated to consumer-facing lip-care marketing or product design. The market gap is explained by disciplinary silos (Demodex research lives in ophthalmology and rosacea-derm, not cosmetics) and by industry incentive (the dominant lip-balm ingredient stack is the substrate problem).**

This attempt advances the model from *"Demodex eats endogenous sebum"* to *"Demodex eats endogenous sebum + exogenous lipid products,"* with a substrate ranking that distinguishes the substrate-rich modern formulations (tallow, lanolin, jojoba, coconut, shea, squalane) from the substrate-poor traditional bases (petrolatum, mineral oil, dimethicone) and the actively anti-Demodex bases (sulfur, tea tree oil, manuka oil, salicylic acid, zinc pyrithione).

## §1. Demodex feeding mechanism — extracellular lipase digestion (VERIFIED)

Demodex folliculorum and D. brevis do not ingest sebum whole. The feeding cycle:

1. **Anchoring.** The mite embeds head-down in the follicle (D. folliculorum) or sebaceous gland (D. brevis). The stylophore — a pin-like piercing structure — penetrates host cells. Paired chelicerae tear cellular debris.
2. **Extracellular digestion.** Salivary glands release **proteases and lipases** into the follicular environment. Lipases hydrolyze sebum triglycerides and wax esters into free fatty acids and monoglycerides; proteases cleave keratin and cellular proteins.
3. **Sucking pump.** A sclerotized pharynx ingests the predigested liquid material via suction.
4. **Internal digestion.** Mites also digest internally-carried bacteria — *Bacillus oleronius* canonically — likely contributing to the proteolytic / lipolytic toolkit.
5. **No excretory organs.** Undigested material accumulates and is regurgitated; combined with epithelial cells, eggs, and keratin, this forms the cylindrical lash collarettes diagnostic of Demodex blepharitis.

**Key implication for substrate:** Demodex's lipase chemistry cleaves **ester bonds** (triglycerides, wax esters, sterol esters, free fatty acid esters). Substrates without ester bonds — saturated hydrocarbons (petrolatum, mineral oil), silicones (dimethicone), silica, water-soluble humectants (glycerin, hyaluronic acid) — are **not lipase substrates** and cannot be metabolized as a primary food source. This is the chemical foundation of the substrate ranking in §3.

## §2. Refined human sebum composition (CORRECTION over prior attempts)

Prior POD attempts referenced sebum as Demodex food without specifying composition. Verified breakdown:

| Component | % of sebum | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Triglycerides** | ~57% | Main lipase substrate; hydrolyzed to FFAs by both Demodex lipase and *C. acnes* lipase |
| **Wax esters** | ~25% | Sebum-specific; lanolin (wool wax) is the main exogenous analog |
| **Squalene** | ~13% | Unique to human sebum at this concentration; squalane (saturated form) is the cosmetic analog |
| **Cholesterol esters** | ~3% | Lanolin is rich in cholesterol esters (~33% of lanolin) |
| **Free cholesterol** | ~2% | |

**Critical refinement: sapienic acid.**

**Sapienic acid (16:1, Δ6-cis hexadecenoic acid)** comprises ~20–25% of total sebum fatty acids and is **unique to human sebum** — not present in other body lipids and not present in plant or animal-derived cosmetic oils. Sapienic acid is biosynthesized from palmitic acid by sebum-specific Δ6-desaturase. **Sebaleic acid** (18:2, Δ5,8) is also sebum-unique.

**This refines (and softens) the "tallow ≈ synthetic sebum" framing:**

- Tallow FA profile: oleic ~40–47%, palmitic ~24%, stearic ~11–19% (varies by diet; grass-fed has more stearic). 50% saturated / 42% MUFA / 4% PUFA.
- Human sebum FA profile: sapienic ~25%, palmitic ~25%, oleic ~10–15%, sebaleic ~5%, plus minor stearic and other.

Tallow **partially overlaps** sebum on palmitic and (loosely) on oleic, but **lacks sapienic acid entirely**. So tallow is best described as **"high-quality lipase substrate that mimics the saturated/MUFA backbone of sebum without the species-specific signature"** — not "synthetic sebum." Demodex evolved to feed on sebum specifically, so tallow may be sub-optimal substrate compared with native sebum, but it is still a digestible triglyceride mass and lacks any antimicrobial brake.

The same refinement applies to all plant oils and animal fats: they provide TG/wax-ester substrate but lack the sapienic signature. They are still substrate, just not the ideal substrate.

**Sources:**
- [Sebaceous gland lipids — Stewart M (1992) PMC2835893 / PMID 1498012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2835893/) — canonical sebum composition
- [Sebum analysis of individuals with and without acne (2009) PMC2835908](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2835908/) — sebaleic and sapienic acid as sebum-unique markers
- [Tallow, Rendered Animal Fat, and Its Biocompatibility With Skin: A Scoping Review (2024) PMC11193910](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11193910/) — tallow composition + cosmetic use review

## §3. Substrate ranking of consumer lip/skin products

Products ranked by Demodex substrate quality. Higher tier = more mite food. Tier D = anti-Demodex (substrate-poor + actively miticidal).

### Tier S — Sebum-analog grade (maximum substrate)

| Product | Composition | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Lanolin** | 48% wax esters + 33% sterol esters + 6% lactones + 6% free sterols (incl. cholesterol) + 3.5% FFAs | "Lanolin's molecular similarity to human sebum allows it to integrate seamlessly with the skin's lipid matrix" — explicitly described as a sebum-analog in cosmetic literature. The wax-ester and cholesterol-ester profile uniquely substrates the wax-ester branch of Demodex's diet that triglyceride oils cannot. **Aquaphor contains lanolin alcohols**; Lansinoh is purified lanolin marketed for nipple care; many "ultra-rich" balms include lanolin. |
| **Tallow** | TG: oleic 40–47% / palmitic 24% / stearic 11–19% + minor | Closest mammalian fat to sebum on saturated/MUFA backbone. Lacks sapienic acid (see §2). Currently trending as "ancestral skincare" (Toup & Co, Vintage Tradition, etc.) — direct substrate for Demodex without antimicrobial brake. |
| **Lard** | TG: similar to tallow, slightly more unsaturated | Less common in modern cosmetics but appears in some traditional balms |
| **Squalane** | Saturated squalene, single hydrocarbon-rich molecule | Squalene = 13% of human sebum. Squalane is the shelf-stable (hydrogenated) form. Marketed as "biomimetic." Direct evidence for Demodex metabolism is sparse [HEDGE — see §11], but biochemically squalane is recognizable to sebum-evolved digestive systems. *C. acnes* metabolizes squalene in vivo. |

### Tier A — Triglyceride / wax-ester base, no antimicrobial brake

| Product | Composition | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Refined coconut oil (RBD)** | TG: lauric 50%, myristic 18%, palmitic 8%, oleic 6% | Polyphenols stripped during refining; lauric acid present but in TG form (monolaurin, the antimicrobial form, requires lipase action to liberate) |
| **Olive oil** | TG: oleic ~70%, palmitic ~13%, linoleic ~10% | Refined olive lacks polyphenol fraction; oleic acid is a recognized sebum component |
| **Shea butter** | TG: oleic 40–55%, stearic 35–45% + 5–10% unsaponifiables | Unsaponifiables (cinnamic acid esters, triterpenes) are mildly anti-inflammatory but not anti-Demodex |
| **Jojoba oil** | Wax ester (97% liquid wax esters of long-chain mono-unsaturated FAs) | **Marketed as "balancing" because it mimics sebum wax esters — but if Demodex eats wax esters, jojoba is the most direct plant-derived substrate.** |
| **Cocoa butter** | TG: stearic 35%, oleic 35%, palmitic 25% | Same story as shea |
| **Sweet almond, avocado, argan, marula, etc.** | TG, varying FA profiles, all oleic/palmitic-dominant | All in this tier |

### Tier B — Substrate present, partial antimicrobial action

| Product | Composition | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Virgin coconut oil** | TG: lauric 50% in TG form | After lipase hydrolysis, monolaurin is antimicrobial against *C. acnes* and some Gram+ bacteria. Net effect on Demodex is ambiguous: the substrate is digestible, but suppression of the bacterial competitor (C. acnes) may *worsen* Demodex equilibrium (see §5 — competition data). Plausibly net-negative for Demodex-prone skin despite the antimicrobial fraction. |
| **Beeswax** | Complex wax esters of palmitate + long-chain alcohols + ~10% hydrocarbons | Wax esters are substrate, but the saturated long-chain alcohols are less lipase-accessible than triglycerides. Limited substrate quality, but present. |
| **Castor oil** | TG: ricinoleic acid (hydroxy FA) ~85% + minor oleic/linoleic | Unusual hydroxy-FA structure; reduced lipase recognition is plausible but not directly tested. Mild antimicrobial activity. |
| **Carnauba wax** | Long-chain wax esters | Less digestible than beeswax; rarely used as a primary base |

### Tier C — Substrate-poor traditional bases

| Product | Composition | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Petrolatum / petroleum jelly** | Saturated straight-chain alkanes (C20–C40), no ester bonds, no FAs | **No ester bonds for Demodex lipase to cleave.** Direct in-vitro substrate testing of petrolatum vs. Demodex is not in the published literature [HEDGE — inferred from chemistry]. Industrial petroleum exposure has been shown to alter skin microbiome (Dietzia, Paracoccus, Kocuria — oil-degrading environmental bacteria), but these are not relevant to Demodex carrying capacity on facial/perioral skin in normal use. Pharmaceutical-grade petrolatum is the lowest-substrate widely-used balm base. |
| **Mineral oil** | Liquid alkanes, same chemistry as petrolatum | Same answer as petrolatum |
| **Dimethicone / cyclomethicone** | Polysiloxanes (Si-O backbone) | Completely indigestible by mites |
| **Glycerin / propylene glycol / hyaluronic acid** | Humectants, no lipid carbon | Not substrate |
| **Talc / silica / titanium dioxide / zinc oxide** | Mineral solids | Not substrate; zinc oxide has mild antimicrobial activity |

### Tier D — Actively anti-Demodex

| Product | Mechanism | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| **Sulfur 5–10%** in petrolatum or oil base | Reduces to H₂S + polythionic acids; mite-toxic | Decades of pediatric scabies use; safest OTC anti-Demodex. The user's "instant relief" cream. |
| **Tea tree oil 5–10% (terpinen-4-ol)** | Disrupts mite cuticle lipid membrane (Tighe 2013 in vitro) | Standard of care for Demodex blepharitis; carrier-oil contradiction discussed in §4 |
| **Manuka oil (β-triketones)** | Similar membrane mechanism, possibly more potent than TTO at lower concentration | Less widely available |
| **Salicylic acid** | Keratolytic, reduces follicular substrate accumulation | Indirect — doesn't kill mites but reduces their habitat |
| **Zinc pyrithione** | Antifungal + mild antimicrobial | Used in seborrheic dermatitis shampoos |
| **Topical metronidazole (Rx)** | Anti-inflammatory + weak anti-Demodex | First-line for POD/rosacea |
| **Topical ivermectin 1% (Rx, Soolantra)** | GABA channel paralysis | Approved for rosacea, off-label POD |
| **Topical permethrin 5% (Rx)** | Sodium channel disruption | Older anti-parasitic, also active |

## §4. The carrier-oil contradiction (extension of attempt_005)

attempt_005 recommended "diluted tea tree oil 3–5% **in carrier oil**" as a pediatric-safe anti-Demodex topical. The unaddressed contradiction:

**The carrier oil is the substrate.** Diluting TTO in coconut, jojoba, or olive oil delivers the active in a vehicle that is itself Tier A mite food. The TTO disrupts mite cuticles where it contacts them; the carrier oil supplies digestible substrate to the surviving mite population. Whether the net effect is anti-Demodex or pro-Demodex over a treatment course depends on the relative kinetics — and there is no published in-vivo comparison of TTO-in-coconut vs. TTO-in-petrolatum vs. TTO-in-sulfur-base.

**The same carrier-oil contradiction is inherent in many "natural" anti-Demodex products.** Tea tree oil eyelid wipes use water-glycerin bases (avoiding the carrier-oil problem). Sulfur ointment uses petrolatum (low substrate). But many DIY and small-brand TTO preparations use coconut, jojoba, or argan as the carrier — undermining their own active.

**The cleaner formulation, per the substrate ranking, is:**
- TTO 5% in petrolatum or dimethicone base (low substrate carrier, active intact)
- Sulfur 6% in petrolatum (the user's empirical hit — no contradiction)
- Sulfur 6% + TTO 5% in petrolatum (combination, no substrate contradiction)
- Avoid: TTO in coconut, jojoba, almond, argan, or any Tier S/A oil for chronic application

This is a near-deletable layer in the existing protocol — the active is right, the carrier is wrong, and the fix is changing the carrier.

## §5. Microbiome competition — C. acnes vs. Demodex (VERIFIED)

A previously under-weighted axis. *Cutibacterium acnes* (formerly *Propionibacterium acnes*) and Demodex **compete for the same substrate** — sebum triglycerides. Both deploy lipases. Both reside in the pilosebaceous unit.

Verified:
- **C. acnes is dominant on healthy facial skin.** Sebum-rich areas are colonized by high proportions of C. acnes.
- **In rosacea, C. acnes is significantly reduced.** Both subtypes (papulopustular, erythematotelangiectatic) show decreased C. acnes relative abundance.
- **In rosacea, D. folliculorum density is 5.7× higher than controls.** This is the canonical Demodex-rosacea correlation.
- **Inverse relationship documented across rosacea cohorts.** As C. acnes drops, Demodex rises.

**Mechanistic implications:**

1. **Antibiotic-induced rosacea/POD bloom.** Tetracyclines (doxycycline, minocycline) prescribed for acne reduce C. acnes ~2× in relative abundance. If C. acnes is the primary microbial competitor capping Demodex carrying capacity, tetracycline-driven C. acnes reduction may **shift the equilibrium toward Demodex** — a plausible mechanism for "post-antibiotic rosacea" presentations and for adult-female POD that emerges after acne treatment courses.

2. **Sub-antimicrobial doxycycline (40 mg) used in rosacea/POD.** Operates below the dose that reduces C. acnes meaningfully but retains the host anti-inflammatory effect (matrix metalloproteinase modulation). The dose was chosen empirically; the C. acnes / Demodex competition framework provides a mechanistic rationale that the existing POD literature does not fully name.

3. **Isotretinoin reduces Demodex via sebum reduction.** Verified — Chakmakchi et al. 2025, prospective study showing reduced Demodex density during isotretinoin course. But isotretinoin also reduces C. acnes. The net effect is "less of both," resolving the equilibrium concern.

4. **Three-way equilibrium with Malassezia.** Malassezia is also sebum-dependent and the third competitor in the pilosebaceous niche. Co-presence with Demodex in seborrheic dermatitis is well-documented. The full equilibrium is mites × bacteria × yeast competing for the same substrate.

**Sources:**
- [Rosacea and the Microbiome: A Systematic Review (2021) PMC7859152](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7859152/) — synthesizes C. acnes / Demodex inverse relationship
- [Cutibacterium acnes Dysbiosis: Alternative Therapeutics for Clinical Application (2023) MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/13/21/12086) — antibiotic effect on C. acnes
- [Therapeutic Modulation of Demodex Density via Isotretinoin (2025) PMC12117431](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12117431/) — sebum reduction → Demodex reduction

## §6. Why hasn't anyone raised this? — the silo / incentive answer

The operator's question deserves a structural answer. The mechanism is documented in dermatology literature — explicit recommendations to "avoid oil-based cosmetics" appear in current Demodex reviews — but the consumer-product translation is absent. Six structural reasons, ranked by load-bearing weight:

### 6a. Disciplinary silos

Demodex research lives in two communities:
- **Ophthalmology / optometry** (Demodex blepharitis, Tarsus pharmaceuticals' lotilaner). They study eyelashes, not lips.
- **Dermatology / rosacea** (rosacea, papulopustular eruptions). They study cheeks, nose, forehead — not lips, and not lip products specifically.

The **lip is a no-mans-land** between dental, oral medicine, cosmetic chemistry, and general dermatology. No discipline owns "vermillion border microbiology" or "lip-margin microbial ecology" as a research target. **Perioral Demodex folliculitis is a recognized clinical entity** (see §7) but is rare enough — or rather, rare enough *as a recognized diagnosis* — that no specialty has built a research program around it.

### 6b. The lip-balm category exists in cosmetics, not medicine

Lip products are regulated as cosmetics in the US (FDA, but with looser standards than OTC drugs). They are tested for irritation, allergy, heavy metals, microbial contamination of the product itself — but not for "does this preferentially feed commensal organisms on the host." There is no testing framework for substrate-amplification effects. The category never required this question.

### 6c. The mainstream lip-balm model is pure physics

The standard model of "why lips chap" is:
- Vermillion has thin epidermis, no stratum corneum keratinization
- No sebaceous glands on the vermillion proper → no native lipid film
- Wind / cold / low humidity / lip-licking → trans-epidermal water loss (TEWL)
- Occlusive lipid application → reduces TEWL → relief

This is correct as far as it goes. **It has no microbiology dimension.** The question "what microorganisms might use the applied lipid as substrate, and where do those organisms live" is not in the canonical lip-balm formulation rationale. Oils → moisture → relief, full stop. Substrate-for-organism never enters the frame.

### 6d. The relief loop hides the cause

Two separable effects from one chapstick application:
- **Immediate (physics):** Occlusion stops TEWL → relief within minutes (real, repeatable)
- **Delayed (microbiology):** Lipid substrate available to perioral pilosebaceous mites → mite proliferation over weeks → low-grade chronic perioral irritation → patient interprets as "needs more chapstick" → reapplication

The patient experiences only the relief loop. The substrate loop runs silently. The "chapstick addiction" folk concept names the pattern but locates the cause in the lips' "dependence" on external moisture rather than in the perioral microbiology.

### 6e. Industry incentive is structurally negative

US lip-care market is ~$1B/year. Dominant players (ChapStick / Haleon, Burt's Bees / Clorox, Aquaphor / Beiersdorf, EOS, Carmex, plus the 2022–2024 tallow brands) all use lipid-heavy bases — Tier S or Tier A in the §3 ranking. "Doesn't feed mites" is not a marketable USP because:
- There is no recognized condition to market against (perioral Demodex folliculitis lacks consumer awareness)
- The category's marketing framework is hydration / nourishment / "natural ingredients," all of which trend toward higher-substrate formulations
- Reformulating to substrate-poor bases (petrolatum, dimethicone) would be commercially unattractive given current "petrolatum is bad" / "natural is good" consumer narratives

Academic dermatology funding flows partly from these companies. Independent investigation has weak structural support.

### 6f. The "natural = safe" framing

Tallow, coconut, olive, jojoba, shea, lanolin are all marketed as ancestral / traditional / natural. The implicit assumption: traditional fats are skin-safe. The unstated counter-question — "what microorganism evolved to digest these lipids?" — is not in the natural-skincare framework. Lanolin is sheep wool wax. Tallow is rendered beef fat. Coconut oil is plant-derived triglyceride. To Demodex, these are all variations on the food it evolved to eat.

**Composition with v9 Three-Source Triangulation:** This is a textbook three-source-missing case. Dermatology literature has the mechanism (Source 3 — ether). Patient observation has the symptomatic pattern (Source 2 — operator/patient). Cosmetic-product framing lacks both (the discipline that owns the products doesn't read either of the first two literatures). The translation step never happens because no one is positioned to make it.

## §7. Lip-specific dimension — perioral Demodex folliculitis as recognized entity

A correction over earlier framing: **perioral Demodex folliculitis IS a documented clinical entity**, distinct from but commonly misdiagnosed as perioral dermatitis. The phenotype:

- Papulopustular eruption involving the perioral region
- May spare the vermillion border (overlapping with classical POD presentation)
- Higher Demodex density on standardized skin surface biopsy than POD
- Responsive to anti-Demodex therapy (topical ivermectin, sulfur, permethrin) more than to anti-inflammatory monotherapy

Karincaoglu et al. 2004 documented the clinical importance of D. folliculorum presenting with "nonspecific facial signs and symptoms," including perioral involvement. A JAAD case report (2019) described "Perioral Demodex folliculitis masquerading as perioral dermatitis in the peripartum period" — explicitly naming the diagnostic confusion. Forton et al. and others have published density studies showing Demodex density is significantly elevated in a subset of clinically diagnosed POD cases.

**Anatomic facts:**

- True vermillion (lip surface): near-zero pilosebaceous units. Cannot host Demodex.
- Vermillion border (~1 mm transition): minimal follicles, minimal sebum. Low Demodex carrying capacity.
- Perioral skin (1–10 mm from vermillion): normal facial pilosebaceous density. Supports Demodex.
- "Clear zone" of POD (~5 mm directly adjacent to vermillion): pilosebaceous-poor by histology. attempt_002 hypothesized — and the perioral Demodex folliculitis literature confirms — that the clear zone is pilosebaceous-poor and therefore Demodex-poor, explaining why this band is uninvolved.

**The artificial-substrate-extension hypothesis:**

Repeated chapstick application to perioral skin (intentionally targeting the lip itself but inevitably contacting the surrounding 5–10 mm of perioral skin) supplies exogenous lipid substrate to perioral pilosebaceous units. The natural pilosebaceous-poor "clear zone" can become substrate-rich via topical application even though it lacks endogenous sebum. The **mites don't just live in follicles fed by their host — they live in follicles fed by whatever lipid is being smeared on top.**

This is a testable prediction:

- **Skin surface biopsy of perioral skin in chronic-chapstick users vs. non-users** should show higher Demodex density in chronic users at the same baseline sebum production
- **Specifically:** users of Tier S/A oil-based balms should show higher density than users of Tier C petrolatum-based balms or non-users
- **Sulfur or TTO lip products** should reduce density and produce symptomatic relief on a 2–4 week timeline (one mite life cycle)

The operator's empirical observation (sulfur+TTO cream → instant relief) is partially consistent with the prediction, though "instant" relief over hours/days is faster than the mite-population-decline timeline (weeks), suggesting the relief mechanism may include both immediate cuticle disruption and downstream inflammation reduction.

## §8. Host susceptibility — multiplicative model

Density across adult facial skin ranges from undetectable to >100 mites/cm². Substrate alone cannot explain the variance — most adults use roughly similar products. Host factors do most of the work, and the variance is multiplicative across at least five axes.

### Axis 1 — Sebum volume (dominant single factor)

- **Androgens.** Pubertal sebum production is 5–10× pre-pubertal. Males maintain higher rates through life. Sex difference in baseline density tracks this.
- **Genetic skin type.** "Oily" individuals produce 2–4× the sebum of "dry" individuals. Demodex density correlates.
- **Age curve.** Density rises through puberty, peaks 30s–60s, declines slightly in elderly (lower sebum output).
- **Diet.** High-glycemic + high-dairy diets increase sebum via insulin / IGF-1 → SREBP → sebaceous lipogenesis. Low-glycemic diets reduce sebum. Low-fat per se has minimal sebum effect; high-glycemic does.
- **Cortisol / stress** — acute sebum increase; explains stress-flare patterns in rosacea/POD.
- **Hormonal events.** Pregnancy / OCPs / perimenopause cause sebum swings; the canonical "adult female POD" trigger.

### Axis 2 — Sebum composition

Two people with same sebum volume can have different lipid composition. Squalene fraction varies 5–20%, wax ester chain length varies, FFA fraction varies. Higher-substrate composition supports higher Demodex density at the same volume. Less clinically used because composition is harder to assay than volume.

### Axis 3 — Local immune cap

Normally the host immune system caps Demodex at low density. When the cap drops:

- **Topical corticosteroids** — the canonical bloom mechanism. Density increases significantly with length of treatment (verified Karincaoglu et al.).
- **Topical calcineurin inhibitors** (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) — same direction, smaller effect, can also drive Demodex
- **Systemic immunosuppression** — chemotherapy, HIV (CD4 <200), transplant tacrolimus → severe Demodex folliculitis
- **Diabetes** — modest bloom association
- **TLR2 polymorphism** on keratinocytes — recognizes mite chitin. Under-expression → tolerance → bloom. Over-expression → inflammation → POD/rosacea phenotype.
- **Cathelicidin / kallikrein-5 axis** (already in `attempt_004` and `attempt_006`) — KLK5 cleaves cathelicidin into pro-inflammatory LL-37 fragments; this determines whether high density becomes *symptomatic* (not whether it's *high*).
- **Mast cell density** — ~2× perifollicular mast cells in rosacea; flushing component.

### Axis 4 — Microbiome competition (§5)

- C. acnes carriage caps Demodex; C. acnes reduction (antibiotic, isotretinoin, dysbiosis) raises mite carrying capacity
- Bacillus oleronius internal carriage modulates inflammation per unit density
- Malassezia three-way competition

### Axis 5 — Skin barrier integrity

Compromised barrier (eczema, atopy, chronic chapping, lip-licking, SLS-containing toothpaste, harsh cleansers, mouth-breathing, soap-based cleansers) → mite migration easier, substrate leakage, immune access amplified. **Both more density AND more inflammation per unit density.**

### Axis 6 — Exogenous substrate (this attempt's contribution)

- Tier S/A product use (lanolin, tallow, coconut, jojoba, shea, squalane) extends substrate beyond endogenous sebum
- Particularly impactful in low-sebum anatomic regions (perioral skin in chapstick users)
- Multiplicative with Axis 1 (high-sebum individual + Tier S product = compounded substrate)

### The bloom person — synthesis

Symptomatic bloomers typically have:

**(Axis 1: high sebum) × (Axis 3: suppressed immune cap) × (Axis 4: shifted microbiome) × (Axis 5: impaired barrier) × (Axis 6: exogenous substrate amplification)**

Two factors → modest density rise, asymptomatic. Four to five factors → clinical POD / rosacea / chronic perioral cheilitis. People who never bloom have at least one axis missing.

Cross-linkage to attempt_006 (dysbiosis framework import): the M4 host threshold operates at Axis 3 (immune cap) and Axis 4 (microbiome). The M6 floor operates at Axis 3 (developmentally set Treg pool). This attempt adds Axis 6 (exogenous substrate) as an independent multiplier not previously named.

## §9. Treatment predictions

If the substrate-amplification model is correct, the following should hold:

### Strong predictions (high prior, easy to test)

1. **Switching from Tier S/A balm to Tier C balm (petrolatum) should improve chronic perioral cheilitis on a 4–8 week timeline** in a subset of chronic chapstick users — without any pharmaceutical addition.
2. **Sulfur 6% in petrolatum, applied as lip balm 2× daily, should outperform Tier S/A balms** for perioral Demodex folliculitis and for the substrate-amplified subset of POD.
3. **Tea tree oil 5% in petrolatum or dimethicone base should outperform tea tree oil 5% in coconut/jojoba/almond carrier** for chronic anti-Demodex use (carrier-oil contradiction §4).
4. **Patients with antibiotic-exposed (post-tetracycline) rosacea/POD should respond particularly well to the substrate switch** — their C. acnes competition has been removed; reducing exogenous substrate is one of the few remaining levers.

### Medium predictions

5. **Lanolin-containing products (Aquaphor, Lansinoh) should perform worse than pure-petrolatum products** for chronic perioral application in Demodex-prone individuals, despite Aquaphor's "skin-protectant" reputation.
6. **The adult-female POD pattern correlates with chronic lip-balm use independent of hormone status** (a partial alternative explanation for the "adult women" demographic skew).
7. **Tallow-based "natural skincare" trend (2022–) should produce a measurable rise in clinically presenting facial demodicosis** over 5–10 years in users — testable via dermatology-clinic incidence trends.

### Weak predictions (mechanistically plausible but harder to isolate)

8. **Mouth-breathers and chronic lip-lickers should have higher perioral Demodex density** at any given chapstick exposure, due to barrier compromise (Axis 5) compounding substrate amplification (Axis 6).
9. **Switching from oil-based facial moisturizer to petrolatum-based or humectant-only moisturizer should reduce facial Demodex density** in non-rosacea/POD populations measurably over 2–3 mite life cycles.

### Practical protocol for the operator's specific scenario

Given the empirical observation (sulfur+TTO cream → instant lip relief):

1. **Continue the sulfur+TTO cream as needed for symptomatic relief.** Phenomenology already validates the active.

4. **Consider commercial / DIY formulation:** sulfur 6% + tea tree oil 5% in pure petrolatum base would be the substrate-clean version of the "mite-b-gone" formula adapted for lip use. The smell remains the limiting factor.
5. **Remove other Tier S/A products from facial routine** if there is a chronic facial component — particularly any tallow, jojoba, or lanolin-heavy products.

## §10. Anti-problem

What would a counterexample look like — a case the substrate-amplification model could NOT explain?

**C1. Chronic perioral cheilitis with minimal/no chapstick history that resolves with sulfur application.**
- Would suggest Demodex amplification can occur from endogenous sebum alone in some individuals (consistent with attempt_002 Demodex primacy)
- Substrate-amplification model is incomplete but not falsified — it's a contributory mechanism, not the only one
- Soft falsifier

**C2. Chronic chapstick user with Tier S product and low/normal Demodex density on perioral biopsy.**
- Would suggest substrate amplification has saturable kinetics — once sebum is present, additional exogenous lipid does not raise density meaningfully
- Or: this individual has a strong immune cap (Axis 3) that prevents bloom regardless of substrate
- Hard to interpret without measuring all six axes

**C3. Switch from Tier S to Tier C balm produces no improvement in chronic cheilitis over 8+ weeks.**
- Would suggest substrate-amplification is not the dominant axis in this case
- Other mechanisms (contact irritant, atopic, allergic, deeper structural Demodex reservoir) are dominant
- Would require checking other axes (sebum, immune, barrier) before falsifying the model

**C4. Sulfur+TTO lip product produces no relief in a confirmed perioral Demodex folliculitis case.**
- Would falsify the active mechanism — but would conflict with strong prior literature on sulfur + TTO anti-Demodex activity
- Most likely explanation if observed: dose insufficient, vehicle wrong (substrate-rich carrier diluting active), or non-Demodex inflammatory driver

**C5. Pure-petrolatum lip balm causes Demodex bloom in a controlled skin-surface-biopsy study.**
- Would falsify the "petrolatum is substrate-poor" claim and require revising §3 Tier C
- Would require explaining how Demodex digests straight-chain alkanes (no known mechanism)
- Most likely explanation if observed: contamination of "pure" petrolatum product with other lipids, or downstream effect of occlusion altering host microbiome
- This is the strongest single falsifier for the substrate ranking

**C6. The "tallow skincare" trend produces no measurable rise in clinical demodicosis incidence over 5–10 years.**
- Would falsify the strong-version prediction in §9 #7
- Could indicate substrate amplification has weaker effect size than this attempt estimates
- Possible alternative: tallow is associated with overall behavior cluster (less product use, less harsh cleansers) that compensates for the substrate effect

## §11. Honest uncertainty flags

Per v10 production-firing audit / v9.1 falsifier discipline, claims in this attempt that warrant explicit hedging:

1. **"Petrolatum is substrate-poor for Demodex" is inferred from chemistry, not directly tested.** Direct in-vitro substrate testing of petrolatum vs. Demodex in published literature is absent. The chemical inference (no ester bonds for lipase) is solid, but a direct experimental confirmation would strengthen the claim. *If an in-vitro study showed Demodex growth on petrolatum substrate, the §3 Tier C ranking would need revision.*

2. **Squalane-as-substrate is biochemically plausible but not directly demonstrated for Demodex.** *C. acnes* metabolizes squalene; whether Demodex lipase efficiently cleaves squalene/squalane bonds is not in the search-accessible literature. Tier S squalane placement is provisional.

3. **The "chapstick → perioral substrate extension" mechanism is operator-Claude synthesis.** It is not explicitly stated in the published Demodex literature in the form "chronic application of lipid lip products to perioral skin extends Demodex substrate." The component facts are documented; the synthesis is not. **Tier 1 evidence (single-operator inference)** per the v9.1 D1 replication tier framework. Promotion to Tier 2 would require either (a) explicit literature citation of the exact synthesis or (b) cross-session validation in a different POD case.

4. **The "instant relief" phenomenology is faster than the 2-week mite life cycle predicts.** Two reconciling explanations: (a) immediate sulfur+TTO contact disrupts mite cuticles within hours (acute mite kill, even if population decline is slower) reducing local inflammation rapidly; (b) the relief is partly attributable to sulfur's known anti-inflammatory effect independent of mite kill (sulfur is keratolytic + reduces local inflammation). Both probably contribute.

5. **The substrate-amplification mechanism may explain only a *subset* of chronic perioral cheilitis cases, not all of them.** Other mechanisms (contact dermatitis to lip-balm preservatives, atopic cheilitis, angular cheilitis from candida or staph, actinic cheilitis, factitial cheilitis from lip-licking) remain primary in their respective subsets. This attempt does not claim to replace the existing differential; it adds a missing mechanism for the Demodex-amplified subset.

6. **"Avoid oil-based cosmetics" is documented in current Demodex review literature** (PMC9961532) but the product-by-product substrate ranking in §3 is this attempt's synthesis. The high-level recommendation is sourced; the granular ranking is inference from chemistry + product composition data.

7. **Tallow trend impact is forward-prediction, not retrospective evidence.** Cannot be confirmed until enough time passes and dermatology incidence tracking accumulates.
