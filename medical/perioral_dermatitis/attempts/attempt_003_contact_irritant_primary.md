# attempt_003 — Contact Irritant as Primary Driver (Mountain 3)

## The claim

**Perioral dermatitis is fundamentally a chronic low-grade contact irritant dermatitis caused by repeated low-dose exposure to topical agents that transit the perioral zone: toothpaste ingredients (fluoride, sodium lauryl sulfate, cinnamaldehyde, mint oils), lip balms, cosmetics, and sunscreens.** Under this hypothesis, the steroid rebound cycle (Mountain 1) and Demodex overgrowth (Mountain 2) are downstream amplifiers, but the INITIATING insult is a persistent environmental contactant. Remove the contactant and POD resolves without any active treatment. The disease is iatrogenic in a different sense: it is a byproduct of modern dental and cosmetic products that deposit residues on perioral skin twice or more per day.

## Why consider this mountain

Mountain 1 and Mountain 2 both take for granted that SOMETHING initiated the inflammation, then explain how it becomes chronic. Neither explains the initial trigger in cases where there's no steroid exposure and no obvious Demodex perturbation. Mountain 3 offers a clean initiator hypothesis: **the trigger is the thing you do twice a day, every day, that you've stopped noticing.**

Observations that Mountain 3 explains better than the others:

- **Geographic distribution matches toothpaste dribble paths.** POD is concentrated around the mouth, sometimes extending to the nasolabial folds and chin. These are exactly the areas where residual toothpaste migrates as it foams during brushing and rinses off incompletely.
- **The clear zone at the vermillion border matches lip protection.** When toothpaste is on the lips during brushing, it physically displaces from the vermillion as the lips close and move. The immediately adjacent skin receives the displaced material. Simple fluid mechanics, not follicular density.
- **Pediatric POD rise since the 1990s coincides with pediatric fluoride toothpaste adoption.** Pre-1990s, children used non-fluoride pastes or water alone. Fluoride pediatric toothpastes with strong flavorings (cinnamon, bubblegum, mint) became standard in the 1990s.
- **Perioral predominance over other facial zones.** If POD were primarily Demodex-driven, it should distribute with pilosebaceous density, which is actually higher on the forehead and cheeks. Perioral predominance is unexplained under M2.
- **Female adult predominance matches cosmetic exposure.** Adult women use more lip products, facial cosmetics, and sunscreens near the mouth than men. The higher POD incidence in adult women tracks cosmetic exposure, not hormonal variation per se.
- **Case reports of resolution on product elimination alone.** Documented in the dermatology literature: POD resolving within weeks of switching to non-fluoride, SLS-free, non-flavored toothpaste with no other intervention.

## Evidence for specific contactants

### Fluoride
- Multiple case series document POD resolution on switching from fluoride to non-fluoride toothpaste
- Mechanism unclear — fluoride at toothpaste concentrations is not a classical inflammatory agent, but may:
  - Disrupt the lipid bilayer of stratum corneum through complexation with calcium
  - Shift perioral microbiome toward POD-favorable species
  - Act as a co-irritant with other ingredients rather than an irritant alone
- Confounded by fact that fluoride toothpastes differ from non-fluoride in other ways (flavoring, surfactants, abrasives)

### Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS)
- Known barrier disruptor — used as a positive control in irritant patch testing
- Present in most toothpastes as the foaming agent
- Concentration (1-2%) is high enough to cause transient barrier effects on direct contact
- Perioral skin receives SLS twice daily during brushing
- SLS-free toothpastes exist and are documented to reduce oral ulcers and perioral irritation in susceptible patients

### Cinnamaldehyde (cinnamic aldehyde)
- Primary flavoring in many "cinnamon" toothpastes and mouthwashes
- Known contact sensitizer in patch testing
- Case reports of cinnamon-induced cheilitis, perioral dermatitis, and stomatitis
- Acts via both irritant and allergic mechanisms depending on individual sensitivity

### Mint oils (menthol, eugenol, carvone)
- Present in most adult toothpastes
- TRPM8 agonists — activate cold-sensing neurons, can cause neurogenic inflammation in susceptible patients
- Case reports of mint-induced contact stomatitis extending to perioral skin

### Cosmetic and sunscreen ingredients
- Octinoxate, oxybenzone (chemical UV filters) — documented contact allergens
- Lip balm active ingredients (camphor, menthol, salicylic acid) — can irritate surrounding skin if spread
- Long-wear lipstick formulations with silicones and film-formers — extend contact duration

## The mechanism under this hypothesis

1. **Chronic low-grade contact exposure.** A specific contactant (toothpaste component, cosmetic, lip product) is applied to perioral skin at least once daily.
2. **Sub-clinical inflammation.** The exposure is below the threshold for acute contact dermatitis but above the threshold for local cytokine induction. TNF-α, IL-1α, and IL-6 are produced locally without visible inflammation.
3. **Barrier compromise accumulates.** Chronic irritant exposure reduces stratum corneum integrity. Transepidermal water loss increases. The perioral epidermis becomes more permeable to subsequent exposures.
4. **Threshold crossed.** At some point — stress, hormone shift, change in product, increased exposure frequency — the accumulated barrier compromise produces visible inflammation. POD appears.
5. **Downstream amplification.** Whatever the primary mechanism (contact irritant), secondary mechanisms take over: Demodex density rises in the now-hospitable follicular environment, topical steroids get prescribed and initiate the rebound cycle, cathelicidin production amplifies the inflammation.
6. **Clinical picture.** By the time the patient presents, all three mountains are active. Removing only one (steroids, or Demodex, or contactants) partially improves the picture but doesn't fully resolve it.

Under this model, the right treatment is **triple removal**: stop the contactant, stop any steroids, and reduce Demodex density. The current standard (metronidazole + stop steroids) addresses two but not the third.

## How this explains the clear zone

Mountain 3's clear zone explanation: during toothpaste application, the lips are actively involved in the brushing action. The vermillion is coated with toothpaste for the duration of brushing and then rinsed. The rinse water + saliva carries residue AWAY from the vermillion and onto the immediately surrounding skin, where it dries. The vermillion gets repeated high-dose exposure that is then rinsed; the surrounding skin gets repeated moderate-dose exposure that remains. The two zones differ in exposure pattern, not in anatomical susceptibility.

**Testable prediction:** The clear zone should correlate with lip mobility and saliva flow, not with follicular density. Patients with reduced lip mobility (e.g., after facial surgery, certain neuromuscular conditions) should have a different clear zone geometry.

## How this explains the rise in pediatric POD since the 1990s

- **1980s:** Children used low-fluoride pastes (250 ppm) with minimal flavoring, or water only. Parents often used adult toothpaste on children sparingly.
- **1990s:** Pediatric fluoride toothpastes with strong flavorings (bubblegum, cinnamon, fruit) became standard. Children were told to brush twice daily.
- **2000s:** Adult fluoride concentration (1000-1500 ppm) became common in pediatric pastes for children over 6.
- **Same period:** Pediatric POD incidence rose. The two trends are correlated. Mountain 1 (iatrogenic steroids) is also correlated, so this isn't conclusive — but Mountain 3 gives a direct causal mechanism without requiring steroid exposure.

## The anti-problem

What would a counterexample look like?

- **POD in a patient with no cosmetic, lip product, sunscreen, or fluoride toothpaste exposure.** If such cases exist and are substantial in number, Mountain 3 is incomplete.
- **POD resolution under a treatment that removes all irritant contactants WITHOUT also addressing steroids or Demodex.** If pure contactant elimination produces rapid cure, Mountain 3 is strongly supported. Case reports exist; systematic studies do not.
- **POD non-resolution despite strict contactant elimination.** Would indicate Mountain 3 is at best a cofactor.
- **POD incidence in populations with no toothpaste access.** Does POD exist in populations that have never used fluoride toothpaste? If yes at comparable rates, Mountain 3 is weakened.

## Treatment predictions if Mountain 3 is correct

1. **Strict contactant elimination for 2-4 weeks should resolve >50% of POD cases without any other intervention.** Specifically: switch to a non-fluoride, SLS-free, unflavored toothpaste (or plain water brushing); eliminate all lip balms, cosmetics, and sunscreens from the perioral area; use only plain water to clean the face.
2. **Patch testing POD patients against common toothpaste ingredients should show positive reactions at a higher rate than controls.** This has been done in small case series and found cinnamaldehyde sensitivity in a subset, but not systematically in larger populations.
3. **Products specifically marketed as "sensitive skin" (SLS-free, fragrance-free, low-flavor) should reduce POD recurrence compared to standard products.** Testable with a simple before/after switching trial.
4. **POD incidence should correlate inversely with use of "natural" or "traditional" oral hygiene products** (miswak, baking soda, salt) that lack modern irritants. This is observable in cross-cultural epidemiology.

## Where M1, M2, and M3 agree and diverge

**All three agree:**
- Metronidazole is effective because it has anti-inflammatory + anti-Demodex + possibly anti-microbial effects
- Topical corticosteroids worsen chronic POD
- Caregiver education matters
- Resolution takes weeks, not days

**M1 vs M2:**
- M1 predicts pimecrolimus monotherapy cures POD; M2 predicts it's insufficient without Demodex reduction

**M1 vs M3:**
- M1 predicts zero-therapy (stop steroids only) cures POD; M3 predicts it's insufficient without contactant elimination
- M3 predicts that patients with no steroid history should still develop POD if they have contactant exposure; M1 predicts these cases should be rare

**M2 vs M3:**
- M2 predicts topical ivermectin monotherapy cures POD; M3 predicts it's insufficient without contactant elimination
- M3 predicts POD distribution matches contactant deposition zones (toothpaste dribble); M2 predicts distribution matches follicular density

**All three together suggest:** The correct treatment protocol is simultaneous **triple removal**: (1) stop topical corticosteroids, (2) reduce Demodex density with metronidazole or ivermectin, (3) eliminate contact irritants (non-fluoride/SLS-free/unflavored toothpaste, no perioral cosmetics for the duration of treatment). This triple removal is stricter than the current standard of care and would predict higher resolution rates.

## Sky bridges

- **Cheilitis and chronic actinic cheilitis** — chronic lip inflammation from irritant/UV exposure. Overlaps mechanistically with contact-irritant POD at the vermillion.
- **Contact dermatitis (broad)** — POD under Mountain 3 is a specific case of chronic low-grade contact dermatitis. The general lessons about exposure accumulation and threshold crossing apply.
- **Dental product formulation** — the rise in pediatric POD is an unintended consequence of pediatric dental public health campaigns promoting fluoride toothpaste twice daily. This is a case study of a well-intentioned public health intervention producing a new disease.
- **Microbiome disruption from oral care** — fluoride and SLS alter the oral and perioral microbiome. Connects to broader microbiome-skin disease questions.

## Integration with M1 and M2 — Mountain 4 sketch

The three-mountain synthesis:

**POD is a multi-hit disease.** Genetic susceptibility (filaggrin variants? innate immunity variants?) sets a baseline threshold. Chronic contact irritants (M3) erode the threshold over time. Demodex density (M2) expands into the compromised environment and triggers cathelicidin-mediated inflammation. Topical corticosteroids (M1) then amplify the condition by allowing further Demodex expansion and producing rebound inflammation on withdrawal. Each hit alone is insufficient; in combination they produce clinical POD.

Under this integration, the effective treatment must remove the active amplifiers (steroids, Demodex) while also eliminating the erosion source (contactants). It must ALSO address the genetic susceptibility if possible — unclear what this means clinically, but filaggrin-supplementing moisturizers (ceramides) are a candidate.

This is the Mountain 4 sketch: the disease as a multi-hit process where no single mechanism dominates. Attempt_004 will explore the genetic susceptibility axis if it has distinct content; otherwise attempt_004 will be the neurogenic/cathelicidin axis.

## Status

Mountain 3 has compelling epidemiological and case-report support but lacks controlled trials. The distribution pattern (perioral predominance matching toothpaste zones), the pediatric POD rise correlation, and the documented case responses to contactant elimination together make it a credible primary-driver hypothesis. Pure contactant elimination as monotherapy has not been tested in controlled fashion. If true, it would predict that a fraction of POD cases are entirely preventable and treatable by product choice alone, without any pharmacological intervention.

Next: attempt_004 will examine the neurogenic inflammation / cathelicidin axis as Mountain 4, which may be the molecular substrate that all three upstream mountains converge into.
