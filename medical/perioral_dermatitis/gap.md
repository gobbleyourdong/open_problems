# Perioral Dermatitis — Mechanistic Gaps

## The core gap: why does POD have a clear zone?

The most diagnostic feature of POD is a ~5mm clear zone of unaffected skin directly bordering the vermillion. This zone spares no matter how severe the surrounding inflammation gets. **No mechanism explains this.**

Candidate hypotheses:
1. **Reduced follicular density:** the perilabial zone has fewer pilosebaceous units than the surrounding skin. If POD is a follicular disease, no follicles → no disease. Testable by histology.
2. **Saliva-mediated protection:** constant low-level saliva contact keeps the zone microbially distinct or barrier-altered in a protective way.
3. **Innervation difference:** TRPV1 distribution differs at the lip border; neurogenic inflammation cannot be sustained there.
4. **Mechanical friction:** the continuous movement of the lip edge physically clears Demodex or inflammatory debris.

None of these have been tested in POD specifically.

## Gap: Demodex — driver or bystander?

Demodex folliculorum overgrowth is observed in POD and rosacea but not proven causal. Tests to resolve:
- Does topical ivermectin (anti-Demodex) resolve POD as quickly as topical metronidazole?
- Does Demodex density at baseline predict POD severity?
- Do POD-resolved patients have lower Demodex density than POD-active patients?

## Gap: why fluoride?

Case series document POD resolution on switching to non-fluoride toothpaste, without any other change. The mechanism is unclear — fluoride is not a known inflammatory agent at toothpaste concentrations. Candidate explanations:
- Fluoride + sodium lauryl sulfate combination disrupts the lipid barrier
- Fluoride shifts the perioral microbiome toward POD-favorable species
- Flavoring agents (cinnamaldehyde, mint) in fluoride toothpastes are the real trigger, and fluoride correlates by coincidence
- Trace contaminants in fluoride formulations

No controlled studies exist. The case reports are compelling enough to justify a 2-week trial of non-fluoride toothpaste in refractory POD, but the mechanism remains undefined.

## Gap: the rebound mechanism

When topical steroids are withdrawn, the rebound flare peaks around day 7-14 and resolves by week 4-6. The molecular dynamics are unclear:
- Is the rebound primarily vascular (vasodilation after chronic vasoconstriction)?
- Immune (rebound cytokine production after chronic suppression)?
- Microbial (overgrowth during immunosuppression that takes time to clear)?
- Barrier (atrophic stratum corneum repairing)?

Each would predict different adjunct strategies. If vascular: topical vasoconstrictor (brimonidine?) during withdrawal. If microbial: topical antibiotic during withdrawal. If barrier: ceramide-rich moisturizer. In practice, clinicians tell patients to "just tough it out" because the mechanism is unclear.

## Gap: why children specifically?

Pediatric POD has been increasing in frequency since the 1990s, paralleling increased use of topical corticosteroids for eczema and inhaled corticosteroids for asthma (which can deposit around the mouth). The epidemiology is clear; the mechanism of greater child susceptibility is not:
- Thinner facial epidermis → more steroid absorption per unit area?
- Immature skin microbiome more vulnerable to perturbation?
- Higher baseline Demodex acquisition patterns?

## Gap: relationship to rosacea

POD and rosacea share many features (follicular, flushing-prone, Demodex-associated, responsive to metronidazole). They may be the same disease at different ages or different severity levels. Or they may be distinct diseases with overlapping features. No molecular distinction has been established.

## Testable predictions

If the **steroid rebound cycle** is the dominant perpetuation mechanism:
- Incidence should correlate with topical steroid prescription rates in the local population
- The rising pediatric incidence since the 1990s should match the rise of pediatric topical steroid use
- Complete steroid cessation should produce cure in >80% of cases within 6 weeks

If **Demodex overgrowth** is the driver:
- Anti-Demodex monotherapy (topical ivermectin) should be as effective as metronidazole
- Demodex density should correlate with POD severity across patients

If **fluoride** is a contributor:
- Non-fluoride toothpaste trial should produce improvement in >30% of cases
- The correlation with fluoride concentration should be dose-dependent

## Status

POD is treatable but not mechanistically understood. The clinical algorithm works (stop steroids, use metronidazole, tolerate rebound). The why remains open.
