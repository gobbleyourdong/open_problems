# Perioral Dermatitis — THE WALL

## Classification Reminder

POD is **NOT** a CVB disease. It is included in this repo as part of the broader medical expansion beyond the CVB campaign. The wall for POD is structurally different from the walls facing CVB-linked diseases: it is not a mechanistic wall (the mechanism is reasonably well understood), it is not a drug development wall (effective drugs exist), and it is not a diagnostic technology wall (POD is visually distinctive once you know the pattern).

## The Four-Mountain Integrated Model (Phase 3 Convergence)

After four attempts climbing the mountains:

**M1 — Iatrogenic steroid rebound cycle** (`attempts/attempt_001`)
**M2 — Demodex folliculorum primary driver** (`attempts/attempt_002`)
**M3 — Contact irritants from toothpaste and cosmetics** (`attempts/attempt_003`)
**M4 — Cathelicidin LL-37 molecular substrate** (`attempts/attempt_004`)

These do not compete; they compose. M1-M3 are upstream trigger hypotheses ("what starts POD"). M4 is a substrate hypothesis ("what POD IS at the molecular level"). The integrated model:

This multi-hit model explains:
- Why single-cause hypotheses leave unexplained case clusters
- Why multiple different treatments (anti-Demodex, anti-inflammatory, sub-antimicrobial doxycycline) all partially work
- Why the standard of care (metronidazole + stop steroids) works in most cases but fails in some
- The dose-dependent concern with Vitamin D (upregulates cathelicidin via VDR)
- The clear zone at the vermillion border (all four mountains offer partial explanations)

## The Wall

**The wall for POD is NOT a single wall.** There are at least two distinct wall types depending on the case variant:

### Wall Type A — Steroid Rebound Compliance (textbook POD)

When a topical steroid is in the rotation, the wall is caregiver compliance with the 1-3 week rebound flare after steroid withdrawal. This is the classical wall described in dermatology literature.

### Wall Type B — Environmental Trigger Identification (contactant-driven POD)

When no steroid is involved, the wall is **finding and eliminating the environmental contactant**. The drugs (metronidazole, pimecrolimus) reduce symptoms, but the rash recurs as long as the trigger is present. This wall is harder than Wall A because the trigger is often unidentified and embedded in routine (toothpaste, school environment, oral habits, mechanical rubbing).

**Diagnostic signal distinguishing A from B:**
- Wall A: rash is constant regardless of environment, worsens on steroid withdrawal
- Wall B: rash has diurnal variation (morning best, evening worst), environment-dependent (clears at home, returns at school), mechanically amplified by habitual rubbing

**In cases where both are active** (steroid rebound + environmental trigger), both walls must be addressed simultaneously. Removing the steroid without finding the trigger produces incomplete resolution. Finding the trigger without removing the steroid produces relapse.

### Wall Type B — why it is actually harder

Wall A has a known trajectory: stop steroid → tolerate 1-3 weeks → resolution. The caregiver just has to endure.

Wall B has no predetermined trajectory because the trigger may be:
- A toothpaste ingredient (easy to swap)
- A school-specific product (harder — requires investigation, teacher cooperation)
- A behavioral habit (rubbing, toy-mouthing — requires behavioral modification in a child)
- Multiple simultaneous triggers (requires elimination protocol, one variable at a time)

Wall B is a detective problem, not an endurance problem. It requires the caregiver and clinician to elicit the right questions (diurnal pattern? environment correlation? mechanical habits? oral behaviors?) and then systematically eliminate candidates. Most clinicians do not ask these questions because the textbook presents POD as a steroid-rebound disease (Wall A) and does not cover Wall B.

### Why this is actually the wall

The integrated 4-mountain treatment protocol is:

1. Stop all topical corticosteroids on the face
2. Eliminate contact irritants (non-fluoride SLS-free toothpaste, no perioral cosmetics during treatment)
3. Reduce Demodex (topical metronidazole cream or topical ivermectin)
4. For moderate-severe cases: add sub-antimicrobial doxycycline (adults) or azithromycin (children under 8)
5. Moderate vitamin D — standard dose OK, avoid high dose during active flare
6. UV protection, avoid heat and spicy food
7. **Tolerate the rebound flare** — 1-3 weeks of visible worsening after starting
8. Caregiver education

Steps 1-6 are medical decisions. Step 8 is informational. **Step 7 is the wall.**

The rebound flare presents as:
- Increased erythema compared to baseline
- Worse papular eruption
- Possible pustule formation
- Burning or itching that wasn't there before
- Child visibly distressed, asks why the face is worse

A caregiver watching their child's face worsen over 1-3 weeks has exactly one coping move if they don't understand the rebound mechanism: re-apply whatever cream was helping, which is usually the steroid they just stopped. This action feels like the right thing to do. It is not. It resets the loop.

### Why the wall is stable

The caregiver-compliance wall has all the features of a persistent wall:

1. **Self-reinforcing** — each failure to tolerate rebound produces another steroid exposure, which produces another rebound cycle, which produces another failure.
2. **Selection effect** — dermatologists see POD patients AFTER the cycle has been running for weeks or months. Early catches never reach the specialist because home management resolves them in the first few steroid applications.
3. **Counter-intuitive physical signals** — "worse is better" is a hard sell to a parent watching a child cry about their face.
4. **Missing middle-layer support** — pediatricians and family doctors often don't recognize POD, prescribe steroids reflexively for "facial rash," and unknowingly initiate or reset the loop.
5. **Asymmetric cost** — the cost of applying a steroid and seeing improvement is low and immediate. The cost of NOT applying is high and delayed. Behavioral economics alone would predict steroid reapplication.

## The Expected Effect of the Integrated Protocol

If the integrated 4-mountain protocol is followed correctly, with the caregiver successfully tolerating the rebound flare:

- **Week 0-1 after starting:** visible worsening (rebound + mite die-off antigen release + contactant withdrawal dysbiosis). This is the hardest week.
- **Week 1-2:** plateau of worsening. The rash is bad but not getting worse. Caregivers often give up here.
- **Week 2-3:** first signs of improvement. Papules reduce. Pustules stop forming.
- **Week 3-4:** rapid improvement. Erythema fades. Clear zone expands.
- **Week 4-6:** complete or near-complete resolution.
- **After week 6:** maintenance phase. Avoid steroid reintroduction. Continue non-fluoride toothpaste if trigger was identified. Occasional topical metronidazole for flare prevention if recurrence occurs.

**Resolution rate with full protocol compliance:** estimated >90% based on the mechanistic coverage (all four mountains addressed simultaneously).
**Resolution rate with typical compliance:** estimated 50-70% because most caregivers break at week 1-2 and reapply steroids.
**Resolution rate with no intervention beyond stopping steroids:** ~60% in 6 weeks but with higher recurrence rate because contact triggers remain.

## The Protocol Addition for POD Patients

### Written instructions to give caregivers (to overcome the wall)

A caregiver handed a prescription for metronidazole cream and told "stop the steroid" will fail. The protocol addition is the **explicit written rebound warning** that must accompany any POD diagnosis:

```
IMPORTANT: What to expect over the next 3 weeks.

The rash will get WORSE before it gets better. This is not the treatment
failing — it is the treatment working. Here is what will happen:

Week 1: The rash looks worse than when you started. Redness increases.
New papules may appear. You will want to put the old steroid cream back
on. DO NOT DO THIS. Putting the steroid back on will restart the cycle
and the rash will not resolve.

Week 2: The rash is at its worst. Your child may be uncomfortable. You
will be tempted to call the doctor and ask for a stronger treatment.
This is normal. The treatment is still working. Continue the prescribed
cream.

Week 3: First signs of improvement. The redness begins to fade. New
papules stop forming. You will see the clear zone around the lips
expanding.

Week 4-6: Rapid improvement. The rash resolves.

During this time:
- Use only the prescribed metronidazole cream (or ivermectin), not the
  steroid cream, not any cream from before
- Wash the face with plain water or Cetaphil only
- Use a non-fluoride, SLS-free, unflavored toothpaste
- No cosmetics, lip balms, or sunscreens on the affected skin
- Keep doing the vitamin D and probiotics if you were already doing them
- Moderate sun exposure only

If the rash is dramatically worse or your child has a fever, call the
doctor. For all other worsening during the first 2 weeks, continue the
protocol.
```

This written reinforcement is the wall-breaking intervention. Without it, the protocol fails at the caregiver-compliance layer regardless of mechanistic correctness.

## What the Wall Tells Us About POD

The caregiver-compliance wall is structurally similar to walls in other conditions where short-term symptom suppression perpetuates long-term disease:

- **PPI rebound in acid reflux** — stopping PPIs causes rebound hyperacidity, patients reinitiate PPIs, chronicity develops
- **Decongestant rebound (rhinitis medicamentosa)** — same pattern with oxymetazoline nasal sprays
- **Opioid-induced hyperalgesia** — same pattern with chronic opioid pain management
- **Topical steroid withdrawal in atopic dermatitis** — related but biologically distinct

In all these cases, the wall is not biological — it is behavioral. The biology of the disease is understood. The drugs to treat it exist. What doesn't exist is a reliable mechanism for getting humans to tolerate the withdrawal transition.

## What Would Break the Wall

Possible approaches to lowering the compliance wall:

1. **Visual progression calendars** — show caregivers pictures of the expected rebound trajectory so they can map their child's progress against a documented timeline
2. **Adjunct symptomatic treatment during rebound** — a non-steroid anti-inflammatory (pimecrolimus or tacrolimus) that partially masks the rebound without resetting the loop. This is already in the protocol as an option.
3. **Brief oral course concurrent with topical withdrawal** — azithromycin or erythromycin oral for 5-7 days can accelerate improvement and reduce the peak rebound severity, making the transition tolerable
4. **Caregiver support infrastructure** — text message reminders, daily photo check-ins, direct access to the prescribing clinician during the first 2 weeks
5. **Earlier diagnosis** — catching POD before the steroid-amplified chronic phase means the rebound flare is less severe

None of these approaches currently have randomized trial evidence in POD specifically. They are derived from related conditions with similar compliance walls.

## Summary

**The biology is solved. The wall is behavioral.**

POD as a medical research problem is essentially closed — the 4-mountain integrated model explains the mechanism, and the multi-target protocol works if applied. POD as a public health problem remains open because the protocol requires tolerating 1-3 weeks of visible worsening, and this requirement fails reliably at the caregiver-compliance layer.

The research question for POD is no longer "what causes it?" or "what treats it?" The research question is "how do we get caregivers and primary-care clinicians to recognize it and correctly apply the treatment in time?"

## Phase 4 Dysbiosis Framework Import — 2026-04-12

### Steroid Rebound Explained at Molecular Level

The compliance wall (Wall Type A) is maintained partly because caregivers lack a mechanistic
model for the rebound. The dysbiosis framework provides this model:

```
Topical steroid (chronic) → glucocorticoid receptor (GR) → NF-κB suppressed → KLK5 activity ↓ →
    LL-37 production ↓ → NLRP3 priming ↓ → visible inflammation suppressed (apparent improvement)
    SIMULTANEOUSLY: local immune suppression → Demodex proliferates (standard known mechanism)

Steroid withdrawal →
    1. KLK5 REBOUND: GR suppression lifted → KLK5 transcription + activity surges above baseline
       (mTORC1 disinhibited → KLK5 mRNA ↑) → LL-37 ↑↑ → TLR2 + TRPV1 simultaneous activation
    2. NLRP3 DISINHIBITION: GR → NF-κB inhibition was suppressing NLRP3 priming; now NF-κB
       is active again → NLRP3 primed → any K+ efflux signal (from Demodex viroporin-like activity
       or from keratinocyte stress) → NLRP3 activates → IL-1β burst → acute inflammatory response
    3. Demodex IMMUNE RESPONSE: suppressed T cell response now re-activates against expanded
       Demodex population → TLR2 primed by B. oleronius + Demodex antigenic load

    TOTAL: three simultaneous inflammatory mechanisms re-activate → the rebound
```

**Clinical use of this mechanism for caregiver communication:**
The rebound is NOT an allergic reaction or a worsening infection. It is the PREDICTABLE consequence
of three inflammatory brakes being released simultaneously. The underlying disease is now VISIBLE
for the first time (steroids were masking it). Treatment (metronidazole, ivermectin, doxycycline)
addresses the actual causes; the inflammation will subside as treatment takes effect.

### TRPV1: The Mechanism of POD Burning and Stinging

The burning/stinging symptom of POD (and especially its rebound) is TRPV1-mediated:
- Chronic steroid → TRPV1 expression suppressed (GR → TRPV1 transcription ↓ at ARE)
- Steroid withdrawal → TRPV1 upregulated (post-suppression rebound)
- LL-37 rebound (KLK5 rebound above) → direct TRPV1 agonist (Buhl 2017 J Allergy Clin Immunol)
- Substance P (neuropeptide, elevated under stress + perioral sensitization) → TRPV1 threshold ↓
→ TRPV1 fires at body temperature → burning without visible cause

**Practical implication:** The burning/stinging in POD rebound is not bacterial infection pain — it
is neurogenic TRPV1 activation. It responds to TRPV1 desensitization (not to oral antibiotics
given for burning alone). Capsaicin 0.025% (BID × 6 weeks, after the acute rebound has resolved)
desensitizes TRPV1 and reduces burning recurrence. Do NOT apply during active rebound (paradoxical
worsening from initial TRPV1 agonism before desensitization).

See `../dysbiosis/numerics/run_015_trpv1_neurogenic_flushing.md`.

### Zinc Deficiency as POD Risk Factor

Zinc deficiency → KLK5 hyperactive (Zn²⁺ inhibits KLK5 serine protease at histidine residues).
In zinc-deficient patients, the KLK5/LL-37 loop threshold is chronically lower → easier to
initiate POD from any upstream trigger (steroid, Demodex, contactant).

**Clinical note:** Acrodermatitis enteropathica (severe zinc deficiency) produces perioral lesions
clinically similar to POD — the perioral location of zinc deficiency dermatosis is not coincidental.
In adults with refractory POD: check serum zinc. Zinc glycinate 25-30mg/day if deficient.

See `../dysbiosis/numerics/run_024_zinc_deficiency.md`.

### Loop 4 Topicals for Perioral Skin (Sebaceous NLRP3)

Sebocytes in perioral pilosebaceous units express the full NLRP3 inflammasome. UV + Malassezia
lipases → squalene peroxide → sebocyte NLRP3 → IL-1β in perioral units. In POD that persists
after Demodex treatment (ivermectin) + contactant removal + steroid withdrawal:

- **Topical niacinamide 4% cream BID:** NAD+→SIRT1→NLRP3 K496 deacetylation in sebocytes;
  also reduces KLK5 indirectly (NF-κB blunted → less KLK5 priming). Safe for perioral skin.
- **SPF 30+ daily:** UV is the primary squalene peroxidation driver for Loop 4.
- Avoid topical vitamin E immediately around mouth (can cause contact dermatitis in perioral zone
  in sensitive patients; test on arm first).

See `../dysbiosis/numerics/run_025_sebaceous_nlrp3_local_loop.md`.

### C. acnes and Loop 4 in Perioral Context (run_038 import)

**POD + acne comorbidity — shared Loop 4 mechanism:**
Perioral pilosebaceous units are colonized by BOTH Malassezia and C. acnes. In POD patients
with concurrent acne-prone skin, BOTH Loop 4 triggers are active simultaneously:
- C. acnes TLR2 → NF-κB → NLRP3 priming (Signal 1)
- C. acnes porphyrins + UV/blue light → squalene-OOH (Signal 2)
- Malassezia lipase → squalene-OOH (Signal 2, second source)

**Blue light devices — contraindicated in POD/combined phenotype:**
Blue light (415nm) phototherapy for acne excites C. acnes porphyrins → singlet oxygen → squalene-
OOH burst → transient NLRP3 flare. In POD, NLRP3 is already sensitized (steroid rebound phase,
Demodex, mTORC1 rebound). Blue light therapy in the perioral area → squalene-OOH burst →
NLRP3 spike → worse POD flare. Contraindicated in perioral area, especially during POD active phase.

**If blue light cannot be avoided:**
Pre-treat perioral area with niacinamide 4% cream + tocopherol oil immediately before blue light
session (not just SPF — SPF blocks UV but blue light is VISIBLE, not UV; standard SPF does not
protect against 415nm blue light squalene-OOH). Topical antioxidant application is the only
available squalene-OOH scavenger option.

See `../dysbiosis/numerics/run_038_cutibacterium_acnes_loop4.md`.

### Capsaicin Desensitization for POD Burning/Stinging (run_042 import)

**TRPV1/SP mechanism in POD burning:**
POD burning and stinging symptoms are driven by TRPV1+ nociceptor sensitization (as previously
noted in run_025 context: TRPV1 → SP + CGRP). Capsaicin desensitization 0.025% × 4 weeks
depletes SP from TRPV1+ nerve endings → burning/stinging ↓ + NK1R-mediated mast cell activation
↓ → neurogenic component of POD resolved.

**Important POD-specific caveat:**
Do NOT start capsaicin during active flare or in the steroid rebound phase (skin is hypersensitive;
initial burning will be severe and patient will stop treatment). Timeline:
1. Weeks 1-4: complete steroid withdrawal + topical azelaic acid
2. Weeks 4-8: acute flare resolved, skin barrier partially recovered
3. Week 8+: introduce capsaicin 0.025% at night to a SMALL test area (forehead/chin margin —
   not full perioral). If tolerated, expand to full perioral zone over 2 weeks.

**Additional benefit: SP → NK1R → KLK5 reduction:**
SP depletion → less NK1R activation on keratinocytes → less KLK5 transcription → lower
LL-37 → less Loop 1 KLK5 self-amplification. Addresses the Loop 1 neurogenic arm in POD.

See `../dysbiosis/numerics/run_042_mast_cell_stabilization.md`.
