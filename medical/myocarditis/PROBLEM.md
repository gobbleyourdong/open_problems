# Viral Myocarditis (Coxsackievirus B)

## The Problem
Inflammation of the myocardium caused by CVB infection (primarily B3). Leading cause of sudden cardiac death in young adults. Acute phase can resolve or progress to chronic dilated cardiomyopathy. No antiviral treatment exists.

## Status: UNSOLVED

## Key Biology

| Component | What | Role |
|-----------|------|------|
| Cardiomyocytes | Heart muscle cells | Targets of CVB infection and immune attack |
| CVB3 | Primary causative serotype | Direct cytolysis + triggers autoimmunity |
| 2A protease | Viral protease | Cleaves dystrophin in cardiomyocytes → sarcolemma disruption |
| Dystrophin | Cytoskeletal protein | Maintains cardiomyocyte structural integrity |
| CAR (coxsackievirus-adenovirus receptor) | Cell surface receptor | CVB entry point, concentrated at intercalated discs |
| TD mutants | 5'-terminally deleted CVB | Persistent low-grade infection in myocardium |
| Molecular mimicry | Cardiac myosin ↔ viral epitopes | Triggers autoimmune attack on uninfected cardiomyocytes |

## The Disease Cascade
1. CVB enters via GI tract (fecal-oral)
2. Viremia → reaches myocardium via CAR receptor at intercalated discs
3. **Phase 1 (acute, days 1-7)**: Direct viral cytolysis of cardiomyocytes
4. **Phase 2 (subacute, weeks 1-4)**: Immune response — T cells, NK cells, macrophages infiltrate
5. **Phase 3 (chronic)**: Viral clearance OR persistence as TD mutants
6. If persistent: 2A protease cleaves dystrophin → progressive structural damage
7. Autoimmune component: molecular mimicry → anti-cardiac myosin antibodies
8. End stage: dilated cardiomyopathy, heart failure, transplant or death

## Connection to T1DM
- **Same virus**, same persistence mechanism (TD mutants)
- Same 2A/3C proteases doing damage (dystrophin in heart, beta cells in pancreas)
- Same autoimmune trigger (molecular mimicry)
- **Any antiviral protocol that clears CVB from pancreas should clear it from heart**
- T1DM patients should be screened for subclinical myocarditis

## Anti-Problem
"What do patients who clear CVB from myocardium without developing DCM look like?"
These patients exist — most CVB myocarditis resolves. Study the immune response that succeeds.
