# Pericarditis — Anti-Problem

## The Question
"Why do 70% of pericarditis patients NOT recur? What do they have that the 30% who recur lack?"

## The Simplest Anti-Problem in the Campaign

Unlike T1DM (complex multi-hit), myocarditis (multiple mechanisms), or ME/CFS (systemic), pericarditis has a CLEAN binary:
- 70% clear → no recurrence
- 30% don't clear → recurrence

**Same disease, same treatment, different outcome. What's different about the 30%?**

## Hypotheses

### H1: Higher initial viral load
- More CVB at start → more TD mutant generation events → higher chance of persistence
- The 30% who recur had a worse initial infection
- **Testable**: CVB PCR viral load at first episode (prospective study)

### H2: Weaker IFN response
- Genetic polymorphisms in IFN pathway (IRF7, IFNAR, MDA5)
- The 30% can suppress inflammation (colchicine works) but can't clear virus
- **Testable**: IFN-α levels during acute episode, genotyping

### H3: NK cell dysfunction
- Impaired NK cytotoxicity → can't kill CVB-infected pericardial cells
- Virus persists behind the colchicine anti-inflammatory screen
- **Testable**: NK cytotoxicity assay at first episode

### H4: Gut-immune axis
- Low butyrate → low Tregs → poor immune regulation → persistence
- The 30% have gut dysbiosis as a cofactor
- **Testable**: stool microbiome + butyrate levels

### H5: Colchicine masks but doesn't cure
- Colchicine is so effective at suppressing NLRP3 that the immune system never fully engages
- The 70% who clear do so DESPITE colchicine, not because of it
- The 30% who rely entirely on colchicine for symptom control never mount clearing response
- **Testable**: compare clearance rates in treated vs untreated (ethical concern — can't withhold treatment)

## The Anti-Problem Prediction

The 70% who don't recur are accidental "resolvers" — their immune system cleared CVB during the colchicine-suppressed window. The 30% who recur are "non-resolvers" — colchicine bought time but the immune system wasn't strong enough to finish the job.

**Adding fluoxetine tips the 30% into resolvers** by reducing viral load pharmacologically, filling the gap that their immune system couldn't.

## The Perfect Natural Experiment

Recurrent pericarditis patients who happen to be prescribed fluoxetine for depression:
- Do they have lower recurrence rates than non-SSRI pericarditis patients?
- Retrospective database study: ICD codes for recurrent pericarditis + SSRI prescription
- If fluoxetine-prescribed patients have fewer recurrences: strong signal for antiviral effect
- **This study could be done TOMORROW with existing claims databases.**

## Study: The 70/30 Profiling Study

### Design
- Prospective cohort of first-episode pericarditis patients
- At presentation, collect: CVB viral load, IFN-α, NK cytotoxicity, Treg %, stool butyrate
- Follow for 18 months
- Compare profiles of those who recur vs those who don't

### Expected findings
The 30% who recur will have ≥2 of:
- Higher CVB viral load at presentation
- Lower IFN-α response
- Lower NK cytotoxicity
- Lower stool butyrate / Treg frequency

### Implication
If the profiling works: you can PREDICT at first episode who will recur. Those predicted to recur get fluoxetine added from the start. Personalized prevention.
