# Attempt 003: Pleurodynia as T1DM/ME/CFS Prevention Window — The Sentinel Disease Strategy

## What Pleurodynia Actually Is

Bornholm disease (epidemic pleurodynia) is sudden-onset severe chest or upper abdominal pain from CVB infection of intercostal or epigastric muscles. It lasts 2-3 days, resolves spontaneously, and is the most RECOGNIZABLE CVB infection in clinical practice.

CVB serotype: primarily CVB5 (myotropic), sometimes CVB4 (diabetogenic).

It is also the MOST ACTIONABLE CVB infection in the campaign — because it is:
1. Acutely symptomatic (patient presents to physician)
2. Clinically identifiable (characteristic pain pattern + season)
3. Confirmed by serology at presentation
4. A signal that CVB is actively circulating in this patient

## The Sentinel Hypothesis

Pleurodynia is not an end-stage disease — it's a visible marker of a CVB infection that simultaneously infects multiple organs. The intercostal muscles show the pain; the islets, myocardium, and CNS are silently seeded.

```
CVB viremia event (summer/fall):
  Intercostal muscles:  Acute infection → pain → symptoms → PHYSICIAN VISIT ← YOU ARE HERE
  Islets (beta cells):  Silent seeding → TD mutants → FOXP1 suppressed → T1DM in 2-5yr
  Cardiomyocytes:       Silent seeding → TD mutants → DMD cleaved → DCM in 5-10yr
  Neurons/CNS:          Silent seeding → TD mutants → ME/CFS in months-1yr
  Testes (if male):     Silent seeding → orchitis (usually subclinical)
```

**The physician visit for pleurodynia is the ONLY routine medical contact that occurs during the CVB infection window.** After the pleurodynia resolves, the patient goes home and has no further medical contact — until T1DM, DCM, or ME/CFS presents years later.

## The LAMP2 Context for Intercostal Muscle

Skeletal muscle LAMP2 baseline ≈ 0.7× average. After CVB: κ_effective_muscle = 0.7/2.7 ≈ 0.26.

Intercostal muscle clearance (unified model): 7 months without trehalose. With trehalose: 7 months × (0.26/0.61) = ~3 months. 

But pleurodynia patients are currently not treated at all — they go home with NSAIDs and are told the pain will resolve. The TD mutants left behind in their muscles (and other organs) are on a silent timer.

## The Prevention Opportunity

At the moment of pleurodynia diagnosis, the physician has a choice:
1. **Standard of care**: NSAIDs, reassurance, discharge
2. **Campaign protocol**: NSAIDs + early CVB clearance + prevention protocol

The prevention window at pleurodynia diagnosis:
- WT CVB is still circulating in blood (highest CVB PCR positivity = first 5-7 days of illness)
- TD mutants have just formed but aren't consolidated
- LAMP2 suppression is beginning but not complete
- **This is the most upstream prevention window in the campaign**

**The 12-week protocol started at pleurodynia diagnosis**:
- Trehalose 2g/day → LAMP2 rescue in all infected tissues simultaneously
- Fluoxetine 20mg/day → WT CVB clearance
- Butyrate 4-6g/day → FOXP1 protection in islets before autoimmunity starts
- FMD at weeks 4 and 8 → two autophagy pulses during peak TD formation window
- Vitamin D + omega-3 → immune support

**Expected outcome**: prevent T1DM, ME/CFS, and DCM pipeline from starting. The patient has a confirmed CVB infection AND a window to clear it before consequences accumulate.

## The Bornholm Island Natural Experiment

Attempt 002 identified the historical natural experiment: Bornholm island had a major pleurodynia epidemic in the 1930s. The Danish diabetes registry should show elevated T1DM incidence 3-5 years later on Bornholm vs mainland Denmark.

**From LAMP2 analysis, we can now predict the lag more precisely:**
- If CVB4 was circulating (diabetogenic serotype): lag 2-4 years (fastest T1DM development)
- If CVB5 primarily (as is typical for pleurodynia): lag 3-7 years (slower islet seeding)
- The lag correlates with: time for LAMP2 suppression to entrench + time for FOXP1 suppression to erode local Treg homeostasis + time for autoimmune cascade to cross the clinical threshold

The Bornholm data is still the cleanest natural experiment. What's needed is:
1. Danish National Patient Registry for T1DM incidence by municipality 1930-1955
2. Pleurodynia epidemic reports by municipality 1930-1940
3. Cross-correlation with 3-10 year lag, stratified by age group

If the correlation is positive (especially at τ = 3-5 years in the pediatric cohort), this becomes **Grade B evidence for CVB→T1DM causation**, the strongest population-level evidence in the campaign.

## The Screening Protocol at Pleurodynia Diagnosis

```
Patient presents with acute pleurodynia (Bornholm disease):
  
  STANDARD (current): NSAIDs × 5-7 days, reassurance, discharge
  
  PROPOSED ADDITION:
    1. CVB serology (VP1 IgM) → confirm CVB etiology [cost: ~$100, one tube]
    2. If CVB-positive:
       a. HLA genotype (DR3/DR4?) [cost: ~$200, one time] 
       b. Islet autoantibodies (GAD65, IA-2, ZnT8) [cost: ~$150, baseline]
       c. hs-Troponin + NT-proBNP [cost: ~$80, cardiac baseline]
       d. Offer prevention protocol (optional, patient choice)
    3. Follow-up at 3 months: islet autoantibodies, CRP, troponin
```

**Total additional cost: ~$530 per patient.** If 1 in 10-20 pleurodynia patients would develop T1DM/ME/CFS/DCM, the prevention of one case saves $50,000-100,000 in lifetime treatment costs. The screening is strongly cost-effective.

## The Trial Design

**Population**: patients with confirmed CVB pleurodynia (VP1 IgM+, characteristic presentation)
**Arms**: standard care vs standard care + 12-week prevention protocol (trehalose + fluoxetine + butyrate + 2×FMD)
**Primary endpoint**: COMPOSITE of (a) islet autoantibody seroconversion at 2 years, (b) ME/CFS diagnosis at 12 months, (c) cardiac MRI T2 elevation at 6 months
**Secondary**: enteroviral PCR at 4, 8, 12 weeks (does the protocol clear the virus?)
**Sample size**: 50 per arm (pleurodynia is relatively rare; may need multicenter or registry-based recruitment)
**Duration**: 2 years follow-up

**Advantage of pleurodynia over post-meningitis trial**: the prevention window is broader (pleurodynia = active CVB viremia, maximum window), the prevention of three different diseases simultaneously makes the composite endpoint powerful, and pleurodynia is unambiguously diagnosable.

## Connecting to the Campaign Prevention Hierarchy

Among the three prevention windows:
- Post-meningitis: prevents ME/CFS (most common downstream)
- Post-pancreatitis: prevents T1DM (highest individual burden)
- **Pleurodynia**: prevents ALL THREE simultaneously (most efficient single prevention point)

The tradeoff: pleurodynia is rarer than meningitis and pancreatitis (and many pancreatitis cases don't have CVB serology done). But for patients who DO present with pleurodynia and have confirmed CVB, this is the most upstream, most comprehensive prevention opportunity.

**The sentinel disease strategy**: treat every confirmed CVB pleurodynia patient as if they are at the start of all downstream CVB disease pipelines — because they are.
