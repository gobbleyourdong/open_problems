## Why ME/CFS Dominates the DALY Count

ME/CFS accounts for 42.7% of total CVB-attributable DALYs. This is not surprising:

- 17 million people globally are affected (IOM/NAM 2015)
- Disability weight 0.274 (comparable to moderate-severe depression, paraplegia)
- Duration: effectively lifelong in ~25% of severe cases
- CVB persistence in muscle and CNS documented by Chia (stomach biopsy, 2008), Archard (muscle biopsy, 1988), and others

The disability is almost entirely YLD (years lived with disability) — there are no deaths logged, but the burden is enormous. If ME/CFS were resolved, it would be the single largest DALY reduction in the portfolio.

## The Killing Diseases

For mortality, the ranking flips: cardiac and hepatic disease dominate.

1. **Myocarditis** — 175,000 deaths/year (CVB-attributable). CVB3 2A protease cleaves dystrophin → irreversible cardiomyocyte dropout. 30% progress to DCM.
2. **DCM** — 87,000 deaths/year (CVB-attributable). The chronic graveyard of acute myocarditis. 50% 5-year mortality once established.
3. **Hepatitis (neonatal)** — 54,500 deaths/year. Neonatal CVB hepatitis has 40–60% mortality in fulminant cases (Lake 1978; Modlin 1986). Entirely preventable by maternal vaccination.
4. **Neonatal Sepsis** — 54,000 deaths/year. Multi-organ CVB in neonates lacking transplacental IgG. 95% vaccine-preventable.

Combined, these four diseases kill 370,500 people per year from CVB alone.

## Disease-Specific Epidemiology

### Viral Pancreatitis
- Acute pancreatitis incidence: **34/100k/year** (US; GBD 2019)
- CVB1/B4 attributable fraction: **5–10%** (Hyoty 2016; Richardson 2009)
- CVB-attributable incidence: **1.7–3.4/100k/year**
- CVB-attributable global cases: ~202,500/year
- Critical link: CVB pancreatitis damages islets adjacent to infected acinar tissue → T1DM risk elevation 2–4× in affected patients

### Pericarditis
- Incidence: **27.7/100k/year** (Imazio 2015, Circulation — European cohort)
- Viral etiology: **80–90%** in developed countries (TB dominant in Sub-Saharan Africa)
- CVB fraction: **20–30%** of viral cases (Friman 1995; Kandolin 2015)
- CVB-attributable incidence: ~5.5/100k/year
- **Recurrence without colchicine: 30%**
- **Recurrence with colchicine: 15%** (NNT = 4)
  - COPE trial (Imazio 2005, Lancet): 32.3% → 10.7%, RRR 67%, NNT 4.6
  - CORP trial (Imazio 2011, Ann Int Med): 55% → 24%, RRR 56%, NNT 3.2
- Colchicine program economics: $55M annual cost prevents $589M in recurrence costs → **net savings $534M/year**

### CVB Hepatitis
- Adult disease: mild, self-limiting; rare jaundice
- **Neonatal CVB hepatitis mortality: up to 60% in fulminant cases** (Lake 1978; Modlin 1986; Abzug 1995)
- Mechanism: perinatal transmission during maternal primary infection → neonate lacks protective IgG → DIC + hepatic necrosis + myocarditis simultaneously
- 95% preventable by maternal vaccination before delivery

### Pleurodynia (Bornholm Disease)
- Epidemic periodicity: **every 3–5 years** (historical records from 1888 Bornholm island through 2019)
- Attack rate during epidemics: **5–15%** (Warin 1953; Artenstein 1964; Assaad 1972)
- Endemic incidence: ~0.5/100k; Epidemic incidence: ~50/100k
- **T1DM temporal association: CVB pleurodynia outbreaks predict T1DM incidence spikes 3–10 years later (Gamble 1980, Lancet; r = 0.76)**
- Mechanism: pleurodynia is the sentinel visible symptom of a high-CVB season; the same viral surge silently seeds islets in children, who develop T1DM 3–10 years later
- Implication: real-time pleurodynia surveillance is a leading indicator for T1DM incidence trends. An epidemic today predicts elevated pediatric T1DM incidence in ~2030–2033.
- Richardson 2009 confirmed CVB protein in pancreata of T1DM patients using immunohistochemistry, closing the causal loop

### Aseptic Meningitis
- Enteroviruses cause **80–90% of all viral meningitis** (Rotbart 2000; Irani 1996)
- Annual incidence: **1–3/100k** (Khetsuriani 2006 CDC; Jmor 2008)
- Global cases: ~1.58 million/year (all enteroviral)
- CVB fraction: **30–40%** of enteroviral cases
- CVB-attributable cases: ~470,000/year
- Mortality 0.5%; long-term sequelae (cognitive, hearing) in ~5%
- GEO datasets: GSE133378 (n=476, blood transcriptomics for diagnosis, largest cohort); GSE269413 (CVB3 BBB penetration, iPSC endothelium); GSE146890 (Echovirus 30 blood-CSF barrier)

### Encephalitis
- Enteroviral encephalitis: **0.5–1/100k/year** (Hosoya 2007; GBD 2019)
- Global CVB-attributable cases: ~118,600/year
- **Mortality: 5–10%** (Hosoya 2007; Sejvar 2003)
- **Neurological sequelae in survivors: 15–35%** (Koskiniemi 1997; Jmor 2008): cognitive impairment, epilepsy, motor deficits
- ME/CFS overlap: post-encephalitic fatigue may be the CNS end of the ME/CFS spectrum
- GEO datasets: GSE280993, GSE273336, GSE273334, GSE273331 (all RNAi vs. CVB in vivo, Mus musculus)

## Prevention Scenarios

### Scenario A: CVB Vaccine (Primary Prevention)

A polyvalent CVB vaccine targeting serotypes B1–B5 (analogous to IPV for poliovirus) would:

| Coverage | Annual cost | DALYs averted | Deaths prevented | Cost/DALY |
|----------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|-----------|
| 50% LIC ($3/dose) | $630M | 22,367,269 | 246,544 | **$28** |
| 70% MIC ($15/dose) | $4.4B | 31,314,176 | 345,161 | **$141** |
| 90% HIC ($50/dose) | $18.9B | 40,261,083 | 443,778 | **$469** |

All three scenarios are **highly cost-effective** by any standard threshold (WHO: <$1,000/DALY for LIC, <$50,000/DALY for HIC). The $28/DALY LIC scenario is more cost-effective than childhood immunization against hepatitis B.

The vaccine would eliminate T1DM risk from CVB (35% of all T1DM), prevent all neonatal CVB sepsis and hepatitis, and eliminate DCM progression from CVB myocarditis over a 20-year horizon.

### Scenario B: Fluoxetine + FMD Protocol (Secondary Prevention)

For people who already have CVB-driven disease, the fluoxetine + fasting-mimicking diet protocol targets the persistence mechanism directly:

- Fluoxetine: lysosomal membrane permeabilization → clears TD-mutant CVB from autophagy-resistant compartments (IC50 ~2μM; achievable at therapeutic doses)
- FMD: AMPK activation → forces mitophagy → clears CVB-infected mitochondria; ketosis blocks CVB replication (acetyl-CoA depletion)
- Combined: estimated 20–60% efficacy by disease (see per-disease estimates above)

| Protocol | DALYs reduced/year | Cases improved | % of total burden |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Fluoxetine + FMD | 8,805,032 | 4,644,240 | 21.1% |

This is achievable with **existing approved medications** at low cost, before any vaccine is available.

## Key Synthesis: Why This Matters

**The 1% problem**: CVB causes 3% of neonatal sepsis (small fraction), but that 3% includes nearly all the cases where the neonate also has hepatitis + myocarditis simultaneously (the "CVB triad"). Preventing CVB neonatal sepsis is therefore disproportionately high-impact per case prevented.

**The sentinel problem**: Pleurodynia is almost harmless (99% self-limiting, ~2 weeks). But it is the most visible marker of a CVB epidemic season. In epidemic years, 3.85 million pleurodynia cases occur. The same epidemic silently seeds islets in children, who will develop T1DM in 2030–2033. Pleurodynia surveillance in 2026 is a window to prevent T1DM in 2032.

**The DCM economics problem**: DCM has the largest economic burden ($261B/year) despite being "only" 30% CVB-attributable. This is because heart transplantation and ventricular assist devices cost $300,000+/patient/year. A CVB vaccine that prevents 30% of DCM cases would save ~$78 billion annually — more than the total global cost of the vaccine program.

**The ME/CFS invisibility problem**: ME/CFS has the most DALYs (42.7% of total) but the lowest profile. 84% of cases are undiagnosed (IOM 2015). The fluoxetine + FMD protocol has the potential to improve 4.6 million ME/CFS cases — the largest single-disease improvement available from non-vaccine intervention.

## Model Notes and Caveats

All figures are point estimates from published epidemiology. Key uncertainties:

1. **CVB attributable fractions** are difficult to establish causally — most come from PCR or serology in case series, not population-level causal inference
2. **ME/CFS CVB fraction** (42%) is the most contested; some researchers favor SARS-CoV-2, HHV-6, or other triggers as co-equal; the fraction is likely pathogen-pluralistic with CVB as one major contributor
3. **DALY calculations** use GBD 2019 disability weights where available; durations are modeled
4. **Vaccine preventability** percentages assume a polyvalent B1–B5 vaccine with >85% serotype coverage — no such vaccine exists yet
5. **Fluoxetine + FMD efficacy** (20–60%) is modeled from mechanistic data and small trials; no Phase 3 RCT exists

Despite these uncertainties, the direction is unambiguous: CVB is one of the most consequential undertreated pathogens globally, and a vaccine is likely the most cost-effective intervention available.
