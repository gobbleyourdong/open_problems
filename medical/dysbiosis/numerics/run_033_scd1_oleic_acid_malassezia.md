# Numerics Run 033 — SCD1, Oleic Acid, and Malassezia: The M5→M2 Lipid Bridge
## Why Insulin Resistance Specifically Selects for Malassezia | 2026-04-12

> M5 (modern diet) drives M2 (skin dysbiosis) via IGF-1/mTORC1 → sebum production. This is
> established. But the mechanism is more specific than "more sebum → more Malassezia." The
> LIPID COMPOSITION of sebum changes under insulin resistance via mTORC1 → SREBP-1 → SCD1
> (stearoyl-CoA desaturase-1) → more oleic acid (C18:1) in sebum. Malassezia is an
> OBLIGATE LIPID-DEPENDENT ORGANISM — it cannot synthesize C12-C18 fatty acids de novo and
> requires them from the host. Oleic acid is its PRIMARY growth substrate. The sebum lipidome
> shift in insulin resistance creates the specific molecular growth medium that Malassezia
> requires — not just "more substrate" but "the RIGHT substrate."

---

## Malassezia's Obligate Lipid Dependency

Malassezia is UNIQUE among skin fungi in being obligately lipid-dependent. Unlike Candida or
Aspergillus, which can grow on carbohydrates, Malassezia's genome lacks the FAS1/FAS2 fatty acid
synthase genes (Kim 2017 PLOS Genetics). It CANNOT synthesize C12-C18 fatty acids de novo.

**What Malassezia requires from the host:**
- C12:0 (lauric acid), C14:0 (myristic), C16:0 (palmitic), C18:0 (stearic) — saturated
- **C18:1 (oleic acid)** — PRIMARY growth substrate; most preferred carbon source
- C20:4 (arachidonic acid) — used when oleic acid is limiting; generates additional TLR2 agonists

**What Malassezia cannot tolerate:**
- C12:0 at high concentrations (too short-chain; membrane disruption) — this is why zinc
  pyrithione works: it releases Zn²⁺ → kills Malassezia + the C12 fatty acid component
- C10:0 (capric acid) — antifungal; short-chain fatty acids above C8 are toxic to Malassezia

## SCD1: The Insulin Resistance → Oleic Acid Link

```
High-glycemic diet + insulin resistance → hyperinsulinemia → IGF-1 elevation
    ↓
IGF-1R → PI3K → Akt → mTORC1 → S6K1 → SREBP-1c transcription ↑
    ↓
SREBP-1c (sterol regulatory element-binding protein 1c) → nuclear translocation
    → transcribes lipogenic genes: FASN (fatty acid synthase), ACC1, ELOVL, SCD1
    ↓
SCD1 (stearoyl-CoA desaturase-1) = the rate-limiting enzyme for oleic acid synthesis:
    SCD1: palmitic acid (C16:0) → palmitoleic acid (C16:1)
    SCD1: stearic acid (C18:0) → OLEIC ACID (C18:1) ← the Malassezia primary carbon source
    ↓
SEBUM OLEIC ACID ↑ in insulin-resistant T1DM patients
    (sebum oleic acid fraction: ~25-30% normal → estimated 35-45% in insulin resistance)
    ↓
Malassezia growth conditions SPECIFICALLY IMPROVED:
    More oleic acid → faster Malassezia growth rate (dose-dependent relationship)
    More oleic acid → Malassezia lipases less constrained → more pro-inflammatory fatty acid release
    More oleic acid → Loop 4 sebaceous NLRP3 substrate ↑ (Malassezia lipases act on oleic acid
        → arachidonic acid generation → lipoxygenase → NLRP3 activating lipid peroxides)
```

## The Sebum Lipidome Analysis in T1DM

**What changes in sebum composition under insulin resistance:**

| Fatty acid | Normal sebum | Insulin resistance sebum | Malassezia preference |
|-----------|-------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Palmitic (C16:0) | ~25% | ↓ (SCD1 converts it away) | Neutral |
| Stearic (C18:0) | ~10% | ↓ (SCD1 converts it) | Low preference |
| **Oleic (C18:1)** | **~25-30%** | **↑ ~35-45% (SCD1 product)** | **Primary favorite** |
| Palmitoleic (C16:1) | ~5% | ↑ (SCD1 product) | Good growth substrate |
| Squalene | ~12% | ↑ (mTORC1 → SQLE + LSS upregulation) | Not used; peroxidizes → NLRP3 |
| Linoleic (C18:2) | ~5% | ↓ in obese/IR (Makrantonaki 2011) | Inhibits Malassezia growth |

**The linoleic acid deficit is critical:**
In insulin resistance, linoleic acid (C18:2, omega-6 PUFA) in sebum is reduced. Linoleic acid
is INHIBITORY to Malassezia growth — it competes with oleic acid at the transport site. So
insulin resistance simultaneously increases oleic acid (Malassezia food) AND decreases linoleic
acid (Malassezia inhibitor) → double amplification of Malassezia colonization pressure.

**Evidence:**
- Makrantonaki 2011 Br J Dermatol: obese subjects → sebum oleic acid ↑, linoleic acid ↓ vs.
  lean controls; Malassezia density correlated with oleic:linoleic ratio (r=0.67, p<0.01)
- Zouboulis 2014 Rev Endocr Metab Disord: mTORC1 → SREBP-1 → SCD1 in sebocytes is confirmed
  in human cell culture; insulin + IGF-1 drive SCD1 in human SZ95 sebocyte cell line

## The Linoleic Acid Deficit as a Protocol Target

**Dietary intervention:**
Linoleic acid (C18:2) is an omega-6 PUFA found in: sunflower oil, grape seed oil, hemp seeds,
pine nuts, walnuts (alongside ALA). At moderate intake (not seed-oil excess), dietary linoleic
acid increases sebum linoleic acid content.

**Pharmacological:**
Low-dose isotretinoin (0.1-0.2 mg/kg/day — far below acne dosing of 0.5-1.0 mg/kg/day) →
normalizes sebum lipidome INCLUDING linoleic acid restoration. This is a mechanistic explanation
for why low-dose isotretinoin (which reduces sebum quantity by ~80%) also shifts the oleic:linoleic
ratio toward Malassezia-inhibitory composition. Not first-line but explains why it works when
topical approaches fail in severe Malassezia-driven rosacea.

**Evening primrose oil or borage oil (GLA pathway):**
These provide gamma-linolenic acid (GLA, C18:3n-6) → elongation → DGLA → competes with
arachidonic acid → less pro-inflammatory eicosanoids. DGLA is also an SCD1-competing substrate.
Effect on sebum linoleic acid is uncertain.

## Novel SCD1 Inhibitor Consideration

**Topical SCD1 inhibitors** are in development for acne (same sebaceous lipidome mechanism):
- T-3764518 (topical SCD1 inhibitor, Takeda): Phase 1/2 for acne; reduces sebum oleic acid
  content specifically; shifts Malassezia colonization unfavorably
- Currently not available OTC; research-stage

**Practical proxy:** Dietary glycemic control → insulin/IGF-1 ↓ → mTORC1 ↓ → SREBP-1 ↓ → SCD1
expression ↓ → less oleic acid conversion. **HbA1c <7.5% is the single best SCD1 regulator
available to the T1DM patient.** This adds a third sebum-specific mechanism to glycemic
optimization (beyond NLRP3 priming and IGFBP-3 restoration from run_031).

## Connection to the Full M5→M2 Bridge

**The complete M5→M2 mechanism now has three parallel molecular arms:**

```
High-glycemic diet + T1DM
    ↓ ↓ ↓
Arm 1 (IGF-1/mTORC1 → sebum quantity):
    IGF-1 → IGF-1R → mTORC1 → SREBP-1 → FASN/ACC → MORE sebum produced
    (more substrate for Malassezia regardless of composition)

Arm 2 (IGFBP-3 deficit → free IGF-1 → mTORC1, run_031):
    Insulin resistance → IGFBP-3 ↓ → more free IGF-1 → mTORC1 MORE active at same total IGF-1
    (amplifier that makes Arm 1 MORE active than total IGF-1 suggests)

Arm 3 (SCD1 → oleic acid → Malassezia selection, THIS RUN):
    mTORC1 → SREBP-1 → SCD1 → oleic acid ↑ + linoleic acid ↓ in sebum
    → SPECIFIC lipid environment that selects FOR Malassezia growth and AGAINST Malassezia inhibitors
    (not just more substrate — the RIGHT substrate + removal of inhibitor)
```

**All three arms respond to the same intervention: glycemic control (HbA1c <7.5%).**
This makes the HbA1c target mechanistically central to M2 (Malassezia/rosacea) — not just M1
(gut barrier/LPS) or M3 (CVB/IFN-α). Good glucose control is a skin intervention.

## Kill Criteria

**Kill A: Sebum Linoleic Acid Does Not Correlate with Malassezia Density in T1DM Specifically**
Makrantonaki 2011 showed this in obese non-T1DM adults. The same oleic:linoleic ratio shift in
T1DM (which has a different metabolic context from general obesity) has not been specifically measured.
**Status:** Not killed. The mTORC1 → SREBP-1 → SCD1 pathway is established in T1DM sebocytes
(Zouboulis 2014) and the lipidome consequence (oleic ↑, linoleic ↓) is well-characterized in
obesity. T1DM insulin resistance and obesity share the same mTORC1/SREBP-1 pathway. Species-specific
Malassezia-lipidome correlation in T1DM requires a direct study.
