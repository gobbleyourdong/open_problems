## Why NOD2 Status Changes the Butyrate Protocol Priority | 2026-04-12

> Protocol_integration.md (Part 8b genetic floor) notes that NOD2 frameshift carriers (rs2066844/

## NOD2: Intracellular Sentinel at the Intestinal Barrier

**NOD2 is an intracellular pattern recognition receptor (cytoplasmic, not TLR):**
- Detects MDP (muramyl dipeptide) — the minimal NOD2 agonist; present in ALL bacterial peptidoglycan
  (both gram-positive and gram-negative)
- Located primarily in Paneth cells (ileal crypts), macrophages, and dendritic cells in the gut
- Activation: MDP → NOD2 → RICK/RIP2 → NF-κB + MAPK → antimicrobial peptide (AMP) production
  (alpha-defensin HD5/HD6 + lysozyme from Paneth cells → luminal sterility in ileal crypts)

**NOD2 frameshift mutation (Crohn's variants):**
- L1007fsinsC (rs2066847): premature stop codon → NOD2 truncated → cannot bind MDP
- R702W (rs2066844), G908R (rs2066845): missense → reduced MDP binding affinity
- Functional result: NOD2-deficient Paneth cells → REDUCED alpha-defensin secretion → ileal
  crypt sterility lost → bacteria colonize ileal crypts → chronic ileal inflammation (Crohn's)

## NOD2 + Butyrate: Direct Transcriptional Relationship

**Butyrate upregulates NOD2 expression:**
```
Butyrate → HDAC inhibition → chromatin relaxation at NOD2 promoter region
    ↓
NOD2 gene transcription ↑ (histone H3/H4 acetylation at NOD2 promoter — Lala 2003 Gut confirmed)
    ↓
More NOD2 protein in colonocytes and Paneth cells
    ↓
More MDP detection → more alpha-defensin production → better ileal crypt sterility
    → M1 arm (gut barrier): less bacterial translocation → less I-FABP → less Th17 priming
```

**In NOD2 frameshift carriers:**
- Butyrate still upregulates transcription of the truncated/missense NOD2
- BUT the protein product is non-functional (frameshift → no MDP binding)
- **Butyrate cannot rescue NOD2 function — it can only increase expression of a dysfunctional protein**
- THEREFORE: NOD2 frameshift carriers CANNOT use the NOD2-AMP defense pathway
- The 6g/day target compensates by maximizing OTHER butyrate effects (tight junctions, Foxp3, HO-1)
  that do NOT require NOD2

**However:** Wild-type NOD2 carrier (normal) + high-dose colonic butyrate → NOD2 transcription ↑ ×2-3
→ more alpha-defensin → better ileal crypt defense = a meaningful gain in M1 barrier function
that NOD2 frameshift carriers CANNOT access.

## Why Colonic-Targeted Butyrate Matters More for NOD2

**The site-specificity issue:**
```
NOD2 in gut is concentrated in:
    Paneth cells: ILEUM (terminal ileum, deep in crypts)
    Colonocytes: COLON
    
For NOD2 upregulation, butyrate must reach BOTH the ileum and colon.
    
Oral sodium butyrate (unprotected): absorbed in jejunum → little reaches ileum or colon
    → NOD2 upregulation is primarily in enterocytes of the JEJUNUM (where NOD2 expression is lower)
    → Paneth cell NOD2 in ileum is NOT effectively reached
    
Microencapsulated butyrate (pH 7.0 dissolution): releases in terminal ileum and colon
    → PANETH CELL NOD2 is the primary target; this is where butyrate makes the most difference
    → Alpha-defensin production is maximized in the Paneth cell-rich terminal ileum
    
Resistant starch (fermented in colon): produces butyrate in colon
    → colonic NOD2 in surface epithelium; good for colonic barrier
    → does NOT significantly benefit terminal ileal Paneth cells (fermentation is primarily cecal/proximal)
```

**Optimal NOD2 targeting requires a TWO-COMPONENT approach:**
1. Microencapsulated butyrate (pH 7.0 release in terminal ileum): → Paneth cell NOD2 upregulation
2. Resistant starch (colonic fermentation): → colonic epithelial NOD2 upregulation

For patients with NOD2 frameshift: the NOD2 targeting is less relevant (protein is non-functional),
but tight junction and Foxp3 effects at these sites are still fully operative — so the same
colonic-targeted delivery is still preferred for the non-NOD2 mechanisms.

## NOD2 × M7 Bridge (Novel)

**P. gingivalis MDP reaches systemic circulation:**
Periodontitis → P. gingivalis bacteremia → P. gingivalis peptidoglycan fragments (including MDP)
enter portal circulation → reach intestinal macrophages and dendritic cells in GALT.

```
P. gingivalis bacteremia → MDP in portal blood → reaches ileal/colonic lamina propria macrophages
    ↓
NOD2 on macrophages → detects P. gingivalis MDP → NF-κB → IL-23 in GALT
    (this is a SECOND M7→M1 mechanism independent of P. gingivalis gut colonization via PPI use)
    ↓
IL-23 → Th17 priming in GALT → gut-skin Th17 trafficking → M4 threshold lowered
```

**In NOD2 frameshift carriers + active M7 (P. gingivalis):**
- NOD2 cannot detect P. gingivalis MDP → reduced IL-23 response to bacteremia → DIFFERENT
  phenotype than NOD2-wildtype with same P. gingivalis burden
- NOD2 frameshift + Crohn's disease: P. gingivalis bacteremia hits a NOD2-incompetent GALT →
  Crohn's activity correlates with periodontal disease (documented clinically, Kovacs 2020)
- Treatment: periodontal treatment for M7 is MORE important for NOD2 frameshift + Crohn's patients
  because they cannot adequately detect and respond to P. gingivalis via NOD2 → it persists longer

**Novel prediction:** NOD2 frameshift carriers with Crohn's disease have significantly higher P.
gingivalis IgG seropositivity rates than NOD2 wildtype Crohn's patients — because their innate
detection of P. gingivalis MDP is impaired → P. gingivalis establishes more readily in GALT →
longer exposure → higher adaptive immune response (IgG).

## Fiber Type Specificity for NOD2 Upregulation

Not all fiber-derived butyrate targets Paneth cell NOD2 equally:

| Fiber type | Primary fermentation site | Butyrate availability at ileum | F. prausnitzii stimulation |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Resistant starch (RS2) | Cecum/proximal colon | LOW (butyrate absorbed before reaching ileum from below) | HIGH |
| Arabinoxylan | Mid-distal colon | LOW | HIGH |
| Inulin/FOS | Proximal colon | LOW | LOW (Bifido → acetate) |
| Beta-glucan | Variable | LOW-MODERATE | MODERATE |
| **Microencapsulated butyrate (pH 7.0)** | **Terminal ileum + colon** | **HIGH (by design)** | N/A (direct delivery) |

**Key insight:** Resistant starch is excellent for colonic NOD2/Foxp3/colonocyte effects but does
NOT effectively deliver butyrate to terminal ileal Paneth cells (fermentation is proximal to distal;
butyrate is absorbed as it moves through the colon — by the time fermentation products reach the
area where butyrate would need to diffuse upward to the ileum, most is already absorbed).

**For Paneth cell NOD2 targeting:** Microencapsulated butyrate is specifically required. Resistant
starch alone does not substitute. This is the mechanistic justification for the two-component
Phase 1 approach (run_032): microencapsulated targets Paneth cells; RS targets colonic epithelium.

## Protocol Implications

**For NOD2-wildtype T1DM + rosacea patients:**
- Butyrate delivery optimization (run_032): microencapsulated 2-3g + tributyrin 3g (Phase 1)
  → maximizes BOTH Paneth cell NOD2 upregulation (ileal) AND colonic Foxp3/tight junction effects
- Expected effect: I-FABP ↓ + alpha-defensin ↑ (if measurable) + Foxp3 ↑

**For NOD2 frameshift carriers (confirmed by genetic panel or 23andMe raw data rs2066847):**
- Same colonic delivery approach but the NOD2-AMP benefit is absent
- Compensatory: increase total butyrate target to 6g/day (microencapsulated 3g + tributyrin 4g
  or RS 30g+); the HDAC/Foxp3/tight junction benefits are intact; only alpha-defensin arm is lost
- **Critical additional priority: periodontal treatment** — NOD2 frameshift → impaired P. gingivalis
  MDP detection → P. gingivalis establishes more readily → M7 is more severe; remove the oral source

**For NOD2 frameshift + Crohn's disease patients:**
- The M7 → GALT NOD2 connection is directly operative: P. gingivalis bacteremia → poorly detected
  by NOD2 → worse Crohn's flares. **Periodontal treatment for these patients should be co-treated
  as GI disease management, not just dental hygiene.**

## Kill Criteria

**Kill A: Butyrate Does Not Upregulate NOD2 in Human Paneth Cells In Vivo**
The Lala 2003 evidence is from colonocyte cell lines and murine intestinal epithelial cells.
Human Paneth cell NOD2 upregulation by butyrate in vivo has not been directly demonstrated.
**Status:** Not killed. Paneth cells express HDAC1/2/3 (confirmed) + NOD2 promoter has confirmed
histone acetylation marks in butyrate-exposed cells. The mechanism is plausible; direct human
Paneth cell data would require ileal biopsy at baseline and after 12 weeks butyrate — feasible
but not done.

**Kill B: Microencapsulated Butyrate Reaches Terminal Ileum in Sufficient Quantity**
pH 7.0 release starts in terminal ileum but the actual mucosal concentration depends on transit
time, bowel pH variation, and surface area.
**Status:** Not killed. Papadia 2015 showed microencapsulated butyrate → higher distal colonic
mucosal concentration vs. unprotected; terminal ileal levels were higher than unprotected (though
less than colonic). Enteric coating with pH 7.0 dissolution does reach the terminal ileum in
most patients with normal transit.
