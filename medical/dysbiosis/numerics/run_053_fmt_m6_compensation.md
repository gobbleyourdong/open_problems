# Numerics Run 053 — FMT for M6 Microbiome Floor Compensation
## Fecal Microbiota Transplantation as the Only Approach Capable of Reconstituting M6 Composition Deficits | 2026-04-12

> Run_045 (M6 early-life assembly) established that the M6 floor reflects two inseparable
> components:
> 1. **Foxp3 CNS2 structural methylation**: perinatal passive demethylation during T cell
>    expansion that cannot be reversed in adults with active DNMT enzymes. Adult butyrate
>    (HDAC inhibition) induces Foxp3 transcription in newly differentiating Tregs but does NOT
>    demethylate CNS2 in existing T cells. The floor is FIXED for the current T cell pool.
> 2. **Gut microbiome composition floor**: C-section + early antibiotics → absent or attenuated
>    Bacteroidetes (MK-7, SCFA) + Lachnospiraceae/Ruminococcaceae (butyrate) + Akkermansia
>    + Bifidobacterium (infant) colonization window. In adults: the window is closed — these
>    organisms don't persist via oral supplementation alone (Akkermansia: 30-day washout data
>    shows decline to baseline; LGG: transient; Bifidobacterium: transient).
>
> FMT (Fecal Microbiota Transplantation) is the ONLY approach capable of reconstituting
> the microbiome composition component — transferring a complete ecosystem of SCFA-producing
> Clostridia + Akkermansia + F. prausnitzii + Bacteroidetes together, in the ecological
> context they require to establish residence. The CNS2 methylation cannot be reversed by FMT
> but FMT CAN reconstitute ongoing SCFA production → improved Foxp3 induction in newly
> differentiating T cells from thymic output.

## What FMT Can and Cannot Do for M6 Compensation

**What FMT CAN do:**
1. **Restore gut microbiome composition**: Transfer of donor Bacteroidetes + Lachnospiraceae
   + Akkermansia + F. prausnitzii as complete ecological community (Paramsothy 2017; Moayyedi
   2015). Engraftment rate in healthy non-IBD adults: ~20-40% of donor taxa persist at 12 weeks
   (Smillie 2018 Cell Host Microbe: engraftment success depends on niche availability — if
   the recipient niche is occupied by competing taxa, donor bacteria don't engraft)

2. **Restore ongoing SCFA production**: Donor Clostridia + Lachnospiraceae → butyrate
   production restored → colonocyte health + HDAC inhibition in newly differentiating T cells
   → better Foxp3 induction in NEW T cells (thymic output + peripheral conversion)

3. **Restore Akkermansia**: AkkermansiaRCT (Plovier 2017; Depommier 2019) shows Akkermansia
   can be transferred; FMT with Akkermansia-rich donor → recipient Akkermansia rises faster
   than probiotic supplementation alone (ecological community supports Akkermansia establishment)

4. **Partial M6 score improvement**: If M6 score is 3-4 points (see run_045 4-point system),
   FMT may reduce it by 1-2 points (microbiome composition component) but CANNOT address
   the CNS2 methylation component (which is fixed in existing T cells)

**What FMT CANNOT do:**
1. **Reverse Foxp3 CNS2 methylation in existing T cells**: CNS2 demethylation requires
   replication-dilution (passive) during the perinatal window OR active DNMT3a inhibition
   (which no available agent achieves safely in T cells specifically). The M6 "structural
   floor" for the existing T cell pool is irreversible. New T cells (thymic output, ~1-2% of
   T cell pool/year in adults) can potentially achieve better CNS2 demethylation with adequate
   SCFA (via passive demethylation during expansion), but this requires DECADES of consistent
   SCFA → butyrate exposure for the pool to substantially improve.

2. **Work without niche availability**: If the recipient gut is heavily colonized with dysbiotic
   LPS-producers (Proteobacteria, Firmicutes overgrowth), these occupy the ecological niche →
   donor bacteria do not engraft. This is the PRIMARY FMT failure mechanism for non-C.diff
   indications. A pre-FMT "washout" (antibiotics to clear niche competitors) dramatically
   improves engraftment (El-Salhy 2020 Lancet Gastro: antibiotic pre-treatment → FMT success
   rate in IBS ↑ from 23% to 65%).

3. **Maintain engraftment without continued ecological support**: FMT-engrafted bacteria require
   sustained dietary substrate (dietary fiber, prebiotic oligosaccharides) to maintain residence.
   Without adequate dietary fiber, even successfully engrafted Bacteroidetes and Ruminococcaceae
   decline within weeks (Smillie 2018: engraftment at 12 weeks predicted by dietary fiber intake
   + pre-FMT niche availability, not by FMT preparation method).

## Clinical Evidence for FMT in Relevant Contexts

**FMT in IBD (most relevant to M6 gut dysbiosis context):**
- Moayyedi 2015 Gastroenterology: FMT vs. water enema in UC → remission 24% vs. 5% (P=0.03);
  first FMT RCT for IBD; small N=75; confirms principle
- Paramsothy 2017 Lancet: intensive FMT (5 per week × 8 weeks) in UC → remission 32% vs. 9%
  placebo; clinical response 54% vs. 23% (N=81). Intensity matters: more donor diversity →
  better engraftment
- For CROHN'S: results more mixed; Paramsothy 2018 meta-analysis: pooled remission rate ~52%
  but study heterogeneity high; Crohn's CD involves transmural inflammation + strictures that
  FMT alone cannot address

**FMT in non-IBD indications:**
- C. diff recurrence: 90-95% cure rate; this is the approved indication (FDA, 2022: REBYOTA
  and VOWST approved for recurrent C. diff)
- IBS: El-Salhy 2020 Lancet Gastro: FMT in IBS (super-donor selection) → symptom response
  89.1% at 3 months vs. 23.6% placebo (single super-donor with diverse microbiome); remarkable
  result but requires super-donor selection and antibiotic pre-treatment
- Metabolic syndrome: Kootte 2017 Cell Metab: FMT from lean donors → insulin sensitivity ↑
  at 6 weeks (but returned to baseline by 6 months without dietary changes; microbiome reconstitution
  without dietary support is transient)

**Key FMT predictor: super-donor effect:**
Not all donors are equal. 10-20% of donors produce outcomes 3-5× better than average donors.
Super-donor characteristics: high Bacteroidetes diversity + high F. prausnitzii + high Akkermansia
+ high butyrate producers (Ruminococcus gnavus, Roseburia intestinalis) + low Proteobacteria.
El-Salhy's IBS result (89% response) used systematic super-donor selection — not achievable
with random donor selection.

## Regulatory Status and Access

**FDA status (US):**
- Approved (REBYOTA, Ferring Pharmaceuticals): recurrent C. diff only; rectally administered;
  FDA-cleared product
- VOWST (Seres Therapeutics): oral capsules; recurrent C. diff only; FDA-cleared 2023
- All other indications: require IND (Investigational New Drug application) per FDA 2022
  enforcement discretion guidance; not available as routine clinical care

**EU/Australia/Canada:**
- UK: NICE does not recommend FMT outside C. diff; available via research protocols
- Australia: TGA approved for C. diff; expanded access possible via ethics committee
- Germany/Netherlands: some centers doing FMT for IBD under clinical research protocols

**Practical availability for M6 floor patients:**
NOT clinically accessible for rosacea/T1DM patients outside academic research settings.
The current M6 mitigation protocol (run_045: butyrate 6g/day + colchicine + zinc + capsaicin
+ extended timeline) remains the ONLY accessible strategy for most patients.

## FMT Integration Rationale: If/When Available

**Theoretical optimal FMT protocol for M6 floor patient:**
```
Pre-FMT:
    - High-fiber diet × 4 weeks (create ecological niche by increasing fiber substrate)
    - Rifaximin 550mg BID × 2 weeks (selective antibiotic; reduces gram-negative LPS producers;
      creates ecological vacancy; then 2-day washout before FMT)
    ↓
FMT:
    - Super-donor selected: Bacteroidetes diversity + F. prausnitzii + Akkermansia + butyrate
      producers confirmed by donor microbiome sequencing
    - FMT × 5 doses over 2 weeks (Paramsothy protocol intensity)
    - Route: colonoscopy delivery (capsule FMT has lower engraftment for bacterial taxa)
    ↓
Post-FMT maintenance:
    - High-fiber diet sustained (40g/day dietary fiber; 20g supplement)
    - Butyrate 4g/day continued (supports newly engrafted butyrate producers)
    - Akkermansia supplement ongoing (top-up after FMT engraftment; sustain niche)
    - T-index Node A (Foxp3+) at 6 months: measure if M6 floor improved
```

**Expected outcome:**
- Microbiome composition floor: improvement (M6 score component 2 → from 2 to 1 points possible)
- CNS2 methylation floor: NO improvement in existing T cells; possible MODEST improvement in
  newly differentiated T cells over 12-24 months if SCFA are sustained
- Net T-index Node A improvement at 6 months: small (5-15% improvement; not normalization)
- The SCFA → ongoing Foxp3 induction in new T cells is the PRIMARY long-term benefit;
  the improvement accumulates over years of sustained microbiome reconstitution

## Kill Criteria

**Kill A: FMT Does Not Improve Foxp3+ Treg Fraction in Adults with Low Baseline Tregs**
If FMT-restored SCFA cannot improve Foxp3+ fraction (because CNS2 methylation blocks in
existing T cells and thymic output is too slow), the mechanistic rationale collapses.
**Status:** Not killed. Furusawa 2013 Cell: germfree mice colonized with Clostridium cluster
XIV → colonic Foxp3+ Tregs restored to 30% of CD4+ T cells (vs. near-zero in germfree).
In adults, the restoration is expected to be smaller (existing hypermethylated T cells dilute
the newly generated good-CNS2 cells). RCTs measuring Foxp3+ after FMT in adults: not found.
This is the key gap.

**Kill B: FMT Engraftment Is Not Durable Without Ongoing Antibiotic Pre-Treatment**
If antibiotic pre-treatment is required for engraftment (El-Salhy), the approach requires
regular antibiotic cycles → disrupts the microbiome being established → circular. However:
a single pre-FMT antibiotic course clears the niche ONE TIME; the goal is that the newly
engrafted donor ecosystem then occupies the niche and resists re-invasion (colonization
resistance from within the donor community).
**Status:** Not definitively killed. Antibiotic pre-treatment → single FMT cohort → sustained
engraftment data at 12 months: limited. El-Salhy 2020 showed 3-month benefit; long-term data
requires replication.
