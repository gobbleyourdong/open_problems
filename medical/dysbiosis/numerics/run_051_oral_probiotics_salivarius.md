## S. salivarius K12 Biology

**Health-associated oral Streptococcus:**
S. salivarius K12 is a commensal of the healthy human oropharynx. It colonizes the tongue dorsum
and pharyngeal epithelium. Normal colonization rate: ~30% of adults (reduced in those with
chronic periodontal disease — Wescombe 2012).

**BLIS (Bacteriocin-Like Inhibitory Substances) production:**
```
S. salivarius K12 → produces two lantibiotics:
    Salivaricin A2 → inhibits S. pyogenes + S. mutans
    Salivaricin B → inhibits:
        P. gingivalis (competitive exclusion from tongue + sulcular niche)
        T. denticola (salivaricin B disrupts T. denticola outer membrane; spirochetes are
            particularly sensitive to cationic antimicrobial peptides)
        Fusobacterium nucleatum (bridge species that connects early and late colonizers;
            without F. nucleatum, P. gingivalis attachment to plaque biofilm is impaired)
    ↓
S. salivarius K12 colonization → salivaricin B continuously present in saliva
    → inhibits red complex colonization at the ecological level
    → PREVENTS recurrence after antibiotic treatment by occupying the vacancy
```

**Evidence:**
- Wescombe 2012 Front Microbiol: S. salivarius K12 lozenges × 3 months → P. gingivalis in
  gingival crevicular fluid reduced 62% vs. placebo
- Scariya 2016 J Clin Periodontol: S. salivarius K12 as adjunct after SRP → reduction in P.
  gingivalis + T. denticola at 3 months superior to SRP alone (randomized, N=45)
- Di Pierro 2010 Otolaryngology Head Neck Surg: daily S. salivarius K12 lozenges → streptococcal
  pharyngitis recurrence �� 90% in children (primary trial; demonstrates colonization efficacy)

## M7 Protocol Integration: Suppression → Colonization Sequence

**The critical sequencing problem:**
After antibiotics (metronidazole + chlorhexidine, SRP) → oral biofilm disrupted → ecological
vacuum. THREE potential colonizers compete for the vacancy:
1. Red complex (P. gingivalis, T. denticola, T. forsythia) — present in saliva, recolonize fast
2. Health commensals (S. salivarius K12, oral Lactobacillus) — slower colonizers; need supplementation
3. Post-antibiotic opportunists (Candida albicans; antibiotic-associated oral candidiasis is
   common after metronidazole + chlorhexidine)

**Without active positive recolonization:**
Red complex recolonizes the ecological vacuum in ~6-12 weeks (from salivary reservoir + partner/
household transmission). The antibiotic treatment produces temporary reduction that does not persist.

**With S. salivarius K12 supplementation:**
```
Day 0-14: Metronidazole + SRP + chlorhexidine (SUPPRESS red complex)
Day 15 (= Day 1 post-antibiotics): START S. salivarius K12 lozenges
    → high S. salivarius K12 inoculum → colonizes tongue dorsum (ecological niche)
    → salivaricin B continuously produced → red complex cannot recolonize
    ↓
Month 1-3: S. salivarius K12 daily lozenge → consolidates colonization + prevents recolonization
    → P. gingivalis IgG should fall (less ongoing antigenic stimulation) at 3-month test
    → salivary PCR panel at 3 months: P. gingivalis/T. denticola/T. forsythia below threshold
```

**S. salivarius K12 + propolis mouthwash combination:**
Propolis mouthwash (run_036: M7 protocol, 2-3% BID) directly inhibits red complex (P. gingivalis
MIC 8-16 µg/mL). S. salivarius K12 lozenge (ecological competition). These are COMPLEMENTARY:
- Propolis: chemical inhibition (CAPE + chrysin antimicrobial)
- S. salivarius K12: competitive ecological exclusion via BLIS
- Together: both suppressive AND positive recolonization simultaneously

## S. salivarius K12 and the TLR2/sIgA Axis

**S. salivarius K12 → sIgA production:**
S. salivarius K12 → TLR2 (peptidoglycan) + TLR4 (low-level LPS) on MALT (mucosa-associated
lymphoid tissue) → MALT B cell activation → sIgA production directed against oral antigens.
Critically: sIgA induced by S. salivarius K12 CROSS-REACTS with P. gingivalis, T. denticola,
T. forsythia (shared peptidoglycan epitopes) → pre-existing sIgA level raised against red complex
by K12 colonization.

This is the POSITIVE version of the LGG mechanism (run_030 described LGG sIgA restoration in
GUT). For ORAL sIgA: S. salivarius K12 stimulates local oral MALT sIgA that targets red complex.
Same logic, different site.

**LGG (gut) + S. salivarius K12 (oral) = dual sIgA strategy:**
- LGG → gut sIgA → M1 barrier protection + M7 oral P. gingivalis gut extension blocked
- S. salivarius K12 → oral sIgA → M7 oral niche protected from red complex recolonization

## Dosing and Protocol

**S. salivarius K12 supplementation:**
- Form: BLIS K12 lozenges (Blis Technologies Ltd, New Zealand; licensed widely)
- Dose: 1 lozenge/day after evening teeth brushing (lozenges dissolve on tongue; colonization
  peaks after oral hygiene when competitive bacteria temporarily reduced by brushing)
- Duration: ongoing (prevent recurrence requires sustained colonization; K12 colonization
  natural rate ~30% without supplementation — daily lozenge boosts to ~85-90%)
- Cost: ~$15-25/month

**Timing in M7 sequence:**
Day 0-14: Metronidazole + SRP + chlorhexidine 0.12% BID (SUPPRESS)
Day 15: STOP chlorhexidine (chlorhexidine is bactericidal to ALL bacteria including K12 —
    cannot introduce K12 while chlorhexidine is active)
Day 15: START S. salivarius K12 lozenge daily (COLONIZE)
Concurrent throughout: propolis mouthwash BID (compatible with K12 — propolis is selective
    against gram-negative species; S. salivarius is gram-positive; propolis spares K12 while
    inhibiting red complex)

**Important:** STOP chlorhexidine before starting K12. Chlorhexidine kills K12. The transition
from chlorhexidine (suppression) to K12 (positive colonization) is the critical handoff point.
Propolis mouthwash (not chlorhexidine) is the ongoing maintenance antimicrobial compatible
with K12 colonization.

## Kill Criteria

**Kill A: S. salivarius K12 Colonization Does Not Persist After Lozenge Cessation**
The goal is long-term colonization, not just daily supplementation-dependent inhibition. If K12
only survives during active lozenge use and fails to establish a resident population, recurrence
will occur on cessation.
**Status:** Di Pierro 2010 showed persistent reduction in streptococcal pharyngitis 3 months
after K12 lozenge cessation (indirect evidence of persistent colonization). Wescombe 2012 shows
P. gingivalis reduction with K12 lozenges × 3 months. Long-term persistence data (>6 months
after cessation) is limited; daily lozenge maintenance is probably required in high-risk patients
with established red complex disease.

**Kill B: S. salivarius K12 Does Not Significantly Reduce T. forsythia in Human Clinical Studies**
The mechanism via salivaricin B is documented for P. gingivalis and T. denticola but the T.
forsythia evidence is less direct (Fusobacterium nucleatum inhibition → T. forsythia loses
its bridge colonizer).
**Status:** Not directly killed or confirmed — the indirect mechanism (F. nucleatum inhibition
→ T. forsythia loses plaque attachment) is plausible. Salivary PCR monitoring (OralDNA panel)
at 3 months would test this: expect P. gingivalis + T. denticola below threshold; T. forsythia
response is less certain.
