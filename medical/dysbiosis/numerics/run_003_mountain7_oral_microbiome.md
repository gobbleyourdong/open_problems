# Mountain 7 — Oral Dysbiosis
## Run 003 | Numerical Instance | 2026-04-11

> Question: is the oral microbiome a distinct mountain, or already covered by M1 (gut)?
> Finding: YES. Distinct site, distinct mechanism, distinct disease linkages. Mountain 7 warranted.

## Why Oral Dysbiosis is Distinct from M1 (Gut)

| Dimension | M1 (Gut) | Mountain 7 (Oral) |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Primary pathogen | Gram-neg LPS → TLR4 | P. gingivalis → TLR2 (unique) |
| Disease driver | Butyrate depletion, LPS translocation | Bacteremia, molecular mimicry |
| Receptor pathway | TLR4 / NF-κB | TLR2 / different cytokine profile |
| Autoimmune mechanism | Leaky gut → autoantigen exposure | Protein citrullination → ACPA |
| Key diseases | IBD, T1DM, metabolic syndrome | RA, CVD, Alzheimer's, preterm birth |
| Intervention | FMT, probiotics, dietary fiber | Scaling/root planing, chlorhexidine, xylitol |

**Critical distinction: TLR2 vs TLR4.** P. gingivalis LPS activates TLR2, not TLR4. This produces a Th17-skewed response rather than the Th1 response from TLR4/LPS. NF-κB-targeting interventions in the CVB protocol are partially effective against TLR4 but may be less effective against TLR2-driven Th17 inflammation. This is a gap.

## Mountain 7: Oral Dysbiosis → Systemic Disease via Bacteremia + Molecular Mimicry

**Claim:** The oral microbiome, when dysbiotic (periodontitis, gingivitis), produces:
1. Chronic low-grade bacteremia with immunosubversive organisms
2. Molecular mimicry via protein citrullination
3. Gut colonization from swallowed oral bacteria (~1.5L saliva/day)
...driving systemic disease beyond what is explained by gut/skin/virome mountains.

---

## Key Organism: Porphyromonas gingivalis

**Why P. gingivalis is unique:**
- Produces gingipain proteases that:
  - Cleave and inactivate complement (immune evasion)
  - Degrade innate immune proteins
  - Citrullinate host proteins (via bacterial PAD enzyme)
- Its LPS: activates TLR2 >> TLR4 (compared to E. coli LPS which is the opposite)
- TLR2 activation → NF-κB + MAPK → Th17 polarization → IL-17, IL-6, TNF-α
- **CTLA-4 mimic:** P. gingivalis FimA fimbriae activate CTLA-4 signaling → suppresses T cell activation → facilitates immune evasion while simultaneously producing chronic TLR2 stimulation

**Unique immunosubversion:** most periodontal bacteria produce local inflammation; P. gingivalis produces SYSTEMIC immune dysregulation with local persistence. This is why it's implicated in distant-site diseases.

## Gut Colonization from Swallowed Oral Bacteria

**Volume:** ~1.5L of saliva swallowed per day. Each mL contains ~10^7 bacteria.
Total oral bacteria swallowed: ~10^10 per day.

Most are killed by gastric acid (pH 1.5-3.5). But:
- H2 blockers or PPIs raise gastric pH → more oral bacteria survive into small intestine
- Fasting hypochlorhydria (after prolonged fasting) → transient higher survival
- Some oral bacteria ARE acid-tolerant (Streptococcus mutans, Lactobacillus sp.)

**Evidence of oral bacteria in gut:**
- Fusobacterium nucleatum (oral anaerobe) is the most overrepresented taxon in colorectal cancer microbiome. It's found in gut despite being primarily oral.
- P. gingivalis detected in gut biopsies (small studies)
- Systematic oral → gut colonization: an emerging concept (Olsen 2019)

**Disease implication:** If gut-resident P. gingivalis persists (especially in PPI users), it could produce TLR2 activation throughout the gut → different inflammatory tone than expected from standard gut dysbiosis models (which assume gram-negative rods from colon, not mouth).

## Gap in Current Protocol

The CVB protocol targets:
- OSBP (viral cholesterol delivery) → CVB block
- NLRP3 → NLRP3 inflammasome suppression
- NF-κB → NF-κB suppression (downstream of TLR4 primarily)

**What it does NOT target:**
- TLR2 (oral P. gingivalis LPS pathway)
- MAPK/AP-1 pathway (TLR2 downstream in addition to NF-κB)
- Protein citrullination (PAD enzyme activity)

**Gap:** if oral dysbiosis is active (periodontitis, even subclinical), the TLR2-Th17 pathway is contributing to the inflammatory load in a way the current protocol doesn't fully address.

## Mountain 7 Intervention Ladder

| Intervention | Evidence | Mechanism | Cost |
|-------------|----------|-----------|------|
| Scaling and root planing (professional) | Strong for RA, T2DM | Reduces P. gingivalis density + total bacterial load | $500-2000 |

| Berberine (oral rinse) | Low | P. gingivalis inhibition in vitro; clinical data limited | — |
| Gingipain inhibitor (COR388, experimental) | Phase 2/3 (AD indication) | Direct P. gingivalis virulence factor inhibition | Not available |

**User-actionable:** Chlorhexidine + xylitol + professional scaling (if not recent) covers most of the Mountain 7 intervention stack with minimal cost.
