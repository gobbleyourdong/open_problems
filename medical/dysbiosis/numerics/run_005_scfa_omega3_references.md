# PART A: SCFA Plasma Reference Ranges

## Published Plasma Concentrations (fasting, healthy adults)

| SCFA | Plasma concentration | Colonic production | Systemic bioavailability | GPR receptor |
|------|---------------------|-------------------|------------------------|--------------|
| **Acetate** | 50-200 µmol/L | ~60% of SCFA production | HIGH (~36% reaches portal; ~5% systemic) | GPR43, GPR41 |
| **Propionate** | 2-10 µmol/L | ~25% of SCFA production | MEDIUM (~9% portal; ~1% systemic) | GPR43, GPR41, GPR109A |
| **Butyrate** | 0.5-3 µmol/L | ~15% of SCFA production | LOW (<2% — consumed by colonocytes) | GPR109A, HDAC inhibitor |

**Sources:** Bloemen 2009, De Preter 2011, den Besten 2013 review.

---

## Why This Matters for the Gut-Skin Axis (Run 002, Mechanism 2)

The gut-skin SCFA transport pathway was rated "LOW-MODERATE" evidence in Run 002. Now quantified:

**Butyrate does NOT reach skin** in physiologically meaningful concentrations:
- 0.5-3 µmol/L plasma is ~1000× lower than concentrations used in cell culture studies (1-5 mmol/L for HDAC inhibition)
- Skin keratinocytes and immune cells are exposed to circulating butyrate at levels ~1000× below effective HDAC-inhibiting concentration
- **Conclusion: butyrate's immune effects are LOCAL (colonocyte, gut-lamina propria), not systemic**

**Propionate reaches systemic circulation at low levels:**
- 2-10 µmol/L systemic
- Cell culture minimum for GPR43 activation: ~0.3-1 µmol/L (Maslowski 2009)
- So propionate IS at or just above GPR43 activation threshold systemically
- This suggests propionate CAN signal via GPR43 on systemic immune cells — but the signal is weak

**Acetate is the primary systemic SCFA signal:**
- 50-200 µmol/L plasma — well above GPR43 activation threshold
- Produced by ALL gut bacteria (non-specific, not just butyrate producers)
- Crosses blood-brain barrier (role in appetite regulation, vagal signaling)
- GPR43 on neutrophils, macrophages, eosinophils, and potentially keratinocytes
- **Acetate is the SCFA most likely to carry a gut-skin signal**

**Revised Mechanism 2 (gut-skin SCFA) from Run 002:**
- "SCFA → skin immune cells" = primarily ACETATE story
- Acetate at 50-200 µmol/L → GPR43 on circulating immune cells → modest anti-inflammatory effect
- Butyrate: local gut only, not systemic
- Propionate: at threshold of GPR43 activation, signal is weak

**Clinical implication:** oral butyrate supplementation is PRIMARILY for gut mucosal immunity (colonocyte fuel, gut Treg induction). Don't expect it to have major skin effects directly. The gut-skin link goes through GALT immune education (Mechanism 3, Run 002) more than through SCFA.

| Test | What it measures | Availability | Cost |

| Plasma SCFA (GC-MS) | Acetate, propionate, butyrate in blood | Research/specialty | $100-200 |
| Stool SCFA (GC-MS) | All SCFAs in stool | Genova CDSA, specialty | $150-250 |
| Breath hydrogen/methane | Indirect fermentation proxy | Clinical, SIBO testing | $100-150 |
| Organic acids urine panel | Some SCFA metabolites | Genova, Great Plains | $100-200 |

**Stool SCFA vs plasma SCFA:** stool SCFA reflects production by gut bacteria; plasma SCFA reflects what reaches systemic circulation. For gut health: stool SCFA is more informative. For skin/systemic effects: plasma acetate is the relevant measurement.

**Most useful for this framework:** stool butyrate + plasma acetate combination. But this requires two separate tests.

**Proxy (cheaper):** HUMAnN3 functional analysis of FASTQ → butyryl-CoA:acetate CoA-transferase gene abundance → proxy for butyrate PRODUCTION capacity even if we don't measure the SCFA directly.

# PART B: Omega-3 Index Dose-Response

## Target: Omega-3 Index >8% (EPA+DHA as % of RBC total fatty acids)

## Published Dose-Response Data

**Harris & Von Schacky 2004 (Preventive Medicine):**
- Baseline US population mean: ~4-5%
- Dose-response at 12 weeks:
  - 1g EPA+DHA/day → +1.0-1.5% (to ~5.5%)
  - 2g EPA+DHA/day → +2.0-2.5% (to ~6.5%)
  - 3.4g EPA+DHA/day → +3.0-3.5% (to ~7.5-8%)
  - 4g EPA+DHA/day → +3.5-4.0% (to ~8-9%)

**Stonehouse 2013 (AJCN):** 1.16g DHA/day (without much EPA) → +2.3% after 6 months. DHA raises index; EPA raises index similarly per gram.

**Key variables:**
- EPA + DHA together: more effect per gram than either alone
- Body weight matters: higher BMI → lower absorption efficiency → need more
- Fish form vs supplement form: triglyceride-form fish oil absorbs 25-70% better than ethyl ester form (most supplements). Krill oil: phospholipid form, higher bioavailability at lower doses.
- Baseline matters: starting at 3% requires more supplementation than starting at 5% to hit 8%

## Omega-3 × Vitamin D Synergy (New Interaction — Run 003 follow-up)

The vitamin D - omega-3 interaction is not just additive:
- DHA → SPMs (specialized pro-resolving mediators): resolvins D1-D6
- Resolvin D synthesis requires: DHA (substrate) + 15-LOX enzyme (aspirin-acetylated COX-2 can also do this) + 5-LOX
- BUT: resolvin D pathway is UPREGULATED by vitamin D (Willems 2020 — VDR activation increases 15-LOX expression)
- Result: the anti-inflammatory benefit of DHA supplementation is PARTIALLY gated by vitamin D status

**Interaction:** low D + adequate DHA → less resolvin D production → less anti-inflammatory benefit from omega-3 supplementation. The same zinc×D logic applies: omega-3 effectiveness is partly gated by vitamin D status.

**T-index update:** this creates a three-way interaction: zinc × vitamin D × omega-3. All three must be adequate for the anti-inflammatory pathways to function optimally. Each deficiency impairs the others.
