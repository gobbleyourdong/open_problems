## Mechanism 1: Systemic Inflammatory Tone (LPS → cytokines → skin threshold)

```
Gut dysbiosis
     ↓
LPS translocation (tight junction failure, mucin degradation)
     ↓
Portal circulation → liver TLR4 → NF-κB → acute phase proteins
     ↓
Systemic cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β) in circulation
     ↓
Skin: TLR4 on keratinocytes, dermal macrophages activated
     ↓
Lowers threshold for local Malassezia/Demodex → disease
```

**Evidence tier:** MODERATE
- LPS in circulation: documented in obesity, T2DM, metabolic syndrome at levels of 50-150 pg/mL
- TLR4 on keratinocytes: well established (Lebre 2007)
- Human gut-to-skin inflammatory priming: demonstrated in germ-free mice (reconstitution with dysbiotic microbiome → skin inflammation). Human: indirect only.

**Intervention data:**
- Dietary intervention (low-GI, high-fiber): reduces LPS exposure + gut dysbiosis → skin improvement (acne, atopic derm: small studies)
- FMT: occasional reports of eczema improvement after gut FMT. Case series, not RCT.
- Oral probiotic: LGG, Lactobacillus rhamnosus studies in atopic derm — ~50% reduction in SCORAD vs placebo in some trials (Kalliomäki 2001, Lancet). But effect size heterogeneous.

**Bottleneck:** Does systemic LPS at non-sepsis levels (50-150 pg/mL) actually cross into skin and prime TLR4? Plasma LPS is largely bound to LBP; free LPS may not reach skin in disease-relevant concentrations. This is the gap.

**Verdict:** PLAUSIBLE, partially supported, magnitude of effect uncertain.

## Mechanism 2: Direct SCFA Transport (butyrate/propionate/acetate → skin immune cells)

```
Gut dysbiosis → loss of butyrate producers
     ↓
Less butyrate, propionate, acetate in gut lumen
     ↓
Acetate (most systemically bioavailable) enters portal circulation
Propionate: intermediate bioavailability
Butyrate: mostly consumed by colonocytes (<5 µmol/L in plasma)
     ↓
Circulating acetate + propionate → skin immune cells (via GPR43/GPR41)
     ↓
HDAC inhibition → FOXP3 expression → skin Treg induction
     ↓
Higher local tolerance threshold → less skin disease
```

**Evidence tier:** LOW-MODERATE
- Plasma acetate: 100-300 µmol/L (detectable)
- Plasma propionate: 2-20 µmol/L (lower but systemic)
- Plasma butyrate: <5 µmol/L (mostly consumed by colonocytes; very little systemic)
- GPR43/GPR41 on immune cells (including skin): confirmed receptor expression
- SCFA → HDAC inhibition → Treg in gut: strong. Systemic delivery to SKIN: animal data only (germ-free models)

**Intervention data:**
- Oral butyrate supplementation for skin disease: no published RCT
- Sodium butyrate enema for IBD → improved gut but skin effect not studied
- Tributyrin (butyrate prodrug) → better systemic bioavailability than oral sodium butyrate

**Bottleneck:** Butyrate is almost entirely consumed before reaching systemic circulation. Acetate reaches skin but its local Treg-inductive capacity relative to systemic gut production is not established.

**Verdict:** Mechanistically plausible for acetate/propionate; low evidence for skin-level effect specifically. Underexplored.

## Mechanism 3: Immune Education (GALT → T cell calibration → skin reactivity)

```
Gut dysbiosis
     ↓
GALT (Peyer's patches, mesenteric lymph nodes, lamina propria DC) is dysregulated
     ↓
T cell education skewed: less Treg induction, more Th17 polarization
     ↓
Skewed T cells enter circulation and home to sites including skin
     ↓
Skin T cell repertoire is Th17-skewed → lower tolerance threshold for skin microbes
     ↓
Same microbial density → disease, in gut-immune-educated host
```

**Evidence tier:** MODERATE
- Gut T cell education: established mechanism in mouse models
- α4β7 integrin: gut-homing receptor, sends cells primarily back to gut
- CLA (cutaneous lymphocyte antigen): skin-homing marker. Gut-educated T cells expressing CLA: documented but quantified poorly
- GALT dysregulation in IBD → systemic T cell changes → skin manifestations (extraintestinal manifestations of IBD include pyoderma gangrenosum, psoriasis, erythema nodosum — supporting immune education pathway)

**Key clinical evidence for this mechanism:**
- IBD extraintestinal manifestations: ~25-40% of IBD patients develop skin/joint/eye disease. This is gut-immune → skin pathway in action.
- Children with gut dysbiosis (antibiotic exposure) → higher atopic dermatitis rates: consistent with immune education pathway
- Psoriasis and IBD co-occurrence: ~6× higher in IBD vs general population

**Verdict:** SUPPORTED by IBD extraintestinal manifestation data. The strongest indirect human evidence for gut-skin immune communication.

## Mechanism 4: Microbiome Migration (gut bacteria → skin colonization)

```
Gut bacteria escape gut (gut permeability + M1)
     ↓
Enter bloodstream (bacteremia)
     ↓
Seed skin or other sites
     ↓
Alter skin microbiome composition
```

**Evidence tier:** LOW
- S. aureus in gut → skin: S. aureus colonizes gut and skin concurrently in atopic derm patients. Timing unclear.
- Candida in gut → systemic candidiasis in immunocompromised: established. In healthy hosts: extremely rare.
- Gut bacteria on skin: may reflect perineal contamination or manual transfer, not hematogenous seeding
- Bacteremia from gut in healthy adults: detectable transiently after vigorous exercise, but immune clearance is rapid

**Verdict:** Not a primary mechanism for dysbiosis-linked skin disease in immunocompetent hosts. Relevant in immunocompromised only.

## Mechanism 5: Vagus Nerve / HPA Axis (gut → CNS → stress → cortisol → skin)

```
Gut microbiome produces neurotransmitters (GABA, serotonin precursors, short-chain FA)
     ↓
Signals via vagus nerve (afferent → NTS → brain)
     ↓
Hypothalamus → HPA activation → CRH → ACTH → cortisol
     ↓
Cortisol: immunosuppression, skin sebum regulation, mast cell sensitization
     ↓
Skin: altered inflammatory tone, sebum production, barrier function
```

**Evidence tier:** MODERATE for the gut-brain connection; LOW for skin endpoint specifically
- Gut microbiome → vagus nerve signaling: established (afferent vagal fibers carry gut signals)
- Germ-free mice have altered HPA axis; specific bacteria normalize it (Sudo 2004)
- Lactobacillus rhamnosus → reduced anxiety-like behavior in mice via vagal-dependent mechanism (Bravo 2011)
- Cortisol → rosacea flare: documented (stress triggers rosacea)
- Cortisol → acne worsening: documented
- But: is the magnitude of cortisol effect from gut microbiome changes (vs psychological stress) clinically significant? Not studied.

**Verdict:** Real pathway but magnitude of gut microbiome contribution to cortisol-mediated skin effects is unknown. Likely secondary to direct psychological stress effects.

## Mechanism 6: Histamine / Mast Cell Axis

```
Gut bacteria produce histamine (from histidine decarboxylase)
     ↓
Gut lumen histamine → absorbed (if DAO deficient) → enters portal circulation
     ↓
Circulating histamine → mast cell sensitization / degranulation
     ↓
Skin mast cells activated → vasodilation, itch, inflammatory mediator release
     ↓
Rosacea flushing, urticaria, eczema exacerbation
```

**Evidence tier:** LOW-MODERATE
- Histamine-producing gut bacteria: Morganella morganii, Klebsiella pneumoniae, some Lactobacillus spp.
- DAO (diamine oxidase): degrades histamine in gut wall. Deficiency → more histamine absorption
- DAO deficiency associated with: food-triggered urticaria, migraine, IBD
- Gut-derived histamine → skin: theoretically possible but most histamine is degraded before systemic circulation in DAO-sufficient individuals

**Intervention data:**
- Low-histamine diet → improvement in histamine intolerance symptoms (urticaria, flushing)
- DAO supplementation → some improvement in histamine intolerance
- Relevance to seb derm/rosacea: rosacea flushing partly mast-cell mediated → DAO status is relevant

**Verdict:** Secondary mechanism. Relevant for flushing/urticaria-prominent rosacea. Not a primary dysbiosis mechanism.

## Dominant Mechanism Hypothesis

**From pattern in intervention data:**
- Probiotics improving atopic derm → Mechanisms 1, 3 (GALT immune education) are most consistent
- IBD extraintestinal skin manifestations → Mechanism 3 (immune education) is strongest
- LPS → TLR4 → skin is mechanistically compelling but human magnitude not established
- SCFA direct transport to skin: probably minor contribution (butyrate doesn't reach systemic circulation)

**Best current bet:** Mechanism 3 (GALT immune education) is the primary gut-skin axis pathway, with Mechanism 1 (LPS inflammatory priming) as secondary amplifier.

**Mechanism 6 (histamine):** specifically relevant for the user's rosacea context. Rosacea flushing = mast cell mediated. DAO status worth checking.

## Testable Prediction

**If Mechanism 3 is dominant:** Gut interventions that improve GALT function (prebiotics → GALT T cell education) should improve skin disease independently of LPS levels or SCFA concentrations.

**Minimal test:** In atopic derm patients, compare:
- Probiotic (shifts composition, affects GALT education)
- Butyrate supplement alone (SCFA direct)
- Neither

If probiotic > butyrate supplement for skin outcome → Mechanism 3 > Mechanism 2. This study doesn't exist.
