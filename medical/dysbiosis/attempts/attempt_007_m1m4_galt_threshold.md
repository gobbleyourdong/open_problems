## Phase 3 Coupled Attack | 2026-04-11

---

## Mountain
Sky Bridge: M1 (gut dysbiosis → systemic inflammation) ↔ M4 (host-microbe threshold / THE WALL)

---

## Hypothesis
Gut dysbiosis produces a population of gut-primed Th17 cells that acquire skin-homing
properties (CLA+/CCR4+) in mesenteric lymph nodes under IL-23 inflammatory signaling.
These cells traffic to skin-draining lymph nodes, deplete local Tregs, and LOWER the threshold
for pathogen-triggered skin inflammatory disease. M4 (the host-microbe threshold) is not
only an intrinsic, static host property — it is partly a dynamic, gut-modifiable parameter
that changes with gut inflammatory activity.

---

## Evidence Base

**IBD + skin EIM epidemiology:**
- Psoriasis in CD: 9.6-11.2% vs 2.2% population controls (4.4× enrichment, PMC5683131)
- UC psoriasis: 5.7% vs 2.2% controls (2.6× enrichment)
- Meta-analysis (PMID 37042969, n=352,454): 24% of IBD patients have ≥1 EIM

**Mechanistic evidence — T cell trafficking:**
- Review (PMID 38654394): gut-primed CD4+ T cells expressing α4β7/CCR9 acquire CLA/CCR4 under inflammatory conditions in mesenteric lymph nodes
- DSS colitis mice: expanded circulating CD4+ cells co-expressing gut AND skin homing markers
- Same IL-23/Th17 axis drives both IBD and psoriasis (PMC5683131)
- "Acute systemic inflammation alone does not trigger skin-specific responses" — T cell recruitment is required

**Skin Treg depletion evidence:**
- Gut-microbe-responsive T cells traffic to skin-draining lymph nodes → deplete skin Tregs (review PMID 38654394)
- Skin Treg depletion = raised susceptibility to Malassezia/Demodex-triggered disease
- This is the M4 THRESHOLD MECHANISM — a gut-derived mechanism

**I-FABP (enterocyte damage) + psoriasis:**
- I-FABP 243 vs 114 pg/mL in psoriasis vs controls (p<0.001)
- r=0.78 with PASI severity score (PMID 31336842)
- Gut enterocyte damage quantitatively correlates with skin disease severity — the strongest human evidence for gut-skin connection

**Anti-LPS argument (what rules OUT LPS as primary mechanism):**
- LBP (LPS surrogate) NOT elevated in isolated psoriasis patients (only elevated in psoriasis + metabolic syndrome)
- This rules LPS out as the primary gut-skin link in clean psoriasis

## Mechanistic Chain

```
Gut dysbiosis (F. prausnitzii depletion, Akkermansia depletion, LPS-producing gram-negatives ↑)
    ↓
Mucosal IL-23 elevation → GALT activation
    ↓
Mesenteric lymph node: naive CD4+ T cells differentiate into Th17 (IL-6 + TGF-β + IL-23)
    ↓ [KEY REPROGRAMMING STEP]
Under sustained gut inflammation: Th17 cells acquire DUAL HOMING capability
    α4β7/CCR9 (gut) + CLA/CCR4 (skin)
    ↓
These gut-origin Th17 cells exit into systemic circulation
    ↓
Traffic to skin-draining lymph nodes (axillary, inguinal, cervical)
    ↓
Deplete local skin Tregs → Th17 > Treg ratio shifts in skin
    ↓
M4 THRESHOLD LOWERED:
  - Demodex density previously tolerated → now triggers rosacea
  - Malassezia density previously tolerated → now triggers seb derm
  - C. acnes density → now triggers inflammatory acne
    ↓
Site-specific dysbiosis disease activates WITHOUT change in pathogen density
```

## Why This Is a Phase 3 Finding

This could NOT have been generated from either mountain alone:

**From M1 alone (gut dysbiosis → systemic LPS):**
Would generate: elevated systemic TLR4 activation. Would NOT specifically explain
skin disease enrichment in IBD (LPS should elevate inflammation everywhere, not specifically skin).

**From M4 alone (host threshold):**
Would describe: threshold exists, probably innate immune. Would NOT generate
the mechanism by which threshold changes over time. The threshold would appear static.

**From M1 + M4 coupling:**
GALT-primed T cell trafficking is the SPECIFIC mechanism that sets the DYNAMIC threshold.
This is the new insight: M4 is not a fixed genetic floor — it is a CURRENT STATE that is
partly driven by gut dysbiosis activity (M1). Treat gut → raise threshold → prevent skin disease.

## Novel Testable Predictions

### Prediction A — IL-23 Blocker Co-Treatment
IBD patients randomized to IL-23 blocker (guselkumab / risankizumab) + standard IBD therapy vs
standard IBD therapy alone:
- Primary: IBD remission rate
- New secondary: skin Treg frequency at 3 months
- New secondary: skin inflammatory disease rate / severity

**If IL-23 blocker raises skin Treg frequency alongside reducing IBD activity →** the shared
IL-23/Th17 axis is the coupling mechanism, confirmed in humans.

**Partial data already available:** Guselkumab and risankizumab are approved for both
psoriasis AND IBD (separately). Whether combined gut+skin outcomes are tracked in clinical
trials should be checked. If yes, retrospective analysis is feasible.

### Prediction B — I-FABP as Threshold Predictor
In patients with skin dysbiosis disease (rosacea, seb derm) with documented high pathogen
density (Demodex, Malassezia) but variable disease activity:
- Measure I-FABP (enterocyte damage marker) and serum IL-17A
- Hypothesis: disease-active patients have higher I-FABP than disease-tolerant high-density carriers

This directly tests whether gut damage (→ Th17 trafficking → skin Treg depletion) predicts
who crosses the threshold from tolerant carrier to active disease.

**This is the M4 non-responder question** — the 20-25% who don't respond to antifungal/antiparasitic
monotherapy. Are they high I-FABP? If yes, the missing treatment is gut-targeted.

### Prediction C — Gut Treatment Raises Skin Threshold
Intensive gut intervention (FMT or intensive fiber/prebiotic protocol) in patients with:
- Active skin dysbiosis disease (rosacea or seb derm)
- Documented gut dysbiosis (F. prausnitzii depleted)
- No change in topical/oral antifungal/antiparasitic treatment

Outcome: skin disease improves without direct skin treatment → gut intervention raised threshold.

**If confirmed:** gut therapy becomes a primary (not adjunctive) intervention for skin disease.
This inverts the current treatment paradigm (treat skin → maybe add gut as adjunct).

## Kill Criteria

**Kill A:** IBD patients in clinical remission (confirmed mucosal healing) have IDENTICAL skin
Treg frequency and skin disease rates as matched non-IBD controls. (Would mean the T-cell
trafficking mechanism is not operating at clinically relevant scale.)

**Kill B:** I-FABP in rosacea/seb derm patients is NOT different between disease-active and
tolerant high-density carrier groups. (Would mean gut enterocyte damage is not the link.)

**Kill C:** IL-23 blocker reduces IBD without reducing concurrent skin inflammatory disease.
(Would mean IBD and skin disease are driven by different IL-23 pools, not shared.)
