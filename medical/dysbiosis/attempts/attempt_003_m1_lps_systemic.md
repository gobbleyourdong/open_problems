## Mountain
M1 — Gut dysbiosis → systemic inflammation (LPS / leaky gut)

## Hypothesis
Gut permeability increase (tight junction failure + mucin degradation) allows LPS translocation at physiologically significant levels that chronically activate TLR4 on systemic immune cells and skin keratinocytes, lowering the inflammatory threshold for ALL site-specific dysbiosis diseases (M2, M3, M4).

## Evidence Base (from numerics)
- `run_001_kill_matrix.md`: M1 predictions — P1.3 (butyrate-producer loss) = 3/3 FOR; P1.6 (LPS→TLR4→NF-κB) = 3/3 FOR. Weakest: P1.2 (zonulin) KILLED.
- `run_002_gut_skin_axis_tree.md`: Mechanism 1 (LPS→systemic→skin) rated MODERATE evidence.
- `run_004_phageome_and_gut_permeability.md`: Zonulin killed (measures C3, not permeability). L:M ratio is the replacement test.

## Mechanistic Chain
```
F. prausnitzii / Akkermansia depletion (modern diet, antibiotics)
     ↓
Butyrate reduction → colonocyte fuel deficit → tight junction protein downregulation
Mucin layer thinning → microbial access to epithelium increases
     ↓
Gram-negative LPS crosses gut wall (paracellular route)
     ↓
Portal circulation → liver TLR4 → NF-κB → LBP, acute phase proteins
Systemic circulation → immune cells, skin keratinocytes TLR4
     ↓
Systemic inflammatory tone raised → lower threshold for site-specific disease (M4)
```

## Kill Test
**Kill criterion:** In humans with documented high F. prausnitzii depletion (from stool metagenomics), plasma LBP and FABP2 (enterocyte damage marker) are NOT elevated compared to F. prausnitzii-replete controls.

**Best available test now:** Genova L:M ratio + plasma FABP2 + LBP panel. If permeability is normal in dysbiotic host → LPS translocation hypothesis weakens significantly.

**Status: UNTESTED at the individual level for this user.**

## Evidence FOR: 2/3
F. prausnitzii loss is real and consistent. LPS→TLR4→NF-κB is textbook. Plasma LPS in metabolic syndrome: documented. Causal arrow for systemic permeability → distant-site disease: animal data strong, human controlled data weak.

## Evidence AGAINST: 1/3
Plasma LPS at non-sepsis levels (50-150 pg/mL) reaching skin at disease-relevant concentrations: not established. Zonulin assay that powered many "leaky gut" papers was measuring C3. Magnitude of human gut-to-skin LPS effect is unknown.

## Current Status
[x] ACTIVE — partially validated but causal magnitude uncertain

## Stall Point
The specific question "how much systemic LPS is needed to prime skin TLR4 meaningfully?" has no published human answer. Animal models use much higher LPS doses than what's measured in human plasma. This magnitude question is the stall.

IBD patients have documented gut permeability increases — do their SKIN conditions correlate with gut permeability markers (L:M ratio, LBP)? If yes, this establishes the human gut-skin LPS link at the population level.
