# attempt_005 — Sex Hormones and Thymic Involution: The Castration-Reverses-Aging Story

> Fifth attempt. attempt_004 established that thymic involution
> begins in early childhood (Steinmann 1985 — pre-puberty) and that
> FOXN1 decline drives it. But puberty ACCELERATES involution
> dramatically, and one of the cleanest natural experiments in
> regeneration biology is what happens when sex hormones are removed
> from adults. Castration regenerates the thymus. So does chemical
> castration. This was first shown in mice in the 1990s and
> demonstrated in humans (prostate-cancer patients on LHRH analogs)
> in the 2000s. The mechanism is direct: androgen receptor signaling
> in thymic epithelial cells suppresses thymopoiesis.

---

## The classical observation

Removing the gonads (surgical castration) of adult male rodents
causes their atrophied thymus to regrow — back toward juvenile size
and function. This was known before molecular mechanism was
characterized.

The reverse experiment also works: administer testosterone to a
castrated animal, and the thymus involutes again.

The same effect is observed for females: ovariectomy (estrogen
withdrawal) causes thymic regrowth.

These were the first clean demonstrations that the thymus retains
regenerative competence into adulthood — the molecular machinery is
intact; it's being actively suppressed by sex hormones.

## Where the receptors are

The critical question for mechanism: ARE androgen receptors on
thymic epithelial cells (TECs) themselves, or on the developing
thymocytes?

Definitive answer came from chimera experiments using **testicular
feminization (Tfm)** mice — these have a non-functional androgen
receptor due to a mutation. Bone marrow chimeras were constructed:

- Wild-type stroma + Tfm hematopoietic cells: thymus responds
  normally to castration / androgen
- Tfm stroma + wild-type hematopoietic cells: thymus is androgen-
  insensitive (does not involute on androgen administration; does
  not regrow on castration)

**Conclusion**: androgen receptors on TECs (epithelial), not on
thymocytes themselves, are the responsive element.

Reference: **Olsen NJ, Olson G, Viselli SM, Gu X, Kovacs WJ (2001).**
"Androgen receptors in thymic epithelium modulate thymus size and
thymocyte development." *Endocrinology* 142:1278-1283. PMID 11181545.

Conditional knockout studies have since refined this:

**Velardi E et al. (2014).** "Sex steroid blockade enhances thymopoiesis
by modulating Notch signaling." *Journal of Experimental Medicine*
211:2341-2349. PMID 25332287. Showed androgen receptor-mediated
suppression of DLL4 (the master Notch ligand for T cell commitment;
see attempt_003) is one of the mechanisms by which androgens
involute the thymus.

**Velardi E et al. (2020).** Confirmed via E-ARKO (epithelial cell-
specific androgen receptor knockout) that AR loss specifically in
TECs increases thymus weight, T cell progenitor numbers, and recent
thymic emigrants.

## The Sutherland & Boyd landmark paper

The seminal paper translating this from mice to humans:

**Sutherland JS, Goldberg GL, Hammett MV, Uldrich AP, Berzins SP,
Heng TS, Blazar BR, Millar JL, Malin MA, Chidgey AP, Boyd RL (2005).**
"Activation of thymic regeneration in mice and humans following
androgen blockade." *Journal of Immunology* 175:2741-2753. PMID
16081852.

What they did:
- **Mouse arm**: aged mice given LHRH agonist (chemical castration).
  Showed thymic regeneration paralleling surgical castration.
- **Human arm**: prostate cancer patients on LHRH analog therapy
  (standard care for advanced prostate cancer; chemically castrates
  via downregulation of pituitary LHRH receptors, eliminating
  testosterone production within 2-4 weeks).

What they found in humans:
- Increased thymic mass on CT imaging
- Increased sjTREC content in peripheral blood T cells (recent
  thymic emigrants)
- Increased naive T cell percentages
- Effect detectable within months of starting LHRH analog therapy

This was the FIRST clean demonstration that thymic regeneration in
humans is achievable in adults and old age. The patients didn't
volunteer for thymic regeneration; they were getting prostate cancer
treatment. The thymic regrowth was a measurable side effect.

## Mechanism: how androgens suppress thymopoiesis

Several levels:

### Direct AR signaling in TECs

- Androgens bind AR in cTECs and mTECs.
- AR translocates to nucleus and modulates gene expression.
- Suppresses key developmental genes including DLL4 (Notch ligand).
- Reduces TEC proliferation and FOXN1 expression.

### Indirect effects via systemic IL-22 reduction

Androgens reduce IL-22 production (cross-reference to attempt_006).
IL-22 is a critical TEC-trophic cytokine. Lower IL-22 = lower TEC
support.

### Suppression of bone marrow lymphopoiesis

Androgens reduce common lymphoid progenitor output from bone marrow,
reducing thymic precursor input.

### Sex differences in baseline

- Males have larger early-life thymus but also faster involution.
- Females retain thymic function modestly longer.
- Both sexes regenerate thymus on hormone withdrawal.

## Estrogens

The female counterpart: estrogens (estradiol primarily) ALSO
suppress thymopoiesis.

- Ovariectomy in adult female mice causes thymic regrowth.
- Estrogen replacement reverses this.
- Estrogen receptors (ERα and ERβ) are present in TECs.
- Pregnancy-associated estrogen surge causes transient thymic
  involution in pregnant mammals (then recovery postpartum).

References:
- **Zoller AL, Kersh GJ (2006).** "Estrogen induces thymic atrophy
  by eliminating early thymic progenitors and inhibiting
  proliferation of beta-selected thymocytes." *J Immunol* 176:
  7371-7378.
- **Erlandsson MC et al. (2010).** "Survival of mature T cells
  inhibition by 17beta-estradiol contributes to thymic involution."
  *Endocrinology* 151:1-10.

The pregnancy-related thymic atrophy is one of the most dramatic
documented hormonal effects: a peripartum mouse can lose ~80% of
thymic mass over gestation, with rapid recovery after birth.

## DHEA: a partial counter-hormone

Dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), the most abundant adrenal androgen,
declines with age in parallel with thymic function. Some evidence:

- DHEA supplementation modestly improves thymic indicators in older
  adults
- DHEA was a component of the TRIIM trial (attempt_010) along with
  growth hormone and metformin
- DHEA's effects in the thymus partially counter testosterone's
  effects (different downstream metabolism)

The TRIIM result included DHEA's thymic contribution; the relative
weight of DHEA vs growth hormone vs metformin in the regeneration is
not cleanly partitionable from the trial design.

## Clinical implications

### Established / observed in patients

- **Prostate cancer patients on LHRH agonists** show measurable
  thymic regeneration (Sutherland 2005 + multiple follow-ups).
  This is incidental to the cancer treatment; not pursued for
  immune benefit.
- **Hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) recipients** given
  LHRH antagonists periTransplant show faster T cell reconstitution
  and reduced infection risk in some trials.
- **HIV patients** (with profound CD4+ T cell loss) have been
  considered candidates for LHRH-based thymic regeneration; small
  studies have shown some effect.

### Trials specifically for regeneration (not as cancer treatment)

- **Several Phase 1/2 trials** of LHRH agonists for thymic
  regeneration in HSCT recipients have shown enhanced T cell
  recovery.
- **Trials in elderly subjects** for general immune enhancement
  have been small and not definitive.

### Why it isn't standard care

Despite clean evidence:
- Side effects of long-term androgen deprivation (hot flashes,
  bone loss, sexual dysfunction, cardiovascular risk) are
  significant.
- A regeneration intervention is hard to justify in healthy
  individuals when the harms accrue immediately and the immune
  benefit is gradual.
- Selective androgen receptor modulators (SARMs) that could
  block AR specifically in TECs without systemic anti-androgen
  effects don't yet exist.

## The TEC-specific question

A major clinical wish: a TEC-specific androgen receptor antagonist
that would regenerate thymus WITHOUT systemic androgen blockade.

Why this is hard:
- AR is the same protein everywhere it's expressed.
- Tissue-specific AR modulators (SARMs) exist for muscle/bone but
  TEC-specificity hasn't been achieved.
- Conditional knockouts in mice (E-ARKO) work because they target
  the gene, not the protein. Pharmacology can't replicate this.

## What this leaves open

- **Estrogen blockade for thymic regeneration in women**: aromatase
  inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole) block estrogen synthesis;
  used in breast cancer. Some thymic regeneration has been
  reported but data are limited.
- **Combined androgen + estrogen blockade**: would be expected to
  be more effective than either alone but increases adverse events.
- **Targeted delivery of AR antagonists to thymic stroma**:
  experimental.

## Adverse considerations

Restoring thymic function is not unambiguously good:

- Thymus produces both **conventional T cells** (good for infection
  defense) and **autoreactive T cells if central tolerance is
  imperfect** (bad).
- LHRH-mediated regeneration restores AIRE+ mTECs in mice (per
  Velardi 2014); whether human regeneration is equally complete on
  the tolerance side is partly open.
- In specific patient populations (HSCT recipients), faster T cell
  recovery is uniformly beneficial. In healthy elderly, the
  risk-benefit of restoring thymic output is less clear.

This connects back to attempt_002's note: any thymic regeneration
intervention needs central-tolerance restoration to be safe.

## Mechanistic summary

```
Sex hormones (testosterone, estradiol) bind AR/ERα in TECs
                ↓
TEC FOXN1, DLL4, IL-7, KGF expression suppressed
                ↓
Reduced thymopoiesis
                ↓
Reduced T cell output

REMOVE the hormone (castration / LHRH analog / aromatase inhibitor)
                ↓
TEC FOXN1, DLL4 re-express
                ↓
Thymus regrows
                ↓
Increased naive T cell output (sjTREC measurable)
```

The chain is well-characterized in mice and demonstrated in humans
via the prostate cancer natural experiment.

## Open at this stage

- Mechanistic detail of how AR suppresses DLL4: direct
  transcriptional or indirect?
- Whether estrogen and androgen pathways converge at the same TEC
  target genes
- Optimal duration and dose of LHRH analog for thymic regeneration
  WITHOUT serious adverse effects
- Whether DHEA's apparent thymic-supportive effect is via
  testosterone-precursor conversion in TECs or via a distinct
  mechanism
