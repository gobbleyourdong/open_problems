# attempt_004 — Thymic Involution: Trajectory and Mechanisms

> Fourth attempt in the thymus thread. attempts 001-003 covered
> what the thymus IS (anatomy), what it DOES (selection), and the
> CHEMISTRY of how it works. This attempt covers what HAPPENS to it
> over time: the human-specific dramatic involution starting in
> early childhood, accelerating at puberty, and continuing through
> old age. Mechanisms include FOXN1 downregulation, TEC progenitor
> exhaustion, mTEC subset loss, ROS accumulation, adipose
> replacement, and chronic inflammation feedback.

---

## The trajectory

The thymus is the FIRST major organ in the human body to begin
visible age-related decline. It has been losing functional
capacity since age 1.

### Early-life peak

- **Embryonic origin**: pharyngeal-pouch derivative, structurally
  complete by ~week 16-20 of gestation
- **Peak relative size**: birth to age 1, the thymus is at its
  largest relative to body weight
- **Absolute peak**: around age 12-15 (~30-40 g organ mass)

### The Steinmann landmark study

**Steinmann GG, Klaus B, Müller-Hermelink HK (1985).** "The
involution of the ageing human thymic epithelium is independent of
puberty: a morphometric study." *Scandinavian Journal of
Immunology* 22:563-575.

Key findings (this is THE classic morphometric study):

- The **true thymic epithelial space (TES)** — where actual
  thymopoiesis occurs — is distinct from the **perivascular space
  (PVS)** which is non-epithelial, non-thymopoietic.
- TES volume DECLINES continuously from year 1 to end of life.
- PVS volume INCREASES (gets filled by adipocytes and connective
  tissue).
- The decline in TES is **independent of puberty** — it begins
  before sexual maturation and continues through it. (Sex hormones
  contribute but don't initiate.)
- TES volume halves approximately every ~16 years from age 1.

### Quantitative trajectory

| Age (years) | Thymopoietic mass (% of peak) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 100 |
| 10 | ~70 |
| 20 | ~50 |
| 35 | ~25 |
| 50 | ~10 |
| 70+ | <5 |

Functional thymic output (measured by sjTREC content of peripheral
T cells; see attempt_003):

- At age 25: ~10⁹ new naive T cells per year
- At age 50: ~10% of that
- At age 70+: ~1% of peak — essentially marginal

By the age of 60-70, the human thymus is histologically dominated
by adipocytes with small islands of residual thymopoietic activity.

## Cross-species comparison

The thymus involutes in all mammals, but rates differ:

- **Mice**: rapid relative to lifespan. Peak at ~4-6 weeks; substantial
  decline by 6-12 months.
- **Rats**: similar.
- **Dogs, cats, sheep, cattle**: peak at sexual maturity; decline
  to ~25% by mid-life.
- **Humans**: dramatic early decline (1-15) followed by long, slow
  late decline. Reduced more dramatically than other mammals when
  scaled to lifespan.
- **Long-lived mammals (whales, elephants)**: data scarce, but
  apparent slower involution rates (proportional to lifespan).

In NON-mammalian vertebrates (covered in biology/evolution/attempt_104
+ 105):
- Many fish show seasonal thymus fluctuations, not pure age decline
- Some long-lived sharks retain substantial thymic function in old age
- Reptiles show temperature- and reproduction-linked thymus changes

The mammalian dramatic-involution pattern is not the universal
vertebrate norm — it's a mammalian life-history feature.

## Cellular mechanisms

### The central question

What is being LOST during involution? Three not-mutually-exclusive
candidates:

1. **TEC numbers**: thymic epithelial cells genuinely decline in
   number with age.
2. **TEC quality**: surviving TECs lose function (especially FOXN1
   expression).
3. **Hematopoietic precursor input**: bone-marrow-derived precursors
   reaching the thymus may decline.

Current evidence: ALL THREE happen, but TEC quality (especially
FOXN1) appears to be the rate-limiting factor.

### FOXN1 downregulation: the central mechanism

**FOXN1** is the master transcription factor for TEC identity
and function (covered in attempts 001 and 003). Without it, no
functional thymic epithelium.

**Key finding**: FOXN1 mRNA and protein levels DECLINE in TECs with
age, even before the cells themselves disappear.

- **Mouse studies**: cortex-specific Foxn1 downregulation between
  1-3 months of age is a key early-involution driver.
- **Human studies**: FOXN1 transcription is suppressed in cultured
  human TECs and in the aging human thymus, with possible
  epigenetic regulation contributing.

Reference: **Reis MD et al. (2015).** "Decline of FOXN1 gene
expression in human thymus correlates with age: possible epigenetic
regulation." *Immunity & Ageing* 12:18. PMID 26516338.

### The Bredenkamp/Blackburn experiment: FOXN1 is causal AND reversible

The most striking demonstration that FOXN1 decline is mechanistically
upstream of involution:

**Bredenkamp N, Nowell CS, Blackburn CC (2014).** "Regeneration of
the aged thymus by a single transcription factor." *Development*
141:1627-1637. PMID 24715454.

What they did: forced TEC-specific upregulation of FOXN1 in
old mice (using transgenic system).

What they found: ROBUST thymus regeneration with:
- Increased thymopoiesis
- Increased naive T cell output
- Architecture and gene expression resembling JUVENILE thymus
- Restoration of cortex/medulla compartmentalization

This single-factor rescue is one of the cleanest "age reversal"
experiments in any organ. It establishes:
- FOXN1 decline is not just correlated with involution; it's
  CAUSAL.
- The thymus retains regenerative competence into old age — the
  master switch needs to be turned back on.
- The TEC progenitor pool is not simply exhausted; it's silenced.

Detailed treatment in attempt_007.

### TEC subset losses: cTECs vs mTECs vs progenitors

- **cTECs**: progressively lose their thymoproteasome-β5t
  expression, reducing positive selection efficiency.
- **mTECs**: AIRE+ mTECs are particularly vulnerable to age-related
  decline. AIRE+ cell numbers drop disproportionately.
- **TEC progenitors**: bipotent FOXN1+ progenitors persist into old
  age but show reduced activation. Stem-cell-like dormancy rather
  than depletion.

Reference: **Wong K et al. (2014).** "Multilineage potential and
self-renewal define an epithelial progenitor cell population in the
mammalian thymus." *Cell Reports* 8:1198-1209. PMID 25131211.

### Adipocytic infiltration

The progressive replacement of thymic parenchyma by adipose tissue
is one of the most striking visual features of involution. In a
60-year-old, MRI of the mediastinum shows nearly complete fatty
replacement.

The adipocytic conversion is not just passive infill:
- Thymic stromal cells (perithymic mesenchyme) appear to convert
  TO adipocytes via PPAR-γ-mediated reprogramming.
- Adipocytes secrete pro-inflammatory adipokines (leptin, IL-6)
  that further suppress TEC function.
- This creates a feedback loop accelerating involution.

Reference: **Yang H et al. (2009).** "Obesity accelerates thymic
aging." *Blood* 114:3803-3812. PMID 19721013.

### Reactive oxygen species (ROS) and oxidative damage

The thymus has unusually low antioxidant defenses (low catalase,
low glutathione peroxidase) for an organ with such high cellular
turnover. Consequence:

- ROS accumulate in TECs with age.
- Oxidative damage to DNA, proteins, lipids accumulates.
- mTORC1 hyperactivation in old TECs drives further ROS.
- Antioxidant treatments (NAC, mitoTEMPO) partially preserve
  thymic function in mouse models.

### Chronic inflammation feedback

Once involution begins, multiple feedback loops accelerate it:
- Increasing systemic IL-6 (with age) suppresses TEC FOXN1
  expression
- TNF-α from accumulating senescent cells damages thymic stroma
- Reduced thymic output → reduced peripheral Tregs → increased
  inflammation → further thymic damage

This is part of why "inflammaging" (biology/evolution/attempt_113)
is intertwined with thymic aging.

### Sex hormones (preview of attempt_005)

Androgens and estrogens accelerate involution. The puberty-associated
sharp decline is hormonally driven (though baseline involution
predates puberty per Steinmann 1985). Castration in mice or
LHRH-blockade in men partially restores thymic function. Detail in
attempt_005.

## The metabolic angle

### LKB1 / AMPK pathway

LKB1 (serine/threonine kinase 11, STK11) and downstream AMPK regulate
TEC metabolism. Loss of LKB1 in TECs leads to mTORC1 hyperactivation,
loss of FOXN1, and accelerated involution.

The metabolic shift in aging TECs:
- Young TECs: balanced oxidative phosphorylation + glycolysis,
  active autophagy
- Aged TECs: skewed glycolysis, reduced autophagy, increased mTORC1
  signaling, accumulated damage

Caloric restriction (which activates AMPK) preserves thymic function
in aged mice. Detailed in attempt_008.

### Mitochondrial dysfunction

Mitochondrial DNA damage and reduced mitochondrial biogenesis in
aging TECs compound the metabolic shift. Mitochondrial-targeted
antioxidants partially preserve thymic function in mouse models.

## Why mammalian thymus involutes so dramatically (evolutionary)

Cross-reference: biology/evolution/attempt_113 covers the
disposable-soma + antagonistic-pleiotropy logic.

Brief restatement: selection invests in immune maintenance only
inasmuch as reproductive fitness is served. Mammals have:
- High juvenile parental investment → strong selection for thymic
  output in youth
- Relatively short post-reproductive life (in ancestral conditions)
  → weak selection for adult thymic upkeep
- Robust adaptive memory + class switching that lets adults rely
  on accumulated memory rather than new T cell generation

Net result: mammals can metabolically afford to throw away the
thymus early. Modern human longevity (now decades past
reproduction) outruns the evolved thymic-maintenance window.

## The reversibility question

Three big-picture observations established by the field:

1. **Adult human thymus contains active thymopoietic islands**
   — even at age 80, residual function exists, just at low levels.
2. **The TEC progenitor pool persists** — not exhausted, just
   silenced.
3. **Single-factor (FOXN1) reactivation regenerates structure** —
   the molecular machinery for thymic function is preserved.

This makes the thymus an unusually attractive target for
regeneration interventions. Detail in attempts 005-010.

## What involution costs

Functional consequences of thymic loss (in humans by age 70):

- ~90% reduction in new naive T cell output
- Reduced TCR repertoire diversity in elderly (compensated by
  peripheral expansion of memory cells, which narrows repertoire)
- Reduced response to NEW antigens (vaccines, novel infections):
  influenza vaccine efficacy ~60% in young adults vs ~30-50% in
  elderly
- Reduced cancer immunosurveillance (rising cancer incidence with
  age has multiple causes; reduced thymic output is one)
- Reduced response to heterologous infections — a young adult is
  exposed to ~10 new pathogens per year in modern society and
  handles them with thymic naive output; elderly handle them by
  reactivating cross-reactive memory
- Increased autoimmune disease incidence (paradoxical given reduced
  thymic output; partly explained by shifting Treg balance)
- Reduced TREC-dependent biomarkers in blood (newborn screening
  uses TRECs; elderly are essentially "TREC-low" by infant
  thresholds)
