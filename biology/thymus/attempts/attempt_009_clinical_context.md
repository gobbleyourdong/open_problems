# attempt_009 — Clinical Thymic Conditions: Congenital Athymia, DiGeorge, Transplantation, Thymectomy

> Ninth attempt. Earlier attempts covered anatomy, selection, chemistry,
> involution, and pharmacological/metabolic levers for regeneration.
> This attempt covers the CLINICAL context: what happens when the
> thymus is missing from birth (FOXN1-null nude/SCID, complete
> DiGeorge), what is done about it (Markert's cultured thymic tissue
> implantation — FDA-approved as RETHYMIC in 2021), and what happens
> when the thymus is REMOVED (neonatal thymectomy during cardiac
> surgery, adult thymectomy for myasthenia gravis / thymoma — now
> known from Kooshesh 2023 NEJM to increase mortality, cancer, and
> autoimmunity risk). These clinical natural experiments are the
> strongest human evidence that the thymus matters throughout life.
>
> The adult-thymectomy data in particular reframe the earlier
> attempts: thymic involution is NOT harmless compensatory decline.
> When adults lose what little thymic function they have, they die
> faster, get more cancer, and develop more autoimmunity. The
> "residual" adult thymus is load-bearing.

## Congenital athymia — the clinical baseline

**Congenital athymia**: absence of functional thymic tissue from
birth. Profound T-cell deficiency (T−B+NK+ SCID phenotype). Fatal
within ~1-2 years without intervention (bone marrow transplant
rescues B/NK function but CANNOT replace thymus — thymic selection
is required for T cell development).

Causes:

### 1. Complete DiGeorge syndrome (cDGS)

- **22q11.2 deletion syndrome**: most common cause. Deletion of
  ~3 Mb region on chromosome 22 affecting ~40-50 genes including
  TBX1 (master regulator of pharyngeal pouch development).
- Phenotype spectrum: "partial" DiGeorge (some thymic tissue, variable
  immune deficit) vs "complete" DiGeorge (NO functional thymus).
- cDGS is the rare end (<1% of 22q11.2 patients); more typical
  DiGeorge has partial thymic hypoplasia with tolerable T cell
  deficit.
- Associated features: cardiac outflow defects (conotruncal),
  hypocalcemia (parathyroid hypoplasia, another pharyngeal-pouch
  derivative), facial dysmorphism, learning/psychiatric risk.

### 2. CHARGE syndrome

CHD7 mutations. Coloboma, Heart defects, Atresia of choanae,
Retardation of growth, Genital abnormalities, Ear abnormalities.
Subset have athymia or severe thymic hypoplasia.

### 3. FOXN1-null mutations (human Nude/SCID)

The rarest and most mechanistically illuminating cause:

**Frank J, Pignata C, Panteleyev AA, et al. (1999).** "Exposing
the human nude phenotype." *Nature* 398:473-474. PMID 10201369.

- Homozygous loss-of-function in FOXN1 (chromosome 17q11.2)
- Phenotype triad: **congenital athymia + total alopecia + nail
  dystrophy**
- T−B+NK+ SCID with NO naive T cells
- ~10 patients described in world literature as of late 2010s
  (extraordinarily rare)
- Direct parallel to the classic "nude mouse" — same gene, same
  phenotype, decades of mouse work directly applicable
- Skin features: tight, hyperplastic basal layer, dysmature keratin
  differentiation (FOXN1 is also required in hair follicle keratinocytes)

This is the "human experiment" that proves FOXN1 is the master
regulator in humans, not just mice. No FOXN1 = no thymus, no hair,
no functional T cells. Reversing FOXN1 activity (attempt_007 work)
has direct clinical relevance.

Reference reviews:
- **Pignata C et al. (2001).** "Congenital alopecia and nail
  dystrophy associated with severe functional T-cell
  immunodeficiency in two sibs." *Am J Med Genet* 65:167-170.
- **Vigliano I, Gorrese M, Fusco A, et al. (2011).** "FOXN1
  mutation abrogates prenatal T-cell development in humans."
  *J Med Genet* 48:413-416.

### 4. Other rare causes

- **PAX1 mutations** (otofaciocervical syndrome type 2): thymic
  hypoplasia
- **FOXI3 mutations**: some cases of athymia
- **TBX2 mutations**: rare syndromic athymia
- **Maternal diabetes / retinoic acid exposure**: phenocopy of
  DiGeorge in some cases

## Markert's cultured thymic tissue — RETHYMIC

The only approved treatment for congenital athymia.

### History

Louise Markert and colleagues at Duke began cultured thymic tissue
transplantation clinical trials in the early 1990s. Over ~30 years,
the protocol was refined, expanded through multiple open-label
trials, and culminated in FDA approval.

**Markert ML et al. (1999).** "Transplantation of thymus tissue in
complete DiGeorge syndrome." *N Engl J Med* 341:1180-1189.
PMID 10519896. **Landmark — first successful human thymus
transplantation.**

### The protocol

1. **Donor tissue**: pediatric thymus from cardiac-surgery patients
   (<9 months, thymus routinely discarded to access the heart —
   attempt_004's thymectomy scenario).
2. **Processing**: donor thymus is **cultured ex vivo for 12-21 days**
   — this is the critical step. Culture depletes donor thymocytes
   and mature T cells while PRESERVING the thymic epithelial
   stroma (TECs + mesenchyme).
3. **Implantation**: slices implanted into the recipient's quadriceps
   muscle (not orthotopic — the quadriceps provides vascularization
   and is accessible).
4. **Engraftment**: over 3-6 months, recipient's own bone-marrow-
   derived precursors colonize the implanted thymic tissue. Host T
   cells develop through donor TEC selection. Donor TECs provide
   positive selection; donor AIRE+ mTECs provide negative selection.

### Clinical efficacy

**Markert ML et al. (2007).** "Review of 54 patients with complete
DiGeorge anomaly enrolled in protocols for thymus transplantation:
outcome of 44 consecutive transplants." *Blood* 109:4539-4547.
PMID 17284531.

- ~75% overall survival (historical otherwise 100% mortality)
- T cell reconstitution over 3-12 months post-implant
- Diverse TCR repertoire generated
- Persistent thymopoiesis documented in follow-up biopsies

### FDA approval (October 2021)

Brand name: **RETHYMIC** (allogeneic processed thymus tissue-agdc).
Approved indication: immune reconstitution in pediatric patients
with congenital athymia.

Pivotal efficacy: 10 prospective open-label studies, 105 patients
enrolled, 95 in primary efficacy analysis. **77% Kaplan-Meier
survival at 1 year post-implant** — vs essentially 0% without
treatment. This is one of the highest-magnitude treatment effects
in immunology.

### Key biological demonstration

RETHYMIC proves several points:
1. **Human thymic tissue can be transplanted ectopically** (into
   muscle, not mediastinum) and function.
2. **Ex vivo TEC culture preserves regenerative capacity** — the
   TECs survive culture and can recruit host hematopoiesis when
   implanted.
3. **Donor HLA can provide selection for host thymocytes** — even
   across some HLA mismatch. (Normally HLA-matched or sibling-matched
   donors are preferred; cardiac-surgery leftover tissue is often
   HLA-disparate but still functional.)
4. **MHC restriction in the resulting T cells**: selected on donor
   HLA, potentially limiting recognition of host APCs. This is a
   known caveat but empirically tolerable.

### Extensions beyond cDGS

- **FOXN1-null SCID**: RETHYMIC has been used off-label; the
  epithelial defect means donor TECs (normal FOXN1) provide the
  missing function.
- **CHARGE with athymia**: used similarly.
- **Immunodysregulation states**: experimental use; less clear
  benefit.

References:
- **Markert ML et al. 2010 *J Allergy Clin Immunol* 126:1236**
  (long-term follow-up, functional T cells).
- **Markert ML et al. 2022 *J Allergy Clin Immunol*** (real-world
  post-approval data).
- FDA Biologics License Application approval: October 2021.

## Thymectomy: the reverse natural experiment

If adding a thymus saves lives, what about removing one?

### Neonatal thymectomy (cardiac surgery)

During pediatric open-heart surgery for congenital defects, the
thymus is often in the way. For decades, surgeons routinely excised
it (partial or total) to access the heart. This created a large
cohort of otherwise-healthy children missing thymic function from
infancy.

Findings from multiple cohort studies:

**Sauce D et al. (2009).** "Evidence of premature immune aging in
patients thymectomized during early childhood." *Journal of
Clinical Investigation* 119:3070-3078. PMID 19770513.

- Children who underwent neonatal thymectomy show **accelerated
  T cell aging** by 10-20 years compared to age-matched controls
- Reduced naive T cells
- Reduced TRECs
- Increased terminally differentiated memory/effector cells
- Reduced TCR repertoire diversity
- Increased exhaustion markers (PD-1, etc.)

**Kurobe H et al. (2013).** "Complete but not partial thymectomy
in early infancy reduces T-cell-mediated immune response." *J
Thorac Cardiovasc Surg* 145:656-662.

- Complete thymectomy: significant long-term immune deficit
- Partial thymectomy (leaving some tissue): less deficit — supports
  modern practice of preserving any accessible thymic tissue

**van den Broek T et al. (2017).** Systematic review — neonatal
thymectomy associates with:
- Higher rates of respiratory infections in childhood
- Possibly higher autoimmune disease risk in adolescence
- Suboptimal vaccine responses

### Gudmundsdottir et al. long-term Danish cohort

**Gudmundsdottir J et al. (2018).** "Early thymectomy leads to
premature immunologic aging: An 18-year follow-up." *J Allergy
Clin Immunol* 141:2319-2322.

Thymectomized patients at 18-year follow-up resembled 60-year-olds
immunologically. This is striking: removing the thymus at age 0
ages the immune system by ~60 years.

### Current surgical practice

Modern pediatric cardiac surgery preserves thymic tissue whenever
feasible (attempted thymic preservation techniques, accessory
tissue preservation). This reflects accumulated awareness that
the thymus is NOT expendable even in the presence of otherwise
intact immunity.

## Adult thymectomy — the Kooshesh 2023 landmark

The study that made the broader scientific community take adult
thymic function seriously:

**Kooshesh KA, Foy BH, Sykes DB, Gustafsson K, Scadden DT (2023).**
"Health consequences of thymus removal in adults." *New England
Journal of Medicine* 389:406-417. PMID 37530823.

### Study design

- Single-center cohort at Massachusetts General Hospital
- 1,420 adult patients who underwent thymectomy (1993-2020)
- Compared to 6,021 matched controls who underwent similar
  cardiothoracic surgery WITHOUT thymectomy
- Median follow-up: ~5 years

### Indications for adult thymectomy in the cohort

- Thymoma resection
- Myasthenia gravis (autoimmune disease where thymectomy has
  therapeutic effect — MGTX trial 2016 established this)
- Incidental thymic mass
- Cardiac surgery access (rare in adults vs children)

### Key findings

1. **All-cause mortality at 5 years**:
   - Thymectomy: 8.1%
   - Control: 2.8%
   - Relative risk: 2.9 (95% CI 1.7-4.8)

2. **Cancer risk at 5 years**:
   - Thymectomy: 7.4%
   - Control: 3.7%
   - Relative risk: 2.0 (95% CI 1.3-3.2)

3. **Autoimmune disease** (after excluding patients with
   pre-existing autoimmune conditions):
   - Thymectomy: 12.3%
   - Control: 7.9%
   - Relative risk: 1.5 (95% CI 1.02-2.2)

4. **Immune-marker subset analysis**: thymectomized patients had
   reduced T cell diversity and increased pro-inflammatory cytokines
   at long-term follow-up.

### Interpretation

Even in ADULTS, where the thymus is already substantially involuted,
removing the residual tissue produces measurable excess mortality,
cancer, and autoimmunity over 5 years. This is load-bearing function
even at age 50-70.

Implication for thymic aging (attempt_004): the SLOW age-related
decline in thymic function is NOT harmless. It's a gradual version
of thymectomy — and we can now quantify the cost by extrapolating
from the rapid-thymectomy data.

Implication for regeneration (attempts 005-008): the bar for
clinical benefit is lower than naïve thinking suggested. Even small
preservation or regrowth of adult thymic function should translate
to mortality/cancer/autoimmunity reduction. The Kooshesh numbers
suggest baseline adult thymic function contributes ~5 percentage
points of 5-year survival — meaningful in public health terms.

## Thymoma and myasthenia gravis

A special clinical context where thymectomy is THERAPEUTIC rather
than incidental:

### Thymoma

- Malignant or benign tumor of thymic epithelial cells
- Accounts for ~20% of mediastinal tumors in adults
- Often associated with paraneoplastic autoimmune syndromes:
  myasthenia gravis (30-50% of thymoma patients), pure red cell
  aplasia, Good syndrome (immunodeficiency + thymoma), others
- **Mechanism of paraneoplastic autoimmunity**: thymoma tissue has
  disordered mTEC architecture; AIRE expression is reduced or
  absent; negative selection fails; autoreactive T cells escape
  to periphery.

### Myasthenia gravis (MG)

Autoimmune disease: antibodies against acetylcholine receptor
at the neuromuscular junction → muscle weakness.

**MGTX trial**:
**Wolfe GI et al. (2016).** "Randomized trial of thymectomy in
myasthenia gravis." *N Engl J Med* 375:511-522. PMID 27509100.

- Randomized trial of thymectomy + prednisone vs prednisone alone
- Thymectomy group had reduced disease activity at 3 years
- Also reduced prednisone requirement
- Established thymectomy as standard of care for MG

The MG-thymectomy story seems to contradict the "thymus is load-
bearing" story, but actually supports it:
- MG thymus contains germinal centers with AChR-autoreactive B and
  T cells
- Thymus is ACTIVELY generating the autoimmune response
- Removing it removes the source — therapeutic benefit
- This is the OPPOSITE of healthy thymic function (negative
  selection has failed)

## Thymic transplantation for transplant tolerance

An emerging application:

Organ transplant recipients must take lifelong immunosuppression
because their donor organ HLA differs from their own. Thymic
tissue co-transplantation is being explored as a way to INDUCE
donor-specific tolerance:
- Co-transplant donor thymic tissue with donor solid organ
- Donor thymus becomes a selection organ: host T cells mature on
  donor HLA → tolerant to donor antigens
- Potential: long-term tolerance without chronic immunosuppression

This is early-stage clinical research, primarily at Duke (Markert
lab extension) and Columbia. Not yet standard of care.

Reference: **Kwun J et al. (2020).** "Thymus transplantation for
inducing tolerance." Review in *J Allergy Clin Immunol*.

## Clinical takeaways

1. **Congenital athymia is fatal without intervention**; RETHYMIC
   rescues most patients. Thymic transplantation is a solved
   problem biologically (cultured donor TEC stroma + host
   hematopoiesis → functional T cell output).

2. **Neonatal thymectomy ages the immune system by decades**.
   Current pediatric cardiac surgery preserves thymic tissue
   whenever possible.

3. **Adult thymectomy increases 5-year mortality, cancer, and
   autoimmunity** (Kooshesh 2023). The residual adult thymus
   matters.

4. **Thymoma/myasthenia gravis are the exception** where
   thymectomy is therapeutic — but this is because the thymus
   is pathologically generating autoimmunity, not because
   removing healthy thymic function is harmless.

5. **The clinical literature validates the regeneration
   ambition** (attempts 005-008): since removing small amounts
   of adult thymic function hurts, adding small amounts should
   help.

## Open at this stage

- **Long-term follow-up of RETHYMIC recipients**: 20+ year data
  emerging; do they retain thymopoietic function into adulthood?
  Do their HLA-mismatched donor TECs persist?
- **Can RETHYMIC-like implantation help INVOLUTION, not just
  athymia?** Autologous iPSC-derived thymic organoids
  (attempt_010) are in preclinical development.
- **Why do adults with MG benefit from thymectomy while adults
  without MG are harmed by it?** The disease-specific pathology
  vs healthy-residual-function distinction is key but not
  fully mechanistically characterized.
- **What is the minimum thymic tissue preservation needed during
  cardiac surgery to avoid long-term sequelae?** Partial
  preservation helps (Kurobe 2013), but how much is enough?
