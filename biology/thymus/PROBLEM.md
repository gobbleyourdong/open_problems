# The Thymus: Growth, Function, Involution, and Regeneration

## Scope

This directory investigates the **thymus** — the primary lymphoid
organ responsible for T cell development — with emphasis on:

1. What it does (mechanistically)
2. Why it grows to peak size in early life
3. Why it shrinks (involution) with age, especially dramatically in
   mammals
4. What is known about regenerating it, in principle and in clinical
   practice

Complementary to but distinct from `biology/evolution/` (which
covered the thymus evolutionarily) and `medical/` (which covers
specific diseases).

## Why focus on the thymus

The thymus is a key node in three big questions:

### a. Immunosenescence and inflammaging
As humans live past 60-80, their immune system declines. Much of the
decline traces to reduced thymic output. If adult thymic function
could be restored, aspects of immunosenescence might be reversible.

### b. Autoimmunity and tolerance
Central T cell tolerance is established in the thymus. Understanding
thymic selection mechanisms is foundational to understanding why
autoimmune diseases emerge (and maybe how to prevent them).

### c. Regeneration biology
The thymus is one of a handful of organs with documented regenerative
potential at the cellular level. The TRIIM trial (Fahy et al. 2019)
showed measurable thymic regrowth in adult men with a specific
hormonal protocol. This is a natural experiment on whether
age-associated tissue decline is reversible.

## The central questions

1. **What determines thymus size at peak** (early childhood through
   puberty)?
2. **What mechanisms drive involution** — sex hormones, FOXN1
   decline, epithelial stem cell exhaustion, inflammation,
   extrinsic factors?
3. **What has been shown to slow involution or regrow the thymus
   in adults** — sex hormone manipulation, IL-7, KGF, IL-22, growth
   hormone + DHEA + metformin (TRIIM), FOXN1 gene therapy?
4. **What are the fundamental limits** — can a 70-year-old thymus be
   regenerated to a 20-year-old's capacity, or only partially?
5. **What does thymic regeneration buy clinically** — better vaccine
   responses, reduced infection incidence, improved cancer
   immunosurveillance, delayed autoimmune flares?

## Why this is a good target

- Well-defined organ with clear anatomy and measurable output
  (naive T cell emigrants, TRECs).
- Multiple experimental handles (gene, hormone, cytokine, cell
  transplantation).
- Natural human experiments (congenital athymia, DiGeorge,
  thymectomy outcomes, centenarians with preserved thymic function).
- Active clinical-trials activity (TRIIM, TRIIM-X, cord-blood-
  derived thymic tissue grafts, engineered thymic organoids).
- Direct implications for aging medicine and for immune-mediated
  disease.
