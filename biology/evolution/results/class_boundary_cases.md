# Class-Boundary Cases — Extending the 7-Class Taxonomy

> Synthesis note. The 7-class persistence-mechanism taxonomy
> (`persistence_mechanisms_taxonomy.md`) was derived from the 10
> organisms in the persistent-organism framework. This note tests
> generalization by extending to 6 organisms NOT on the original
> list: *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, *Toxoplasma gondii*,
> *Trypanosoma cruzi*, *Mycobacterium leprae*, *Treponema
> pallidum*, *Plasmodium vivax*. Each either fits an existing class
> with refinement or motivates splitting a class.
>
> **VERIFICATION STATUS:** generated from trained priors; structural
> claims stable; specific numbers (e.g. global burden, prevalence)
> should be verified at audit-loop discretion.
>
> Companions: `persistence_mechanisms_taxonomy.md` (the 7 classes);

## Why these 6 organisms

Each represents a different potential framework extension:

| Organism | Framework test |
|----------|---------------|
| *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* | Does class 6 extend to **intracellular chronic bacterial**? |
| *Mycobacterium leprae* | Extreme **reductive-genome bacterium** — does the eukaryotic class-7 reductive-genome signature also apply to bacteria? |
| *Treponema pallidum* | **Latent syphilis** — chronic with quiescence; extends class 6 or requires new class? |
| *Toxoplasma gondii* | **Protozoal tissue cyst persistence** — class 7 (eukaryote) but intracellular not ecto; does the class split? |
| *Trypanosoma cruzi* | **Chronic intracellular + antigenic variation** — class 4 and class 7 hybrid |
| *Plasmodium vivax* | **Hypnozoite dormancy** — a protozoal "latency" that resembles class 1 (encoded quiescence) |

The 6 together span bacteria-intracellular + protozoa + spirochete,
covering the main persistence-organism categories not represented
by the original list.

## Case 1 — *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (latent TB)

**Class placement:** **class 6 (chronic active colonization +
host-tolerance induction), intracellular variant.**

- Persists in alveolar macrophages and granuloma structures
- Granuloma is an immune-privileged niche with low O₂, low pH,
  limited immune-cell access
- Reactivation triggered by host immunosuppression (HIV, anti-TNF,
  aging)
- Global burden: ~1.7 billion latently infected humans; ~10 million
  new TB-disease cases per year; ~1.3 million deaths
- Drug-resistant TB (MDR, XDR, pre-XDR) is evolving under treatment
  pressure
- Bacille Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine from 1921 — partially
  protective, not eradicating

**Framework fit:** extends class 6 **but inward into
intracellular persistence**. The H. pylori / P. gingivalis class-6
instances live in mucosal surface niches; MTB lives inside
macrophages. Both mechanisms share chronic-active + host-tolerance
induction but the intracellular variant requires additional
machinery (ESX-1 secretion system, cord factor, lipid-rich cell
wall).

**Refinement:** class 6 should be noted as having **two subtypes** —
6a (mucosal-luminal: H. pylori, P. gingivalis, C. acnes) and 6b
(intracellular: MTB, also M. leprae, T. pallidum latent phase).

## Case 2 — *Mycobacterium leprae* (leprosy)

**Class placement:** **class 6b (intracellular chronic) + class-7-
like reductive genome signature.**

- Intracellular bacterium; tropism for Schwann cells (peripheral
  nerves) + skin macrophages
- Very slow replication (~14 day doubling time — slowest of any
  known human pathogen)
- **Reductive genome** — ~3.3 Mb with ~1,100 functional genes +
  ~1,100 pseudogenes. Extensive gene loss including lipid
  biosynthesis, DNA repair, central metabolism. Obligate intra-
  cellular with substrate dependence.
- Global burden: ~200,000 new cases/year globally (mostly India,
  Brazil, Indonesia); slow decline under multidrug therapy (MDT)
- Strict or near-strict human specificity (armadillos are the only
  major non-human reservoir)

**Framework fit:** **M. leprae is the clearest case that the
reductive-genome signature (class 7) applies to bacteria, not just
eukaryotes.** Combines class-6b (intracellular chronic) with class-7-
like reductive genome.

**Framework refinement:** reductive-genome signature is **a
correlate of deep host-dependence, independent of taxonomic kingdom**.
Class 7 was originally defined as eukaryotic obligate ectoparasite;
the evolutionary signature (reductive genome, host-substrate
dependence) actually spans kingdoms.

**Updated class-7 definition:** **"obligate host-dependent organism
with reductive-genome signature, regardless of kingdom,"** with
ectoparasite (Demodex, lice) and obligate-intracellular (M. leprae,
Rickettsia) as subtypes.

## Case 3 — *Treponema pallidum* (latent syphilis)

**Class placement:** class 6 (chronic + tolerance) with
**quiescent phase resembling class 1**.

- Spirochete with very reduced genome (~1.14 Mb)
- Extreme host-dependence (cannot be cultured in vitro for decades
  — only recently achieved with specific media conditions)
- Disease course: primary → secondary → **latent (early + late)** → tertiary
- Latent syphilis has clinical quiescence for years to decades
  between secondary and tertiary phases
- Global burden: ~6 million new syphilis cases/year; rising in
  many regions due to multiple drivers
- Treponemal antigenic variation (tprK gene) + extensive immune
  evasion machinery

**Framework fit:** hybrid between **class 6 (chronic + tolerance)
and class 4 (antigenic variation)** with phased quiescence. Latent
phase resembles viral class-1 latency but is bacterial and involves
low-level replication (not full dormancy).

**Framework refinement:** **class 6 should include bacterial
organisms with phased quiescence**, not just continuous active
colonization. T. pallidum and MTB both show this — extended periods
of clinically-silent low-level persistence punctuated by
reactivation or late-stage disease.

## Case 4 — *Toxoplasma gondii* (tissue cysts)

**Class placement:** **class 7 (obligate host-dependent eukaryote)
+ class-1-like encoded quiescence in cysts**.

- Apicomplexan protozoan parasite
- Definitive host: Felidae (cats) — sexual reproduction
- Intermediate host: ~any warm-blooded animal including humans
- Persistence mechanism in intermediate hosts: **tissue cysts
  (bradyzoites) in neurons + muscle cells** — long-lived
  intracellular form
- Reactivation in immunocompromised (cerebral toxoplasmosis in AIDS,
  transplant)
- Global seroprevalence: ~30% (varies 0% to 60%+ by region)
- Behavioral-effect literature — Toxoplasma-positive rats lose
  innate fear of cat urine (selection for vector transmission);
  some correlative data in humans

**Framework fit:** **class 7 with phased latency (class-1-like)**.
The tissue cyst is an encoded quiescence program (specific gene
expression pattern distinguishing bradyzoite from tachyzoite);
within a eukaryotic organism committed to host-dependent lifestyle.

**Framework refinement:** class 7 with phased dormancy is worth
naming as a distinct subtype. Combined with case 5 (Trypanosoma)
and case 6 (Plasmodium), protozoal persistence warrants
systematic attention.

## Case 5 — *Trypanosoma cruzi* (Chagas disease)

**Class placement:** class 7 + class 4 (antigenic variation) hybrid.

- Kinetoplastid protozoan
- Transmission: Triatomine bugs (vector-borne); also blood
  transfusion, vertical, organ transplant
- Acute phase: flagellated trypomastigote in blood (~4-8 weeks)
- **Chronic intracellular amastigotes in cardiac + GI smooth muscle
  cells** — decades of persistence
- Chronic Chagas: cardiomyopathy (dilated, arrhythmias), megacolon,
  megaesophagus
- Global burden: ~6 million infected (mostly Latin America + some
  US/European migrant populations)
- **Trans-sialidase family + mucin-like glycoprotein family
  rearrangements provide antigenic variation**

**Framework fit:** eukaryotic + obligate-intracellular + antigenic
variation. Explicitly combines classes 4 and 7.

**Framework consequence:** classes are **not mutually exclusive**.
Many real organisms use a primary + secondary mechanism, and the
taxonomy should accommodate this.

## Case 6 — *Plasmodium vivax* (hypnozoites)

**Class placement:** class 4 (antigenic variation) + class-1-like
quiescence.

- Apicomplexan malaria parasite
- Most Plasmodium species: acute → cleared or death
- **P. vivax + P. ovale: hypnozoite dormancy in liver hepatocytes**
  — latent form that can reactivate months to years after primary
  infection
- Antigenic variation: var gene family in P. falciparum (PfEMP1
  expression switching)
- Global burden: P. vivax responsible for ~14 million malaria cases/
  year (P. falciparum is higher-burden in Africa but P. vivax is
  dominant in much of Asia + Latin America)

**Framework fit:** **class-1-like encoded dormancy** (hypnozoite is
a distinct gene-expression program) applied to a protozoan, with
class-4 antigenic variation during active phase.

**Framework extension:** the hypnozoite phenomenon in Plasmodium
parallels viral latency (EBV, HCMV) mechanistically — encoded gene-
expression program producing dormancy. This is another case of a
persistence mechanism crossing kingdoms.

## Framework summary after extensions

Reviewing the 7-class taxonomy against 16 organisms (10 original +
6 extensions):

| Class | Original examples | Extension examples | Refinements needed |
|-------|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 1 Encoded latency | EBV, HCMV | (Plasmodium hypnozoite as protozoal analog) | Extends to protozoa |
| 2 Mutation-driven defective | CVB | — | Stable |
| 3 Lifecycle coupling | HPV | — | Stable |
| 4 Antigenic variation | (weak on list) | T. cruzi, T. pallidum, Plasmodium | Stronger in protozoa |
| 5 Integration/germline | HHV-6 | — | Stable; HERVs as end-state |
| 6 Chronic + tolerance | H. pylori, P. gingivalis, C. acnes | **MTB, M. leprae (intracellular), T. pallidum (phased)** | **Split into 6a mucosal + 6b intracellular; add phased-quiescence subtype** |
| 7 Obligate host-dependent | Demodex, Malassezia | **M. leprae (reductive bacterium), Toxoplasma, T. cruzi** | **Extend definition to all kingdoms; reductive-genome signature is class-7 marker across bacteria + protozoa + eukaryotes** |

**Key refinements:**

1. **Class 6 has subtypes 6a (mucosal-luminal) and 6b
   (intracellular).** Both share chronic-active + tolerance-
   induction; differ in cellular compartment.
2. **Class 7 extends across kingdoms**, not eukaryote-only. The
   defining signature is reductive-genome + host-substrate-
   dependence + strict host specificity.
3. **Classes are not mutually exclusive.** Many organisms (T. cruzi,
   T. pallidum, Plasmodium) use 2+ mechanisms in combination.
4. **Phased quiescence** (latent syphilis, P. vivax hypnozoites,
   MTB latency) is a cross-class feature that should be named
   explicitly.

## Is there an 8th class — biofilm chronic?

**Candidate**: biofilm-resident chronic infection as a distinct
mechanism class.

**Candidate organisms:**
- *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* in cystic fibrosis airways
- *Burkholderia cepacia* complex (also in CF)
- *Staphylococcus aureus* biofilm on prosthetic joints
- *Cutibacterium acnes* (class 6 already named — overlaps with
  biofilm mechanism)
- Polymicrobial periodontal biofilms (subgingival red complex)

**Mechanism features:**
- Extracellular matrix production (exopolysaccharides, eDNA,
  proteins) creating a physical barrier
- Metabolic stratification within the biofilm (aerobic outside,
  anaerobic center)
- Persister cells (phenotypic antibiotic tolerance)
- Quorum sensing–regulated genome expression
- Horizontal gene transfer enrichment (closer cell-cell contact)

**Is this a distinct class?**

- Biofilm production is a **mechanism** that organisms use, not a
  fundamental persistence class distinct from class 6
- Many class-6 organisms form biofilms as part of their chronic
  colonization (P. gingivalis, C. acnes)
- Biofilm is a physical strategy, not a coevolutionary-trajectory
  class

**Conclusion:** biofilm is a **sub-mechanism of class 6 (chronic
active colonization)** rather than a distinct 8th class. Class 6b
could be further subdivided into "intracellular" vs "biofilm" but
this is refinement within the existing taxonomy, not a new top-
level class.

**No 8th class is warranted by the organism set considered so
far.** The 7-class taxonomy holds across 16 organisms spanning
bacteria, viruses, eukaryotes, protozoa.

## What the extensions teach

1. **The framework generalizes.** Kingdom-specific details differ
   (protozoal mechanisms vs bacterial vs fungal vs viral) but the 7
   persistence-mechanism classes accommodate all.
2. **Reductive-genome signature is universal to deep host-dependence.**
   Not eukaryote-specific. M. leprae, Rickettsia, Mycoplasma, and
   Chlamydia all show bacterial reductive genomes with the same
   signature.
3. **Classes combine.** Real organisms use hybrid strategies.
   Single-class assignment is a simplification; many organisms
   require 2-class description.
4. **Phased quiescence is a cross-class feature.** Viral latency,
   bacterial latent syphilis, protozoal hypnozoites, protozoal
   tissue cysts — all instances of the same phenomenon at different
   levels of biology. Could warrant being elevated to a
   cross-class principle.
5. **The host-coevolution pattern extends.** HLA polymorphism is
   driven by MTB + protozoal exposure nearly as strongly as by
   persistent viruses (HLA-DR2 / HLA-DQ associations for TB,
   leprosy leprosy-reaction polymorphisms); KIR receptors show
   malaria-driven signatures; the framework's host-side claims
   hold for these extensions too.

## Open questions

1. **Does the framework predict any organism currently classified
   as "acute" will shift to "persistent"?** Some acute zoonotic
   reservoirs (avian influenza, coronaviruses in bats) could evolve
   human-adapted persistent forms. Cannot currently predict which,
   but the framework provides the criteria: long coevolution,
   tolerated niche, immune-modulation machinery.
2. **What mechanisms apply to fungi beyond Malassezia?** *Candida*,
   *Cryptococcus*, *Aspergillus*, *Histoplasma* — all show chronic
   human infection in specific contexts. Class 6 or class 7?
3. **Are there persistent protozoal or metazoan infections that
   fit class 1 encoded latency?** The Toxoplasma bradyzoite +
   Plasmodium hypnozoite both show encoded-latency-like features.
4. **What does the framework predict for newly-emerging
   persistent organisms?** Novel pathogens that establish in
   humans would test the framework.
5. **Is "persistence" itself something that can be lost?** Some
   organisms may evolve away from persistence (HCV now curable
   with DAAs — selection pressure to shift strategy?). Understudied.
