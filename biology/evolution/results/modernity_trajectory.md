# Modernity Trajectory — Persistent Organisms Under Modern Conditions

> Synthesis note. Modern human environments are reshaping the
> persistent-organism composition in measurable ways: some
> organisms are declining (H. pylori, HPV in vaccinated cohorts),
> some are rising (P. gingivalis, Demodex, Malassezia, EBV-driven
> disease via hygiene delay), and most are shifting in second-order
> ways. The pattern predicts chronic-disease burden over the next
> 50–100 years.
>
> **VERIFICATION STATUS:** structural claims drawn from per-organism
> attempts (5 batches audit-corrected). Quantitative projections
> are hypothesis-level — forecast over decades is inherently
> uncertain.
>
> Companions: `persistence_mechanisms_taxonomy.md`,

## The drivers of modernity change

Six forces reshape the persistent-organism ecosystem:

1. **Sanitation** — reduces fecal-oral, saliva, and skin-contact
   transmission. Delays age of first infection.
2. **Diet shift toward Western patterns** — refined sugars, high
   glycemic load, low fiber, high dairy/meat. Increases sebum
   output, shifts gut microbiome, increases systemic inflammation.
3. **Antibiotic + acaricide + antifungal use** — applies selective
   pressure against specific organisms.
4. **Vaccination** — strong negative selection on covered organisms
   (so far: HPV via Gardasil); would apply to HCMV, EBV, CVB if
   successful candidates emerge.
5. **Smaller family size + delayed childcare exposure** — alters
   mother-child and sibling-sibling transmission dynamics.
6. **Population aging + longer lifespan** — more decades available
   for persistent-organism disease to manifest; chronic inflammation
   accumulates.

Each organism on the persistent-organism list responds differently
to this mix.

## Per-organism trajectories

### CVB — rising T1DM burden via hygiene delay

- **Transmission**: fecal-oral; declining with sanitation
- **Age of first infection**: rising
- **Disease consequence**: delayed-first-infection is associated with
  higher T1DM risk (parallels the hygiene hypothesis for polio →
  paralytic disease)
- **Net trajectory**: CVB population prevalence ↓ but CVB-associated
  T1DM ↑; also rising DCM and myocarditis incidence in some
  populations
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: T1DM continues to rise until a
  vaccine (PRV-101 in trials) is deployed; if PRV-101 is successful,
  modern T1DM could decline substantially over 20–30 years post-
  licensing. Failure would leave T1DM on current trajectory.

### EBV — rising MS burden via hygiene delay

- **Transmission**: saliva; declining with sanitation + smaller
  families + less kissing-of-infants-by-siblings in developed
  countries
- **Age of first infection**: rising from childhood to adolescence
- **Disease consequence**: infectious mononucleosis (delayed
  infection) is a strong MS risk factor (Bjornevik 2022); MS
  incidence has been rising in developed countries for decades
- **Net trajectory**: EBV population prevalence relatively stable (
  near-universal), but delayed-first-infection drives MS + chronic
  fatigue syndromes ↑
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: MS continues to rise unless a
  pre-infection EBV vaccine (Moderna mRNA-1189 in phase 1) becomes
  available. Moderna-1189 + a decade of deployment could bend the
  MS incidence curve downward; uncertain timing.

### HPV — declining with Gardasil deployment

- **Transmission**: skin/mucosal contact; largely unchanged by
  sanitation
- **Vaccination**: Gardasil 9 covers ~90% of cervical cancer
  etiology; deployment in adolescence precedes typical sexual
  exposure
- **Net trajectory**: **vaccine-covered HPV types declining sharply
  in vaccinated populations**; type replacement by non-covered HR
  types is modest so far
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: **the clearest positive
  persistent-organism trajectory in the modern world**. Cervical
  cancer projected to become a rare disease within 2–3 generations
  at current vaccination rates (WHO 90-70-90 cervical cancer
  elimination target by 2030 in vaccinated cohorts). Oropharyngeal
  cancer and anal cancer will follow.

### HCMV — relatively stable; Moderna vaccine failure 2025

- **Transmission**: saliva + vertical + sexual + blood; resilient
  to sanitation changes
- **Age of first infection**: rising modestly with hygiene
- **Vaccination**: **Moderna mRNA-1647 phase 3 CMVictory failed
  (Oct 2025)**; Moderna discontinued congenital CMV development.
  No other late-stage candidate.
- **Net trajectory**: population prevalence stable; congenital
  CMV incidence stable
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: without a vaccine, HCMV remains
  the leading non-genetic cause of infant sensorineural hearing
  loss. Treatment advances (earlier antivirals in congenital CMV
  may improve outcomes) but prevention remains elusive. Next
  vaccine attempt likely 5–10 years away from phase 3.

### HHV-6 — stable, ciHHV-6 slowly accumulating in gene pool

- **Transmission**: saliva (near-universal acquisition in infancy);
  + germline (ciHHV-6) via integrated carriers
- **Net trajectory**: population prevalence stable at
  near-universal by age 2; ciHHV-6 frequency ~1% (or higher per
  recent UK data) stable, very slowly accumulating over generations
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: stable. Unlikely to see major
  change without specific ciHHV-6 screening/management
  interventions.

### H. pylori — declining with sanitation; GERD/EAC rising to
compensate

- **Transmission**: oral-oral + fecal-oral; strongly declining with
  sanitation
- **Age of first infection**: rising; in some developed countries
  primary infection is now rare
- **Disease consequence** (bidirectional):
  - PUD, gastric cancer, MALT lymphoma **declining** — positive
  - GERD, Barrett's esophagus, esophageal adenocarcinoma **rising**
    — Blaser's "disappearing microbiota" concern
- **Net trajectory**: population prevalence ~44% globally (Hooi
  2017), declining by ~1% per year in developed countries. Regional
  variation large (Africa 70%, Europe 30–40%).
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: continued decline in developed
  countries toward <20% by 2070; Africa may follow more slowly.
  **Net health effect is contested** — PUD + gastric cancer ↓ vs
  GERD + EAC ↑. Policy debate continues on whether targeted
  eradication is net beneficial.

### P. gingivalis — rising with Western diet despite better
hygiene

- **Transmission**: oral (parent-child, family); mixed direction
  under modern conditions
- **Dental hygiene**: improving (brushing, flossing, professional
  cleaning); should reduce P. gingivalis
- **Western diet**: increases carbohydrate load + biofilm favorable
  environment; **drives P. gingivalis and total periodontitis
  burden upward**, offsetting hygiene gains
- **Net trajectory**: **periodontitis rising in developed
  countries despite dental-care investment**. WHO Global Burden of
  Disease data shows severe periodontitis affected ~11% of
  global adults in 1990s and ~10% in 2010s — slight improvement;
  but moderate periodontitis + tooth loss trajectories differ.
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: depends on whether Western-diet
  trajectory continues or reverses. RA, Alzheimer's association,
  atherosclerosis links will scale with P. gingivalis burden. If
  anti-gingipain drugs (Quince Therapeutics successor to COR388)
  succeed, a clean positive turn is possible; if not, continued
  slow rise.

### Demodex — rising with Western diet + delayed-clearance

- **Transmission**: mother-infant (stable) + adult-adult (stable)
- **Diet**: Western diet increases sebum output → Demodex density ↑
- **Age + immune changes**: aging + immunosuppression rates
  increasing in developed populations → Demodex-associated disease
  ↑
- **Treatment**: topical ivermectin, TTO, lotilaner (Xdemvy)
  available for individual patients; no population-scale
  intervention
- **Net trajectory**: **rosacea prevalence has been rising in
  developed countries** (consistent with Demodex density
  increasing); blepharitis and MGD similarly
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: rosacea / blepharitis / chalazion
  continue to rise in parallel with Western-diet penetration.
  Treatment improves (more accessible acaricides) but doesn't
  reverse population-level trend. Could plateau or reverse if
  dietary patterns change.

### Malassezia + C. acnes — rising with Western diet

- **Transmission**: universal skin colonization; age-dependent
  density
- **Diet**: Western diet increases sebum → Malassezia + C. acnes
  density ↑
- **Net trajectory**: **seborrheic dermatitis prevalence rising;
  acne prevalence high and not declining** (~85% of adolescents
  globally by some estimates)
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: seborrheic disease continues
  rising in parallel with Demodex rosacea. Acne remains near-
  universal; shift toward adult-onset acne is documented. Isotretinoin
  and spironolactone use rises. No clean declining trajectory
  without dietary intervention or microbiome-targeted therapy
  breakthroughs.

### Composite gut dysbiosis — rising IBD, metabolic syndrome

- **Transmission**: mother-infant at birth (vaginal microbiome
  transfer); modified by antibiotic exposure in early life
- **Cesarean section rate, formula feeding, early-life antibiotic
  use** — all reshape gut microbiome from birth
- **Net trajectory**: **IBD prevalence has crossed 0.3% in Western
  countries** (Ng SC et al., Lancet 2017 PMID 29050646); high-
  burden regions (Canada, parts of US + Norway) reach 0.5–0.7%.
  Accelerating rise in newly industrializing countries. Metabolic
  syndrome rising similarly; T1DM rising (via both CVB and gut
  microbiome)
- **Projected 50-year outlook**: gut-dysbiosis-driven disease
  burden continues to grow unless microbiome interventions (FMT,
  rationally-designed probiotics, prebiotics, dietary patterns)
  become systematically effective. Current outlook: rising.

## Pattern: rising vs declining organisms cluster by persistence class

- **Declining with modernity**: H. pylori (class 6), vaccine-
  covered HPV (class 3). Both are beneficiaries of specific
  modern interventions (sanitation; vaccination).
- **Rising with modernity**: P. gingivalis (class 6), Demodex
  (class 7), Malassezia (class 7), C. acnes (class 6), gut
  dysbiosis (class 6/7 composite), EBV-MS indirectly (class 1),
  CVB-T1DM indirectly (class 2).

**Pattern observation:** **class-6 organisms split by substrate**
— Gram-negative aerobes in the stomach (H. pylori) decline with
sanitation; Gram-negative anaerobes in dental pockets
(P. gingivalis) rise with Western diet. Both are class 6 but react
oppositely to modernity because their habitat requirements differ.

**Class-7 organisms uniformly rise** (Demodex, Malassezia) because
sebum-rich environments are what Western diet produces.

**Class-1, -2, -3, -5 viruses are relatively insensitive to
modernity** on the short timescale; only HPV has seen its
trajectory reversed by a specific intervention.

## Framework-level prediction

**Modernity is shifting the persistent-organism composition away
from classic Gram-negative luminal persistence and toward biofilm /
ectoparasite / diet-dependent persistence.**

Clinical consequence: the chronic-disease burden is shifting from
cancer (gastric adenocarcinoma ↓) to **inflammation + autoimmunity +
metabolic** (rosacea ↑, periodontitis ↑, RA ↑, IBD ↑, T1DM ↑,
atherosclerosis high, cardiovascular stable-to-rising).

**Public health implication:** the 21st-century chronic-disease
landscape looks like an inflammation epidemic driven by persistent-
organism composition shifts. The two-phase therapeutic architecture
(`medical/persistent_organisms/PROBLEM.md`) becomes more important
over time as more diseases enter its scope.

## What could reverse the trajectory

1. **Vaccination success against CVB + EBV + HCMV** — 3 major
   persistent viruses currently driving rising disease trajectories
   could be individually addressed by successful vaccines. Gardasil
   precedent shows it works; HCMV precedent shows it's hard.
2. **Dietary / microbiome intervention at scale** — if Western-diet
   prevalence reverses (for any reason — climate, cultural, health
   policy), P. gingivalis, Demodex, Malassezia, gut dysbiosis
   burden would decline in parallel. Unlikely in short term.
3. **Improved antimicrobial + antiparasitic therapies** — better
   gingipain inhibitors, next-generation acaricides, targeted
   microbiome modulators. Incremental but possible.
4. **Biomarker-driven inflammatory-substrate therapy** (per
   `therapeutic_convergence.md`) — doesn't change organism burden
   but can reduce downstream disease. Low-hanging fruit.
5. **Population-scale screening for ciHHV-6** — would identify
   carriers for whom different management applies. Limited impact.
6. **Wider sub-antimicrobial doxycycline deployment** — cross-
   class anti-inflammatory at low cost. Practically implementable.

## Open questions

1. **How fast is the H. pylori decline happening?** Different
   estimates give different rates; quantitative cohort tracking
   would clarify.
2. **What is the relative contribution of Western diet vs dental
   hygiene for P. gingivalis?** Both matter, but the proportion is
   unclear.
3. **Will Moderna mRNA-1189 or successor EBV vaccines reach
   phase 3 in time to bend the MS curve within the 50-year
   horizon?** Currently in phase 1.
4. **Can we measure "class-7 density" at the population scale?**
   Demodex + Malassezia density by skin site in representative
   samples would give direct measurement of the trajectory.
5. **What would the demographic transition look like if all
   persistent-organism-directed interventions succeeded
   simultaneously?** Thought experiment: near-complete elimination
   of the chronic-disease portfolio shifts life-expectancy and
   disease-burden profile dramatically. Not achievable in 50 years
   but informative to think about.
