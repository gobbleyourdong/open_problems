# attempt_104 — Variable Lymphocyte Receptors: The Jawless-Vertebrate Adaptive Immune System

> Fourth attempt in the 100-series. Around 500 million years ago, at
> or near the agnathan/gnathostome split, TWO independent adaptive
> immune systems emerged in vertebrates. One is familiar — V(D)J
> recombination with immunoglobulin-fold receptors, used by every
> jawed vertebrate from sharks to humans (see attempt_105). The other
> is equally elaborate, equally specific, equally memory-capable,
> but built from completely different molecular parts: leucine-rich
> repeats, rearranged by gene conversion rather than by V(D)J, and
> deployed in lymphocytes that separately gave rise to T-like and
> B-like lineages. This is the lamprey/hagfish VLR system.

---

## The landscape of 500 Ma

The Cambrian explosion (~538-509 Ma) produced the major animal body
plans. Vertebrates appeared by ~525 Ma (fossil *Haikouella* and
similar early chordates). The split between jawless vertebrates
(Agnatha: lampreys + hagfish) and jawed vertebrates (Gnathostomata:
cartilaginous + bony fishes and their descendants) is estimated at
~500 Ma. Both living agnathan lineages survived to the present
without evolving jaws.

At this split, an extraordinary parallel innovation occurred:
adaptive immunity evolved independently in BOTH lineages, using
completely distinct molecular machinery.

## Discovery of VLRs

**Pancer Z, Amemiya CT, Ehrhardt GRA, Ceitlin J, Gartland GL,
Cooper MD (2004).** "Somatic diversification of variable lymphocyte
receptors in the agnathan sea lamprey." *Nature* 430:174-180.
PMID 15241406. DOI 10.1038/nature02740.

The discovery: sea lamprey (*Petromyzon marinus*) lymphocytes express
a surface receptor DIFFERENT from Ig or TCR. It is built from
leucine-rich repeats (LRRs). Each lamprey lymphocyte expresses ONE
variable receptor. Across the lymphocyte population, the repertoire
is vast and diverse — antigen-specific, clonally restricted,
memory-forming. The three features of adaptive immunity are present.
But there are no Igs, no MHC, no V(D)J.

### Mechanism of somatic assembly

The VLR-B gene in the germline is INCOMPLETE — it contains 5' and
3' constant regions flanking an empty variable region slot. Nearby
on the chromosome sit hundreds of LRR "modules" — N-terminal LRR,
several internal LRR cassettes, C-terminal LRR — in a donor bank.

During lymphocyte development, a copying process (resembling gene
conversion) transfers LRR modules from the donor bank into the
germline gene, assembling a complete variable region one module at
a time. The assembled gene is now expressed as the receptor.

### Role of cytidine deaminases

The copying process is driven by LAMPREY cytidine deaminases:
**CDA1 and CDA2**, homologs of the jawed-vertebrate AID/APOBEC
family. CDA1 acts in one lymphocyte lineage and CDA2 in another
(see lineage section below).

Reference: **Rogozin IB, Iyer LM, Liang L, Glazko GV, Liston VG,
Pavlov YI, Aravind L, Pancer Z (2007).** "Evolution and diversification
of lamprey antigen receptors: evidence for involvement of an AID-APOBEC
family cytosine deaminase." *Nature Immunology* 8:647-656.
PMID 17468760.

The AID/APOBEC family is ancient in vertebrates — predates the
jawed/jawless split. Its use in somatic hypermutation (in jawed
vertebrates) and in VLR assembly (in jawless) is convergent:
jawed and jawless vertebrates BOTH use AID-family enzymes to
diversify lymphocyte receptors, but the mechanism is different
(point mutation vs gene conversion/copying).

## Three VLR genes, three cell lineages

Lampreys have three VLR genes: **VLRA, VLRB, VLRC.**
Each is expressed in a DIFFERENT lymphocyte lineage:

### VLRA+ lymphocytes ≈ T-cell-like

**Hirano M, Guo P, McCurley N, Schorpp M, Das S, Boehm T, Cooper MD
(2013).** "Evolutionary implications of a third lymphocyte lineage in
lampreys." *Nature* 501:435-438. PMID 23934109.

VLRA+ cells are NOT secreted. They express the receptor on their
surface, require thymus-like tissue (the "thymoid") for development,
and produce Th-like and Tc-like cytokines upon activation. They are
functionally analogous to jawed-vertebrate T cells.

### VLRB+ lymphocytes ≈ B-cell-like

VLRB+ cells CAN secrete the VLR as an antibody-like soluble receptor.
They develop independent of thymoid (in a hematopoietic tissue
analogous to bone marrow). Functionally analogous to jawed-vertebrate
B cells.

VLRB is the lineage that Pancer & Cooper 2004 primarily characterized.
When people say "lamprey antibodies," they usually mean secreted VLRB.

### VLRC+ lymphocytes ≈ γδ-T-like

VLRC+ cells are also surface-expressed, non-secreted, and develop
thymoid-dependently. They express VLRC alongside VLRA at some stages.
Functionally closer to γδ-T cells of jawed vertebrates (perhaps).
The exact functional role is less characterized.

### The three-lineage parallel

Jawed vertebrates have: αβ-T cells, γδ-T cells, B cells.
Jawless vertebrates have: VLRA+, VLRC+, VLRB+ lymphocytes.

**Three-way parallel convergence.** Both systems evolved three
lymphocyte lineages with analogous functional roles despite
completely different receptor architectures. This is the
strongest evidence for "adaptive immunity" being a defined
functional solution — any vertebrate-like organism under similar
pathogen pressure converges on three specialized lineages.

## The repertoire size

Theoretical maximum diversity of lamprey VLRs: ~10^14 receptors,
comparable to or exceeding the theoretical ~10^11-10^13 of jawed
vertebrate BCRs/TCRs.

Observed diversity in individual lampreys: estimated 10^7-10^9
distinct VLR sequences per animal, again comparable to mammalian
lymphocyte repertoires.

Reference: **Alder MN, Rogozin IB, Iyer LM, Glazko GV, Cooper MD,
Pancer Z (2005).** "Diversity and function of adaptive immune
receptors in a jawless vertebrate." *Science* 310:1970-1973.
PMID 16373579.

## The thymoid

The structure where VLRA+ cells develop in lampreys was only
identified in 2011:

**Bajoghli B, Guo P, Aghaallaei N, Hirano M, Strohmeier C, McCurley N,
Bockman DE, Schorpp M, Cooper MD, Boehm T (2011).** "A thymus
candidate in lampreys." *Nature* 470:90-94. PMID 21293374.

The thymoid is a tissue at the tip of gill filaments where VLRA+
cells undergo selection. It expresses Foxn1 and other transcription
factors that in jawed vertebrates specify thymic epithelium — so
there is molecular conservation of the thymic environment between
jawless and jawed vertebrates, even though the anatomy is
completely different.

This is a remarkable convergence: the SAME genes specify the
lymphopoietic niche in both lineages, despite the niche itself
(thymoid gill-tip vs true thymus in pharyngeal pouches) being
anatomically distinct.

## MHC-like molecules in lampreys

Jawed vertebrates have a genomic MHC locus with class I and class II
genes that present peptides to T cells. Lampreys lack this locus
entirely.

Do lampreys have an analogous antigen-presentation system? The
answer is still partly open:

- Lampreys have genes encoding class-I-like and class-II-like
  proteins at genomically distinct sites; these are NOT assembled
  into a classical MHC-like cluster.
- The SAS (Salt-Adapted-System) and UA-like genes in lampreys may
  play a peptide-presenting role for VLRA+ cells.
- Evidence of peptide antigens being "presented" in a mechanistic
  sense to VLR-bearing cells is limited.

Reference for this area: **Boehm T, Swann JB (2014).** "Origin and
evolution of adaptive immunity." *Annu Rev Anim Biosci* 2:259-283.
PMID 25384142. (Review from the Boehm lab at Freiburg.)

## Comparison with jawed-vertebrate adaptive immunity

| Feature | Jawless (VLR) | Jawed (V(D)J + Ig/TCR) |
|---------|---------------|------------------------|
| Receptor scaffold | Leucine-rich repeat | Immunoglobulin fold |
| Diversity mechanism | Gene conversion / copying | Site-specific V(D)J recombination |
| Driver enzyme | CDA1/CDA2 (AID family) | RAG1/RAG2 (Transib-derived) |
| Assembly substrate | LRR donor modules | V, D, J gene segments |
| Cell types | VLRA+ (T-like), VLRB+ (B-like), VLRC+ | αβ-T, γδ-T, B cells |
| Soluble form | Secreted VLRB ≈ "antibody" | Secreted Ig ≈ antibody |
| MHC | No classical MHC | MHC class I + II |
| Thymus | Thymoid at gill-tip | True thymus (pharyngeal) |
| Age | ~500 Ma | ~450 Ma (marginally younger) |

Key observation: neither system is "primitive" relative to the
other. Both are fully elaborated adaptive systems that coevolved
with their hosts for ~500 Ma.

## Why two solutions evolved in parallel

The selective pressure driving adaptive immunity around the
jawless/jawed split: pathogen diversity exceeded the capacity
of innate immunity alone. Innate systems have a fixed repertoire;
pathogens can evolve around it. A clonally-varying adaptive system
provides an essentially unlimited repertoire — evolution in the
pathogen becomes easier to track.

Why two independent systems rather than one? Possible reasons:

1. **Geographic/temporal separation**: the agnathan and gnathostome
   lineages diverged before the invention of adaptive immunity in
   either. Each independently faced the selection pressure.

2. **Different starting material**: the jawed lineage had access
   to a Transib-derived RAG transposon (see attempt_105); the
   jawless lineage had access to AID/APOBEC deaminases but not
   active RAG. Different raw materials led to different solutions.

3. **Convergent constraint**: once receptor diversification is
   selected for, it requires (a) a diversifiable protein scaffold,
   (b) a site-specific machinery, (c) selection against autoreactivity.
   The details are interchangeable; the functional architecture is not.

## Disease in lampreys

Lampreys rarely get serious infections, which is consistent with
an elaborate immune system. They have a commercially important
parasitic phase (parasitic lampreys feed on fish blood), during
which the parasite itself is exposed to host immune responses from
the blood meal.

Little is known about lamprey autoimmunity. The animal is
understudied medically but well-studied biologically because of
its phylogenetic position.

## Biotechnological use of lamprey VLRs

Lamprey VLRs are used as research reagents: they can recognize
glycan and carbohydrate antigens that conventional antibodies
struggle with. Commercial VLR reagents (e.g., targeting the H-antigen
on Type O blood cells) are being developed.

Reference: **Herrin BR, Alder MN, Roux KH, Sina C, Ehrhardt GR,
Boydston JA, Turnbough CL Jr, Cooper MD (2008).** "Structure and
specificity of lamprey monoclonal antibodies." *PNAS* 105:2040-2045.
PMID 18238899.

## Open questions

1. **Did the ancestor of vertebrates have ANY form of adaptive
   immunity?** The Pancer & Cooper discovery forces a reconsideration:
   maybe the vertebrate ancestor had PRE-adaptive components that
   each descendant lineage developed into full adaptive systems.
   Alternatively, the two systems arose ENTIRELY independently
   post-split.

2. **Is there immunological memory in lampreys?** Yes — secondary
   VLRB antibody responses are stronger and faster than primary
   responses. Classical B-cell-like memory.

3. **What about T-cell-like memory?** Less well characterized, but
   likely present based on transcriptome studies of VLRA+ cells
   post-challenge.

4. **How do we reconcile the parallel three-lineage structure?** Is
   this a deep homology (common ancestor had three proto-lymphocyte
   lineages)? Or is it parallel evolution driven by three parallel
   functional niches (soluble-effector / cell-contact-effector /
   intermediate-effector)?

## Why this matters for the layered-architecture framework

One critical conclusion: **"V(D)J recombination is not what makes
adaptive immunity."** Adaptive immunity is a FUNCTIONAL solution
(clonal diversity + specificity + memory). V(D)J is one molecular
implementation; VLR is another. Other implementations are likely
possible and may exist in organisms we haven't yet examined
(e.g., some invertebrates show "trained immunity" hints).

This reframes the evolutionary question: what did the COMMON
ancestor of jawless and jawed vertebrates have, and what did the
two separate lineages build from it?

Current best answer: the common ancestor likely had (a) lymphocyte-
like cells with some form of clonal commitment; (b) cytidine
deaminases; (c) thymoid-like peptide-exposure environments; (d)
some complement-like humoral defense. The JAWED lineage added
RAG + Ig-fold scaffolds; the JAWLESS lineage added LRR-based
gene conversion. The starting material was there; the molecular
machinery was added in parallel.

- **What is the "pre-adaptive" state?** A common-ancestor
