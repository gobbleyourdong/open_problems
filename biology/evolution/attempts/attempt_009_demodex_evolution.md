# attempt_009 — Demodex: Obligate Ectoparasitism, Reductive Genome, and Density-Dependent Pathogenesis

> First non-microbe entry in the persistent-organism evolution series.
> Demodex mites are arthropods (eukaryotic multicellular animals) that
> have undergone radical genome reduction to live as obligate
> ectoparasites on mammalian skin. They occupy a persistence mechanism
> class distinct from all previous attempts — not immune evasion at all,
> but **obligate host-dependence with no free-living form**. They are
> the cleanest example of the **commensal → pathogen transition being
> density-dependent rather than species-distinguished**.

## Phylogenetic placement

**Kingdom:** Animalia
**Phylum:** Arthropoda
**Class:** Arachnida
**Order:** Trombidiformes (prostigmatid mites)
**Family:** Demodecidae
**Genus:** *Demodex*
**Species on humans:**
- ***Demodex folliculorum*** — lash follicle, sebaceous follicle
- ***Demodex brevis*** — meibomian gland, sebaceous gland

**Genus diversity:** ~100+ Demodex species have been described across
mammalian hosts. Each mammalian species typically carries its own
Demodex species repertoire. Canine *D. canis*, feline *D. cati*,
bovine *D. bovis*, ovine *D. ovis*, porcine *D. phylloides*, murine
*D. musculi*, and so on. The host-specificity is near-absolute —
human Demodex does not establish on non-human hosts under normal
conditions, and vice versa.

**Evolutionary timescale:** Demodex likely co-speciated with mammals.
The ancestral mammalian-skin-symbiont Demodex lineage is inferred to
have existed at least as long as placental mammals (~100+ Ma). The
two-species repertoire in humans (folliculorum + brevis, occupying
different skin microhabitats) suggests niche partitioning within the
host — a hallmark of successful coevolutionary specialization.

**An important deviation:** most mammal species host ONE Demodex
species. Humans host TWO. This may reflect:
1. Ancient bifurcation of Demodex into follicle-dwellers and
   gland-dwellers before host speciation
2. Or a duplication event within hominin coevolution
3. Or a feature of human skin architecture (prominent meibomian
   gland system) creating a distinct-enough niche to support a
   second species

The two-species arrangement is not unique to humans — some other
primate species also carry two Demodex species — but humans are among
the best-documented cases.

## The reductive genome — a hallmark of obligate parasitism

Like other obligate parasites (head lice *Pediculus humanus*, bed
bugs *Cimex lectularius*, some tick species), Demodex has undergone
dramatic genome reduction:

- **Demodex folliculorum nuclear genome**: first assembly published
  by **Smith et al. 2022 Mol Biol Evol (PMID 35724423, "Human
  Follicular Mites: Ectoparasites Becoming Symbionts") — ~51.5 Mb**,
  one of the smallest arthropod nuclear genomes. 9,707 proteins
  encoded; only ~7% repeats. Smaller than earlier claims.
  [Palopoli et al. 2014 BMC Genomics PMID 25515815 sequenced the
  mitochondrial genome only (14,150 bp), not the nuclear — commonly
  confused in secondary sources.]
- **Loss of many biosynthetic pathways** — Demodex cannot synthesize
  various amino acids and lipid precursors; obtained from host sebum
- **Loss of detoxification enzymes** — absent from Demodex but
  present in free-living mites; host environment is non-toxic, no
  need for xenobiotic metabolism
- **Mitochondrial gene reduction + rearrangement** — mitochondrial
  genome in Demodex is highly derived with gene loss and altered
  gene order vs other arthropods (Palopoli 2014)
- **"No anus" claim is REFUTED** — the long-standing legacy belief
  that Demodex lacks an anus and accumulates waste until death was
  **overturned by Smith et al. 2022**, which confirmed anus
  presence by electron microscopy + identification of anus-
  development genes. The inflammation-at-death mechanism still
  operates, but via cuticle rupture releasing *B. oleronius*
  (now *Heyndrickxia oleronia* — see below) rather than
  retained waste.
- **Reduced cellular repertoire** — nervous system, digestive
  system, reproductive system all streamlined

The pattern is identical to what is seen in other obligate
ectoparasites (lice, ticks, mites). Obligate-host dependence leads
to loss of gene content supporting free-living functions, in exchange
for specialized host-dependent machinery.

**Evolutionary interpretation:** Demodex is in the mature phase of
obligate parasitism — has fully committed to the host niche, can no
longer survive off-host, and has discarded the metabolic machinery
for any other lifestyle.

## The endosymbiont — Bacillus oleronius (now Heyndrickxia oleronia)

Inside Demodex, a bacterial endosymbiont lives: historically
described as *Bacillus oleronius*. **Taxonomic update per Gupta et
al. 2020 reclassification: the organism has been moved to
*Heyndrickxia oleronia* (family Bacillaceae, new genus
Heyndrickxia).** Most clinical and biology literature still uses
"Bacillus oleronius" because the rename is recent and the old name
is widely entrenched.

This is a gram-positive rod-shaped bacterium,
vertically transmitted through Demodex reproduction. Functions
suggested:
- Nutritional supplementation (vitamin synthesis?)
- Possible waste detoxification
- Unknown other functions

When a Demodex dies at high density, the cuticle ruptures and
releases B. oleronius fragments. B. oleronius LPS, peptidoglycan,
and specific proteins (62 kDa, 83 kDa; see Lacey 2007 PMID 17596156)
activate host TLR4, TLR2, Dectin-1, and NOD1/2 pathways, triggering
NF-κB and NLRP3 inflammasome — the core mechanism of rosacea and
Demodex blepharitis.

**This is a three-tier symbiotic relationship:**

```
human host (skin)
    ↓ hosts
Demodex mite (ectoparasite)
    ↓ hosts
Bacillus oleronius (endosymbiont inside Demodex)
```

Each tier has its own coevolutionary dynamics. B. oleronius is
nearly species-specific to Demodex; its phylogenetic position within
the Bacillus genus shows the reductive-genome signatures of
obligate endosymbiosis.

**From the host's perspective**, the immune response targets
B. oleronius more than Demodex itself. Host immunity has evolved to
recognize bacterial antigens (LPS, peptidoglycan) more than
arthropod-specific antigens. The B. oleronius endosymbiont is the
reason Demodex at high density becomes pathogenic — not the mite
per se.

## Transmission

- **Primary route:** mother-to-infant in early childhood, via
  prolonged close contact (face-to-face, kissing, breastfeeding).
  Infants are Demodex-free at birth; colonization occurs gradually
  through the first years of life.
- **Secondary routes:** family members, close contacts, shared
  bedding (see `medical/blepharitis/attempt_005_reinfestation_dynamics`)
- **Timeframe:** Demodex prevalence rises from 0% at birth to
  near-universal (~95%+) by adulthood (Post 1963 classical reference;
  specific age-curve requires verification per the audit)
- **Off-host survival:** Demodex dies within hours to days off-host.
  Cannot survive in environment. Transmission is intimate-contact
  dependent.

**Evolutionary significance of mother-infant transmission:**
vertical transmission (functionally analogous to what HCMV does
through placental crossing) guarantees Demodex continuation across
generations even in the absence of community transmission. Each new
generation of humans is seeded by maternal contact.

## Density-dependent pathogenesis — the defining feature

Unlike viruses and bacteria, Demodex at low density causes **no
disease**. The commensal-to-pathogen transition is determined by
density:

```
Density <1–2 mites/cm²: commensal (no disease)
    ↓ sebum-rich environment favors reproduction
Density ~5 mites/cm²: clinical threshold
    ↓ B. oleronius TLR4 activation starts
Density 10–18 mites/cm² (rosacea range, Forton & Seys 1993): disease
    ↓ maintained inflammation
Density >20 mites/cm²: severe (papulopustular rosacea, demodicosis)
```

Factors that elevate density:
- **Age** (prevalence rises with age — progressive lifetime
  accumulation)
- **Sebum output** (androgens, insulin/IGF-1, diet)
- **Rosacea susceptibility** (host innate immune biology; specific
  TLR2 variant genotypes allow higher density)
- **Immunosuppression** (demodicosis in HIV, chemo, organ transplant)
- **Steroid use** (reduces local surveillance)
- **Skin barrier defects** (atopic conditions)

**The evolutionary logic of density-dependence:**

From Demodex's perspective, there is **selection pressure to maximize
density** (more mites → more reproductive output → more transmission
events). But there is a ceiling: too high a density → overt host
inflammation → host scratches/washes/treats → mite elimination.

The "successful" density is one just below the host-response
threshold. In modern humans with:
- Western diets (more sebum substrate)
- Reduced mite grooming (less infant-style parental touching)
- Later-age environmental cleanliness (delayed immune calibration)
...the density has drifted upward, pushing more people over the
disease threshold. This is why **rosacea is disproportionately a
modern Western disease** despite Demodex itself being ancient.

This parallels the H. pylori → GERD trajectory in a different
direction: H. pylori declines with modernity and protective effects
shift; Demodex density rises with modernity and pathogenic effects
rise.

## Persistence mechanism — class 7

Demodex does not use any of classes 1–6 from the viral + bacterial
framework. It uses **class 7: obligate ectoparasitism with
host-dependent lifecycle**:

- Not latent — actively replicating throughout infection
- Not integrated
- Does not evade immunity via antigenic variation
- Does not use coded latency machinery
- Does not use mutation-driven defective replication
- Does not use lifecycle compartmentalization (uses it incidentally
  — but the virus-style compartmentalization isn't the primary
  mechanism)
- **Does use**: physical niche (hair follicle, sebaceous gland — not
  immune-accessible); obligate host dependence; no free-living form;
  reductive genome committed to the host

Class 7 is characterized by:
- Multicellular or complex eukaryotic organism
- Obligate host-dependent (cannot live free)
- Reductive-genome coevolution signature
- Long coevolutionary timescale (mammals-lifetime)
- Pathogenic only when host conditions dysregulate density or
  immunity

Other class-7 candidates on the broader list: *Malassezia* yeast
(next attempt), *Pediculus humanus* (head lice), *Sarcoptes scabiei*
(scabies), *Enterobius vermicularis* (pinworm).

## Disease-as-incidental or disease-as-selected?

Unlike viruses where disease is clearly post-transmission and
evolutionarily irrelevant, Demodex is more complex:

- **Mild-density Demodex** (commensal) — no disease; fitness-neutral
  or mildly beneficial (possibly cleanses clogged follicles?)
- **High-density Demodex** (pathogenic) — causes rosacea,
  blepharitis, etc.; does the disease reduce Demodex fitness?
  Marginally: host treats the skin → kills some mites → but
  transmission likely already occurred during asymptomatic phase

So disease is weakly negatively selected from Demodex's perspective:
treated skin has fewer mites, reduces future transmission of that
sub-lineage. But the selection is weak because:
- Treatment (ivermectin, TTO) is a recent development
- Most symptom-triggering density occurs after mites have already
  transmitted to next generation via mother-child contact
- Untreated "burden" is tolerated by most individuals across
  history

Net: disease is weakly-to-neutrally fitness-relevant to Demodex, and
the density-dependent pathogenesis is not under strong selection to
reduce.

## Genome + ancient DNA

- Published Demodex folliculorum genome assembly — modest size,
  showing reductive features (Palopoli et al. and subsequent
  studies)
- **Ancient DNA:** Demodex is theoretically recoverable from
  archaeological skin / hair samples but the recovery has not been
  widely reported. The mite body is fragile and decays quickly.
  Unlike H. pylori (stomach preservation) or herpesvirus DNA
  (integrated or dense latent stocks), Demodex aDNA requires
  specialized conditions.

3. **Species-specific host range:** strictest of any organism on

4. **Low mutation rate during persistence:** moderate — eukaryotic

   per-unit-time mutation accumulation.
5. **Ancient DNA recovery:** limited so far. ◐
6. **Vaccination prevents but doesn't cure:** no vaccine exists for
   Demodex; treatment is acaricidal (TTO, ivermectin, lotilaner).
   Prediction not testable in current form.

**Framework extension:** the "large genome" prediction of attempt_001
needs reformulation. It holds for viral + bacterial persistence but
NOT for eukaryotic obligate-parasite persistence, where **genome
reduction (not expansion)** is the signature. A better formulation:

> Persistent organisms show **genome specialization** relative to
> free-living relatives — either expansion (viral/bacterial
> immune-evasion machinery) or contraction (eukaryotic commitment to
> host). Both are signatures of long coevolution.

## Host-side coevolution

- Human skin immune biology (TLR2, TLR4, inflammasome) is calibrated
  to tolerate low-density Demodex + B. oleronius without
  inflammatory response
- Specific TLR2 polymorphisms affect Demodex-density tolerance —
  higher-activity TLR2 variants correlate with lower Demodex density
  and lower rosacea risk (some studies)
- The cathelicidin / KLK5 / LL-37 system (per
  `medical/dysbiosis/numerics/run_046`) is the innate-immunity arm
  that interacts with Demodex-associated inflammation
- HLA-class-II association with rosacea severity — moderate

## Current selection pressures

1. **Topical ivermectin** (Soolantra, approved 2014): directly
   kills Demodex via GABA-Cl channel. Widespread off-label use in
   rosacea and blepharitis. Is selection for resistance being seen?
   Limited; so far no clear ivermectin-resistant Demodex lineage
   reported.
2. **Tea tree oil (T4O)**: acaricidal via membrane disruption; wide
   consumer use. Resistance potential uncertain.
3. **Lotilaner (Xdemvy, 2023)**: GABA-Cl channel blocker as ocular
   drop. Too new for resistance observation.
4. **Dietary change** (Western diet) drives density upward — against
   the grain of the treatment pressure.

Net selection: mild downward pressure on Demodex density from
modern treatments, mild upward from diet. Demodex continues to
coexist with humans at historically high density in the West.

## Open questions specific to Demodex

1. **Why two species on humans and not one?** Comparative genomics
   of D. folliculorum vs D. brevis would clarify the divergence
   timing and cause.
2. **Can Demodex evolve resistance to ivermectin?** Treatment is
   still relatively new; long-term surveillance will answer.
3. **What is the fitness impact of B. oleronius on Demodex?** Is
   B. oleronius strictly beneficial or is it a passive parasite on
   the mite itself? Answer affects the 3-tier symbiosis model.
4. **What is the ancestral Demodex ecology?** Before commitment to
   mammalian skin, what did the lineage do?
5. **Why do some humans have high-density Demodex without rosacea
   and vice versa?** Host immune biology is the likely answer but
   the detailed genetic determinants are incompletely characterized.
