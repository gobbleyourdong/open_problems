# attempt_007 — Helicobacter pylori: Co-Migration with Humanity and the "Good" Pathogen

## Phylogenetic placement

**Domain:** Bacteria
**Phylum:** Pseudomonadota (formerly Proteobacteria)
**Class:** Epsilonproteobacteria
**Order:** Campylobacterales
**Family:** Helicobacteraceae
**Genus:** *Helicobacter*
**Species:** *Helicobacter pylori*

Other Helicobacter species infect non-human animals (dogs, cats,
mice, various primates), mostly asymptomatically or with mild GI
disease. *H. pylori* is the only human-specialist in the genus and
its host-restriction is near-absolute.

**Epsilonproteobacteria** as a group are largely anaerobic or
microaerophilic; *H. pylori* fits this — requires reduced oxygen for
growth and is therefore niche-restricted to mucus-protected
microaerophilic environments like the gastric lumen.

**Coevolution signal:** stronger than any other human bacterial
pathogen. Discussed in depth below.

## Coevolution with humans — the phylogeographic signal

**This is the key evolutionary feature of H. pylori**: its strain
phylogeography tracks human population migrations so closely it can
be used as a marker for population history independent of human
genetics.

Major H. pylori populations and their human-population associations:

| H. pylori population | Associated human population |
|----------------------|-----------------------------|
| **hpAfrica1** | Sub-Saharan Africans |
| **hpAfrica2** | Southern African Khoisan |
| **hpNEAfrica** | Northeast African (Nilotic) populations |
| **hpEurope** | European populations (admixed hpAsia2 + hpNEAfrica ancestry) |
| **hpAsia2** | South/Central Asian |
| **hpEastAsia** (subdivides into hspEAsia, hspAmerind, hspMaori) | East Asian + migration descendants |
| **hpSahul** | Australian Aboriginals |

The clean alignment between these strain groupings and human
migration routes (out-of-Africa ~50–70 kya, Eurasian expansion, Asian
→ Americas, Asian → Oceania) establishes that **H. pylori was
carried along as humans dispersed**. The age of the H. pylori–human
association is at least 60,000 years and arguably deeper.

**The ancient DNA confirmation:** in 2016, Maixner et al.
sequenced H. pylori from the 5,300-year-old "Iceman" (Ötzi, a
Copper-Age individual preserved in the Alps). The strain was
**hpAsia2-like**, not the modern hpEurope variant — showing that
the current European strain is a relatively recent admixture event,
and that population turnover in Europe's H. pylori has occurred
within the last ~5,000 years. This matched the separately-known
human-population turnover in Europe from steppe ancestry expansion.

**The signal is strong enough that** H. pylori typing has been used
in human migration studies where human DNA evidence alone was
ambiguous. Few other organisms provide this level of historical
resolution.

## Niche: the gastric lumen

*H. pylori* lives in the **mucus layer overlying the gastric
epithelium**, not in the gastric lumen per se. Key adaptations:

1. **Urease** — converts urea to NH₃ + CO₂, locally neutralizing
   stomach acid. Urea diffuses from blood into gastric mucosa;
   H. pylori's urease (among the most catalytically active enzymes
   known) buffers the bacterium's immediate microenvironment to
   near-neutral pH despite gastric acidity.
2. **Flagella + helical shape** — corkscrew motility through the
   mucus layer; cells penetrate close to epithelium where pH is less
   extreme
3. **Adhesins (BabA, SabA, OipA)** — bind Lewis-type blood group
   antigens on gastric epithelial cells; specific adhesin/antigen
   combinations determine colonization success in different human
   hosts
4. **Outer membrane vesicles (OMVs)** — export toxins at distance
   from the bacterium
5. **Biofilm formation** — resists antibiotic penetration

**Host range:** nearly all H. pylori–infected individuals are
colonized early in childhood (age 2–5 typically) via oral-oral or
fecal-oral transmission, most often from mother or close family
members. Adult-acquired infection exists but is less common. Once
established, colonization is lifelong in the absence of antibiotic
eradication therapy.

## Disease-causation biology

**H. pylori disease spectrum:**

| Condition | Strength of H. pylori causal evidence | Notes |
|-----------|:--------------------------------------:|-------|
| **Peptic ulcer disease** | Strong (~90% of duodenal ulcers, ~70–80% of gastric) | Marshall/Warren original finding |
| **Gastric adenocarcinoma** | Strong (IARC class I carcinogen) | WHO designation since 1994 |
| **MALT lymphoma** (gastric mucosa–associated lymphoid tissue) | Strong (remission often follows eradication alone, no chemo) | Single best example of bacterium-driven cancer |
| **Atrophic gastritis** | Strong | Precursor for both cancer types |
| **Iron-deficiency anemia (refractory)** | Moderate | H. pylori competes for iron and reduces absorption |
| **ITP (idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura)** | Moderate | Subset of ITP patients respond to eradication |
| **GERD / esophageal adenocarcinoma** | **NEGATIVE — H. pylori is protective** | See below |

**Protective effects — the "disappearing microbiota" argument:**

Several chronic conditions have **rising incidence as H. pylori
prevalence declines** in developed countries:

- **Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)** — H. pylori–associated
  hypochlorhydria (reduced acid from atrophic gastritis) mechanically
  reduces reflux; eradication of H. pylori can precipitate new GERD
- **Barrett's esophagus** and **esophageal adenocarcinoma** — same
  mechanism; rising EAC incidence in developed countries parallels
  falling H. pylori prevalence
- **Childhood-onset asthma** (controversial) — some epidemiology
  suggests early H. pylori colonization may be protective against
  atopic disease
- **Inflammatory bowel disease** (controversial) — similar protective
  association in some studies

This makes H. pylori **the strongest example on the persistent-
organism list of a pathogen with measurable fitness-relevant benefits
to the host under some conditions.** Blaser (Martin Blaser, NYU)
coined "disappearing microbiota" to describe this dual role —
population-scale H. pylori eradication may be producing a net health
cost when GERD/EAC increases are factored against PUD/gastric-cancer
decreases.

## The Cag pathogenicity island and CagA

**A subset of H. pylori strains carry the *cag* pathogenicity island
(cagPAI)** — a ~40 kb genomic region containing ~30 genes including:

- **Type IV secretion system (T4SS)** — a bacterial "syringe" that
  injects effector proteins into host cells
- **CagA** — the primary effector, a bacterial oncoprotein that
  after delivery into gastric epithelial cells is phosphorylated,
  binds SHP-2, and dysregulates multiple host signaling pathways
  (Ras/ERK, cell polarity)

**Cag-positive strains have much higher disease risk** than
cag-negative. Cag-positive infections cause:
- Higher gastric cancer risk
- Higher MALT lymphoma risk
- Stronger inflammatory response

**East Asian CagA variants** (with specific EPIYA motif repeats —
EPIYA-D in East Asia vs EPIYA-C in Europe) have even higher
carcinogenicity. This may partially explain why gastric cancer is
more common in East Asia than in Europe/US.

**Evolutionarily:** the cagPAI is acquired horizontally (has
different GC content from core H. pylori genome, consistent with
being introduced from another bacterium). Not all H. pylori strains
have it — it's part of strain-level variation within the species.

## Persistence mechanism — a new class for the framework

Previous attempts established 5 persistence-mechanism classes (viral):
- Encoded latency (EBV, HCMV)
- Mutation-driven replication defect (CVB)
- Lifecycle-differentiation coupling (HPV)
- Antigenic variation (HIV, HCV)
- Chromosomal integration with germline transmission (HHV-6)

*H. pylori* doesn't fit any of these. It uses a **sixth class**:

**Class 6 — chronic active colonization of an immune-privileged niche
with active host-tolerance induction**:

- Not latent — H. pylori actively replicates throughout infection
- Not integrated — no host-genome interaction
- Niche is physically protected (mucus layer, acidic environment
  excludes most immune cells)
- **Actively induces host tolerance** via:
  - VacA cytotoxin — binds T cells, blocks activation
  - γ-glutamyltranspeptidase (GGT) — amino acid modulation inducing
    regulatory T cells
  - Low-grade chronic inflammation (not resolving) — keeps immune
    response in a non-clearing mode

This is closer to how *Plasmodium* chronic malaria or
*Mycobacterium tuberculosis* latent infection work — bacterial/
protozoan mechanisms of persistence that differ fundamentally from
viral ones.

**Updated attempt_001 framework:**

The persistence-strategy spectrum extends further. A sixth class is
needed for bacteria that maintain chronic active colonization via
host-tolerance induction at a niche the immune system can't
efficiently reach. *H. pylori* is the clearest example; applies also
to *P. gingivalis* (next attempt), *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*
(latent TB), and others.

## Coevolutionary genetic signatures

- **Human ABO blood group / Lewis antigen systems** — H. pylori
  BabA adhesin binds Lewis-b antigen; blood-group-specific H. pylori
  strains have been selected in different populations. ABO antigen
  diversity in humans may be partly shaped by H. pylori and other
  gut pathogens.
- **IL-1β genotype** — specific IL1B polymorphisms affect gastric
  cancer risk given H. pylori infection (El-Omar et al. 2000
  Nature). The human polymorphism persists at meaningful frequency,
  consistent with balancing selection.
- **MHC class II** — gastric cancer risk stratifies by HLA-DQ
  haplotype in H. pylori–infected individuals.

## Current selection pressures

1. **Antibiotic eradication therapy** — since Marshall/Warren's
   Nobel Prize (2005), triple (clarithromycin + amoxicillin + PPI) or
   quadruple (add bismuth) therapy has been deployed globally.
   **Clarithromycin resistance has risen dramatically** (~15–30%
   depending on region), forcing shift to alternative regimens.
   Fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin) resistance follows similar pattern.
2. **Improved sanitation** — reduces childhood transmission.
   **H. pylori prevalence (Hooi 2017 Gastroenterology PMID 28456631):
   pooled global 44.3% (~4.4 billion in 2015).** Regional variation
   is large: Africa ~70%, Nigeria 87.7%; Switzerland 18.9%,
   Oceania 24.4%. Chen 2023/24 meta-analysis confirms continued
   decline to ~43.1% in the 2015–2022 window. Declines are sharper
   in developed countries.
3. **PPI use** — reduces local gastric acidity, can paradoxically
   create worse H. pylori colonization in some individuals.
4. **Increasing intra-host strain diversity** — as individuals
   acquire strains from multiple family members, contacts, and
   (reduced) environmental sources, co-infection dynamics
   complicate evolution.

## Predictions from attempt_001 framework (now extended to bacteria)

1. **Transmission-mode + niche clustering:** oral-oral + fecal-oral
   + early-childhood + long-term gastric — fits low-intensity,
   high-duration pattern. ✓
2. **Larger "genome" encoding persistence machinery:** H. pylori
   has a relatively compact ~1.6–1.7 Mb genome by bacterial standards
   but encodes extensive counter-immunity functions (urease, VacA,
   CagA, GGT, OMVs). Not a "genome size" prediction in viral terms
   but the counter-immunology investment is significant. ◐
3. **Species-specific host range:** strict — only humans among major
   hosts. ✓ (strongest host-restriction of any Helicobacter species)
4. **Low mutation rate during persistence:** bacteria mutate
   steadily via DNA replication errors, but within-host population
   structure stabilizes after initial colonization; evolution is
   slow in practice. ✓ in net
5. **Ancient DNA recovery:** H. pylori aDNA recovered from 5,300-
   year-old Iceman. ✓✓ strongly
6. **Vaccination prevents but doesn't cure:** no licensed H. pylori
   vaccine exists despite decades of effort; candidates have moved
   through phase 1–2 trials without strong protection. Testing
   this prediction awaits a working vaccine. Pending.

**H. pylori fits the framework well, and its ancient-DNA recovery +
human-migration tracking extend prediction 5 to the strongest
example on the list.**

## Therapeutic implications (cross-link to medical/persistent_organisms/)

- Eradication is achievable (~80–90% success with modern quadruple
  therapy) but increasingly challenged by resistance
- Disease prevention: eradication reduces PUD, gastric cancer, MALT
  lymphoma risk
- **Mass eradication policies are contested** because of protective
  GERD / EAC effects; the net population-level benefit of eradicating
  vs tolerating H. pylori is unsettled
- Population-scale H. pylori is declining; gastric cancer incidence
  is declining in parallel; EAC and GERD are rising — consistent
  with the "disappearing microbiota" dual-effect

## Open questions specific to H. pylori

1. **Is net population-health outcome of eradication positive or
   negative?** Factor PUD reduction + gastric cancer reduction
   against GERD/EAC increase. No clear consensus; may differ by
   region (East Asia vs West).
2. **Can we design strain-specific therapy?** Cag-positive vs
   Cag-negative strains have very different disease risk. Targeted
   treatment of Cag-positive infections (while tolerating
   Cag-negative) might preserve protective effects.
3. **How will antibiotic resistance evolve under continued
   eradication pressure?** Current trajectory suggests progressive
   loss of first-line options within ~10–20 years.
4. **Can probiotic or microbiome-based strategies restore the
   beneficial H. pylori role without its pathogenic role?**
   Attenuated-live H. pylori strain engineering has been proposed;
   no clinical deployment.
5. **What human genetic variants are under selection against
   Cag-positive H. pylori?** Signals exist but aren't
   comprehensively mapped.

## Cross-link to medical/persistent_organisms/

The medical-side framework lists H. pylori as organism #5. The
therapeutic architecture (Phase 1 clearance via triple/quadruple
antibiotic therapy; Phase 2 adjunct via PPI + mucosal healing) fits
the two-phase pattern observed across organisms. Unique feature for
H. pylori: **the adjunct phase includes maintaining hypochlorhydria
for ulcer healing, which can destabilize reflux control** — clinical
complexity the other organisms on the list don't share.
