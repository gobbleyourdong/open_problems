# attempt_113 — Immunosenescence and Inflammaging: The Late-Life Phase

> Sixth extension after the main sweep (100-107) and prior extensions
> (108 trained immunity, 109 EVEs, 110 protist, 111 plant, 112 mucosal
> evolution). This attempt addresses the CURRENT FRONTIER of human
> immunology: why the immune system changes with age in ways that
> produce both elevated susceptibility to infection AND chronic
> inflammation. "Inflammaging" is the term introduced by Claudio
> Franceschi in 2000 for the low-grade chronic inflammation that
> accompanies human aging. Evolutionarily, it reflects the
> near-absence of selection for post-reproductive immunity — a
> predictable consequence of disposable-soma theory applied to
> immune maintenance.

---

## Two linked phenomena

### Immunosenescence

The age-related decline in immune function:

- **T cell compartment**: thymic involution (begins at puberty;
  near-complete loss of thymic function by ~60). Peripheral T cell
  pool shifts from naive-dominant to memory-dominant. CD8+ T cells
  accumulate large senescent clones (especially CMV-driven; see
  attempt_107).
- **B cell compartment**: reduced new B cell output from bone
  marrow; reduced class-switching efficiency; reduced germinal
  center responses; narrower antibody repertoire.
- **Innate compartment**: reduced neutrophil function; altered
  monocyte/macrophage activation; NK cell dysregulation.
- **Outcomes**: increased infection susceptibility (influenza,
  pneumonia, UTI), reduced vaccine response, slower wound healing,
  increased cancer incidence.

### Inflammaging

A distinct but linked phenomenon: chronic, low-grade systemic
inflammation that develops with age. Measurable as elevated IL-6,
TNF-α, CRP in resting blood samples. Different from acute
inflammation (no specific insult) and different from autoimmunity
(no organ-specific target).

The term was coined in:

**Franceschi C, Bonafè M, Valensin S, Olivieri F, De Luca M,
Ottaviani E, De Benedictis G (2000).** "Inflamm-aging: an
evolutionary perspective on immunosenescence." *Annals of the New
York Academy of Sciences* 908:244-254. PMID 10911963. DOI
10.1111/j.1749-6632.2000.tb06651.x.

Key proposal: the aging immune system is not simply weakening; it
is shifting to a chronically active state that is "antagonistic
pleiotropy" writ large — a response useful early in life becomes
harmful when maintained lifelong.

## The evolutionary logic: disposable soma + antagonistic pleiotropy

### Why the immune system declines with age

Two evolutionary frameworks:

**Disposable soma theory** (Kirkwood 1977): natural selection
invests in somatic maintenance only to the extent that
reproductive fitness is served. After reproduction (or outside
reproductive age), somatic mechanisms — including immunity — are
not maintained; decay accumulates.

**Antagonistic pleiotropy** (Williams 1957): genes that are
beneficial early in life can be detrimental late in life; because
selection acts strongly on early-life fitness and weakly on
post-reproductive, the early-life benefit dominates evolutionary
fixation even if late-life harm follows.

Applied to immunity:
- A robust pro-inflammatory response is beneficial for pathogen
  control during young adulthood (when reproductive fitness is at
  stake).
- That same response, if chronic, drives atherosclerosis, cancer,
  neurodegeneration — all primarily post-reproductive harms.
- Selection strongly favors the young-adulthood benefit, pays no
  price for the late-life harm.

Inflammaging is therefore **not a disease**. It is an unselected
byproduct of a selected trait.

### The human-specific angle

Humans have an unusually long post-reproductive lifespan compared
to most mammals (grandmother hypothesis proposes positive
selection for longevity beyond reproduction). Even so, the
disposable-soma logic applies: selection for immune maintenance is
MUCH weaker after age 40-50 than before.

The chronic-disease burden of aging populations (cardiovascular
disease, cancer, Alzheimer's, T2DM) has large inflammaging
contributions. Modern medicine has extended lifespan beyond the
period of robust immune maintenance; inflammaging is the
consequence.

## Mechanisms of inflammaging

### Cellular senescence and SASP

Senescent cells (cells that have exited the cell cycle but remain
metabolically active) accumulate with age. Senescent cells exhibit
the **senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP)**: sustained
production of inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-8), chemokines,
proteases, and growth factors.

SASP-driven inflammaging:
- Senescent cells accumulate in all tissues.
- Their secretions recruit immune cells.
- Resulting inflammation accelerates surrounding-cell senescence
  (feedback).
- Clearing senescent cells (senolytics) in mice reduces
  age-associated disease.

Reference: **van Deursen JM (2014).** "The role of senescent cells
in ageing." *Nature* 509:439-446. PMID 24848057.

### CMV-driven T cell clonal expansion

Human cytomegalovirus (HCMV, attempt_005 of 001-series) persistently
infects ~60-90% of adults. Over decades, CMV-specific CD8+ T cells
accumulate to constitute a massive fraction of the peripheral
repertoire — up to 30% of CD8+ T cells in some elderly individuals
are CMV-specific.

This "T cell space" expansion:
- Reduces room for other T cell specificities.
- Correlates with reduced vaccine response (influenza).
- Is a proposed driver of immunosenescence.

Reference: **Nikolich-Žugich J (2018).** "The twilight of immunity:
emerging concepts in aging of the immune system." *Nature
Immunology* 19:10-19. PMID 29242543.

### Microbiome changes

Gut microbiome diversity declines with age; pro-inflammatory taxa
increase; barrier-function-supportive taxa decrease. Dysbiosis-driven
chronic low-level bacterial antigen leakage (LPS through weakened
gut barrier) drives systemic inflammation.

Reference: **Thevaranjan N et al. (2017).** "Age-associated microbial
dysbiosis promotes intestinal permeability, systemic
inflammation, and macrophage dysfunction." *Cell Host & Microbe*
21:455-466. PMID 28407481.

### Mitochondrial dysfunction and mtDNA release

Aged cells release mitochondrial DNA fragments into cytoplasm.
cGAS-STING (attempt_102) detects this as "foreign" DNA and triggers
inflammatory signaling. A feedback driving inflammaging.

## Persistent organisms and inflammaging

The 001-series persistent organisms contribute to inflammaging:
- **HCMV** → T cell clonal expansion (discussed above)
- **EBV** → chronic B cell stimulation
- **H. pylori** → chronic gastric inflammation (some carriers)
- **P. gingivalis** → chronic oral inflammation → systemic effects
  via circulating LPS
- **CVB persistent** → chronic tissue-level inflammation in pancreas,
  heart, CNS

These are not primary DRIVERS of inflammaging — it would occur
without them — but they are substantial contributors and the
mechanism by which early-life persistence translates to late-life
disease.

This is the cross-track link: 001-series describes the persistent
organisms; 100-series (this attempt) describes the inflammaging
consequence; medical/ (other repo trees) describes specific
diseases that emerge.

## Why this fits the framework

The 100-series framing: immune architecture built over 3.5 Ga in
layers for young-adult pathogen response. Inflammaging is a
**failure mode** of that architecture when maintained decades
beyond its evolutionary-useful window.

This is qualitatively different from the other attempts' contents
(which described immune EMERGENCE at various stages). attempt_113
describes immune DECAY — a necessary complement to the
evolutionary story.

## Open questions

1. **Is inflammaging treatable?** Senolytics (dasatinib +
   quercetin, etc.) reduce senescent cells in mice, reduce SASP,
   reduce age-associated disease. Clinical trials are underway in
   humans for specific conditions (idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis,
   diabetic kidney disease).

2. **How do we measure inflammaging?** The current standard is
   resting serum IL-6/CRP, but this is a crude summary. Detailed
   "immune age" metrics (Alpert et al. 2019 *Nat Medicine*) are
   being developed.

3. **Does lifespan extension require inflammaging treatment?**
   Healthspan (years of healthy life) vs lifespan (total years).
   Treating inflammaging might extend healthspan without much
   change in lifespan — a frequently-cited goal of aging
   research.

4. **Why do some individuals age without inflammaging?** Centenarian
   studies show surprisingly low inflammatory markers. Genetic and
   environmental factors protect. Understanding this would inform
   therapy.
